# Patrick Lencioni Leadership Frameworks — Complete Reference

Synthesized from Patrick Lencioni's complete body of work: *The Five Dysfunctions of a Team*, *The Ideal Team Player*, *The 6 Types of Working Genius*, *The Advantage*, *Death by Meeting*, *Silos, Politics and Turf Wars*, *The Three Signs of a Miserable Job*, *Getting Naked*, and *The Motive*. Lencioni is the founder of The Table Group and one of the most influential voices on team health and organizational clarity.

---

## THE FIVE DYSFUNCTIONS OF A TEAM (The Pyramid)

Lencioni's most famous framework. A pyramid where each layer builds upon the one below. Fix them from the bottom up — skipping a layer guarantees failure.

### Dysfunction 1: Absence of Trust (Foundation)

This is NOT predictive trust ("I trust you'll deliver"). It is **vulnerability-based trust**: the willingness to expose weaknesses, admit mistakes, and ask for help without fear of judgment.

**Teams without trust:**
- Conceal weaknesses and mistakes from one another
- Hesitate to ask for help or provide constructive feedback
- Jump to conclusions about others' intentions
- Hold grudges and dread meetings
- Waste energy managing their behaviors for political advantage

**The leader must go first** — modeling vulnerability before expecting it from others. "The only way for the leader to create a safe environment for team members to be vulnerable is by stepping up and doing something that feels unsafe and uncomfortable first."

**Servant Leadership Link:** Vulnerability-based trust IS the foundation of Self-Governing Culture. Without it, teams default to Centralized Control — hiding mistakes, blaming others, protecting ego (the Me There). Trust maps directly to the Moment of Truth process: you cannot get to shared facts if people are afraid to be honest. S-L-Y only works when people trust that the investigation starts with structure, not blame.

**Building Trust — The Personal Histories Exercise:**
- Go around the table, each person answers: Where did you grow up? How many siblings and where in the birth order? What was the greatest challenge you faced as a child?
- Takes 30-45 minutes. Even teams together for 10+ years discover things they never knew.
- Creates a foundation for deeper vulnerability over time.

---

### Dysfunction 2: Fear of Conflict

Without trust, teams avoid productive conflict. Lencioni distinguishes between **productive ideological conflict** (passionate debate about ideas) and destructive personal politics.

**Teams that fear conflict:**
- Have boring meetings where nothing real gets discussed
- Ignore controversial topics that need addressing
- Focus on politics and personal risk management
- Miss opportunities because no one challenges assumptions

**When trust exists:** "Conflict becomes nothing but the pursuit of truth, an attempt to find the best possible answer."

**Servant Leadership Link:** Maps directly to the Conflict Resolution model. You cannot skip competition and jump straight to collaboration — healthy conflict requires the courage to advocate for your position. Lencioni adds the WHY: teams skip conflict because they lack trust. Also connects to Groeschel's "Direct AND Kind" paradox — productive conflict requires both courage and care. The key insight from both frameworks: avoiding conflict is NOT kindness — it's cowardice that harms the team.

---

### Dysfunction 3: Lack of Commitment

When teams avoid conflict, they cannot achieve genuine buy-in. People who don't weigh in can't buy in.

**What commitment requires:**
- **Clarity** — everyone clearly understands what was decided
- **Buy-in** — even if someone disagrees, they commit after being heard

**Commitment does NOT require consensus.** Lencioni argues consensus often means compromise, and compromise may not yield the best result. What it requires is that every voice is heard before the decision is made. Once the decision is made, everyone commits — even dissenters.

**Key insight:** "If everyone is in agreement, then someone is not thinking."

**Teams lacking commitment:**
- Have difficulty making decisions and second-guess themselves
- Revisit the same discussions repeatedly
- Develop a fear of failure that leads to analysis paralysis

**Servant Leadership Link:** This is the HERE-Path-THERE model in action. The THERE must be clear (clarity) and shared (buy-in). When teams skip conflict, the THERE stays vague — everyone interprets it differently and pulls in different directions. Nesting THEREs require commitment at every level: "If everyone owns it, no one owns it." Maxwell's Law of Buy-In reinforces: people buy into the leader, then the vision — but they won't buy in if they weren't heard.

---

### Dysfunction 4: Avoidance of Accountability

This is **peer-to-peer accountability** — team members holding EACH OTHER to high standards, not waiting for the leader to do it.

**Lencioni's reframe:** Accountability is an **act of care**, not aggression. "I'm telling you this because I care about you and the team."

**Why accountability fails without commitment:** You cannot hold someone accountable for a commitment that was never clearly made. Clarity of commitment must come first.

**Teams avoiding accountability:**
- Have low standards that no one tries to raise
- Turn a blind eye to poor performance to avoid damaging personal relationships
- Miss deadlines and deliverables without consequence
- Place an undue burden on the leader as the sole source of discipline

**Servant Leadership Link:** This is the Freedom V operationalized at the team level. The Freedom V provides the structure (clear boundaries and consequences), but Lencioni adds the cultural expectation that PEERS enforce it, not just the leader. In a Self-Governing Culture, accountability is distributed — the inverted pyramid means the team holds itself to the standard. S-L-Y still applies: when someone misses, check structure first (was the commitment clear?), then leadership (did we set them up for success?), then the individual.

**This is typically the hardest behavior for a team to master.**

---

### Dysfunction 5: Inattention to Results (Top)

When accountability is absent, team members prioritize individual goals, ego, career development, or departmental status over collective team results.

**A functional team:**
- Defines success collectively, not individually
- Celebrates group achievements over personal wins
- Makes sacrifices for the team's benefit
- Retains achievement-oriented employees (who leave teams without results focus)

**Servant Leadership Link:** This is We There vs. Me There at the organizational level. "Inattention to results" IS the Me There — ego, status, department, career over mission. The structural fix: incentives that reward collective results (Structure Demands Behavior). Lencioni and Gatena arrive at the same conclusion from different angles — if you want We There behavior, build structures that honor it.

---

### The Positive Flip (What Functional Teams Do)

| Dysfunction | Functional Behavior |
|---|---|
| Absence of Trust | They **trust** one another (vulnerability-based) |
| Fear of Conflict | They engage in **unfiltered conflict** around ideas |
| Lack of Commitment | They **commit** to decisions and plans of action |
| Avoidance of Accountability | They hold one another **accountable** for delivering |
| Inattention to Results | They focus on **collective results** |

## THE IDEAL TEAM PLAYER (Humble, Hungry, Smart)

A hiring, developing, and evaluating framework. The ideal team player possesses all three virtues — missing even one creates significant dysfunction.

### The Three Essential Virtues

**Humble**
- Lencioni considers this the single most important virtue
- Lacks excessive ego or concerns about status
- Quick to point out others' contributions, slow to seek attention for their own
- Shares credit, emphasizes team over self, defines success collectively
- Open to seeking help and learning from others
- **Servant Leadership Link:** Humility IS the foundation of servant leadership. The inverted pyramid requires leaders who put others first. Maps to We There — humble people naturally orient toward the mission, not themselves.

**Hungry**
- Self-motivated and dedicated — never has to be pushed to work harder
- Always looking for more: more to learn, more responsibility to take on
- Constantly thinking about the next step and new opportunities
- Diligent and willing to go above and beyond
- **Servant Leadership Link:** Maps to D.R.E.A.M.'s Devotion pyramid — a hungry person is at Level 4 (Builder: "How do I help the opportunity?") or Level 5 (Missionary: "This is my calling"). Also connects to Maxwell's Law of Process — hunger is what drives daily growth.

**Smart (People Smart)**
- This is NOT intellectual intelligence — it is **interpersonal intelligence**
- Common sense about people: how to deal with others effectively
- Ability to pick up on the needs and feelings of others
- Awareness of group dynamics and how their words and actions affect people
- **Servant Leadership Link:** Maps to D.R.E.A.M.'s Empathy value and the Listening/Perspectives mental model — seeing what others see, not just hearing their words. Also connects to Maxwell's Law of Connection and Groeschel's "Direct AND Kind" paradox.

### When One Virtue Is Missing (The Dangerous Archetypes)

**Only One Virtue Present:**
| Archetype | Has | Missing | Behavior |
|---|---|---|---|
| **The Pawn** | Humble only | Hungry, Smart | Nice but doesn't contribute or challenge |
| **The Bulldozer** | Hungry only | Humble, Smart | Gets things done but destroys relationships |
| **The Charmer** | Smart only | Humble, Hungry | Plays the game, doesn't really contribute |

**Two Virtues, Missing One:**
| Archetype | Has | Missing | Behavior | Danger Level |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Accidental Mess-Maker** | Humble + Hungry | Smart | Accomplishes a lot but leaves interpersonal wreckage — doesn't realize their impact | Medium |
| **Lovable Slacker** | Humble + Smart | Hungry | Pleasant and well-liked but does the minimum — only what is required | Medium |
| **Skillful Politician** | Hungry + Smart | Humble | Appears effective but is self-serving; works for personal gain while appearing collaborative | **Highest** — hardest to detect, most damaging |

### Application as Hiring Filter
Use Humble-Hungry-Smart as a non-negotiable hiring rubric alongside D.R.E.A.M. values:
- Interview for all three — don't assume hunger from a resume or smarts from charm
- The Skillful Politician is the most dangerous hire — they interview brilliantly but poison culture
- When developing existing team members: identify which virtue is weakest and coach specifically to it

## THE SIX TYPES OF WORKING GENIUS

Lencioni's most recent major framework — a productivity and team composition tool. Not a personality test but a work-preference assessment.

### Core Concept
Every person has:
- **2 Working Geniuses** — areas where you thrive and feel energized
- **2 Working Competencies** — things you can do but don't love
- **2 Working Frustrations** — tasks that drain you and lead to burnout

### The Six Types (In Work-Phase Order)

**IDEATION PHASE:**

| Genius | Description | Type |
|---|---|---|
| **Wonder (W)** | Pondering, questioning, asking "what if?" and "is there a better way?" Identifies when something is off or ripe for improvement. | Responsive |
| **Invention (I)** | Creating new solutions and original ideas in response to problems identified by Wonder. The creative spark. | Disruptive |

**ACTIVATION PHASE:**

| Genius | Description | Type |
|---|---|---|
| **Discernment (D)** | Evaluating ideas using intuition and good judgment. Natural filter between ideas and action — knows what will work. | Responsive |
| **Galvanizing (G)** | Rallying and inspiring others to act. Provides the energy and momentum to turn evaluated ideas into initiatives. | Disruptive |

**IMPLEMENTATION PHASE:**

| Genius | Description | Type |
|---|---|---|
| **Enablement (E)** | Helping and supporting others. Responds to needs, provides encouragement and assistance to move projects forward. | Responsive |
| **Tenacity (T)** | Pushing through to completion. Finishers who drive results, overcome obstacles, and ensure high standards to the end. | Disruptive |

### Practical Application
- **Burnout often comes from spending too much time in your frustrations** — not from overwork
- **Teams need all six geniuses represented** to function effectively across ideation, activation, and implementation
- **Gaps create bottlenecks:** A team with no Galvanizer has great ideas that never get activated. A team with no Tenacity starts everything and finishes nothing.
- **Servant Leadership Link:** Use alongside D.R.E.A.M. for team composition. D.R.E.A.M. measures values alignment; Working Genius measures how they'll contribute. A team of Inventors with no Tenacity will be creative but unreliable. Also connects to the Three-Legged Stool — ensure each strategic leg has the right geniuses represented.

## ORGANIZATIONAL HEALTH (The Advantage)

**Core thesis:** "Organizational health is the single greatest competitive advantage in any business." It's so simple that leaders overlook it — it doesn't require intelligence or sophistication, just "uncommon levels of discipline, courage, persistence, and common sense."

### The Four Disciplines

**Discipline 1: Build a Cohesive Leadership Team**
- Small team (3-10 people) built on the Five Dysfunctions model
- The leadership team must model health before the rest of the organization can follow
- **Servant Leadership Link:** The Three-Legged Stool requires a cohesive leadership team at the center. If the leaders don't trust each other, the stool wobbles.

**Discipline 2: Create Clarity**
Answer six critical questions:

1. **Why do we exist?** — Core purpose (completely idealistic and aspirational)
2. **How do we behave?** — Values and behavioral expectations
3. **What do we do?** — Simple description of the business
4. **How will we succeed?** — Strategic anchors and deliberate decisions
5. **What is most important, right now?** — The rallying cry / thematic goal
6. **Who must do what?** — Roles and responsibilities

**Servant Leadership Link:** Questions 1-2 map to the Transcendent There and D.R.E.A.M. values. Question 4 maps to the Three-Legged Stool strategies. Question 5 maps to Nesting THEREs (the current strategic milestone). Question 6 maps to Freedom V's (clear boundaries for each role).

**Discipline 3: Over-Communicate Clarity**
- People need to hear something **seven times on average** before they believe it
- Most leaders under-communicate because they feel redundant
- Healthy organizations aren't afraid to repeat themselves constantly
- **Servant Leadership Link:** This validates the mental models principle that people must hear the purpose 7-11 times before it lands. Lencioni and Gatena independently arrive at the same number.

**Discipline 4: Reinforce Clarity**
- Every policy, system, and program should remind employees what is really important
- Hiring, firing, performance management, compensation, and onboarding must all reinforce the answers to the six questions
- **Servant Leadership Link:** This IS Structure Demands Behavior. Every structure either reinforces or undermines clarity. What you honor, you get more of.

## DEATH BY MEETING (The Four Meeting Types)

Meetings fail for two reasons: they lack **drama/conflict** and **structure**. The solution: four distinct meeting types, each with a different purpose.

### Meeting Type 1: Daily Check-In
- **Duration:** 5-10 minutes
- **Purpose:** Share daily priorities; administrative alignment
- **Rules:** Don't sit down. Keep it administrative. Never cancel.
- **No agenda needed** — just quick sharing

### Meeting Type 2: Weekly Tactical
- **Duration:** 45-90 minutes
- **Purpose:** Review weekly activities/metrics, resolve tactical obstacles
- **Structure:**
  - **Lightning Round** (1 min per person): 2-3 priorities for the week
  - **Progress Review**: Critical business metrics
  - **Real-Time Agenda**: Built from what surfaced in the lightning round
- **Key Rule:** Do NOT set the agenda in advance. Let it emerge. **Postpone any strategic discussions** — save them for the Monthly Strategic.

### Meeting Type 3: Monthly Strategic (Ad Hoc)
- **Duration:** 2-4 hours
- **Purpose:** Discuss, analyze, and decide upon critical strategic issues
- **Rules:** Limit to 1-2 topics. Prepare in advance. Engage in real conflict.
- **This is where the big decisions happen** — give them the time they deserve.

### Meeting Type 4: Quarterly Off-Site Review
- **Duration:** 1-2 days
- **Purpose:** Step back from operations and look at the business holistically
- **Four topics:** (1) Strategy reassessment, (2) Team behavior review, (3) Personnel review, (4) Competitive and industry review

**Key insight:** "A leader seeing meetings as drudgery would be like a doctor viewing surgery that way." Meetings ARE where leadership happens.

**Servant Leadership Link:** Meeting structure IS structure demanding behavior. If you don't separate tactical from strategic, you'll drown in the urgent and starve the important. The Weekly Tactical maps to operational strategy (leg 1 of the stool). The Monthly Strategic maps to organizational and financial strategy (legs 2 and 3). The Quarterly Off-Site is where you recalibrate the Nesting THEREs.

## SILOS, POLITICS AND TURF WARS (The Thematic Goal)

### The Problem
Silos turn colleagues into competitors. They devastate organizations, kill productivity, push good people out, and jeopardize goals.

### The Solution: The Thematic Goal Model

**Thematic Goal (Rallying Cry)**
- A single, qualitative focus shared by the entire leadership team
- Time-bound (3-12 months)
- Bridges long-term vision and short-term tactics
- Qualitative, not quantitative — a rally cry, not a metric

**Defining Objectives**
- 4-6 temporary, qualitative components that clarify the Thematic Goal
- **Shared by ALL leaders** — not departmental. Every leader owns every objective.

**Standard Operating Objectives**
- Ongoing objectives that don't change period to period (revenue, quality, engagement, etc.)
- Monitored continuously alongside the thematic goal

### How It Breaks Silos
When everyone rallies around shared defining objectives, departments stop competing and start collaborating. The key: **every leader owns every defining objective**, not just the ones in their department.

**Servant Leadership Link:** The Thematic Goal IS the current Nesting THERE milestone. The Defining Objectives are the Strategic THEREs supporting it. The structural insight is the same: "If everyone owns it, no one owns it" — but Lencioni adds that EVERY leader must own EVERY objective, which prevents silo thinking. This is We There at the organizational level.

## THREE SIGNS OF A MISERABLE JOB (Employee Engagement)

Any job — from CEO to janitor — becomes miserable when these three conditions exist. And any job can be made fulfilling when they're eliminated.

### Sign 1: Anonymity
- Feeling that your manager has little interest in you as a human being
- The manager knows nothing about your life, aspirations, or interests
- **Solution:** Managers must take a genuine interest in their people as individuals — know their stories, families, goals
- **Servant Leadership Link:** Maps to Maxwell's Law of Connection ("touch a heart before asking for a hand"), Groeschel's "Needed and Known" principle, and the Listening/Perspectives mental model. Anonymity is the opposite of servant leadership.

### Sign 2: Irrelevance
- The inability to see how your work matters to someone
- Everyone needs to know their job matters **to someone**
- **Solution:** Help every employee understand specifically who benefits from their work and how
- **Servant Leadership Link:** This is the Transcendent There applied at the individual level. When people can connect their daily work to a purpose bigger than themselves, they move from Me There to We There. Nesting THEREs should make this connection explicit all the way down to SMART goals.

### Sign 3: Immeasurement
- The inability to assess your own contribution or success
- When you can't measure your own progress, you depend on the subjective opinions of managers
- **Solution:** Help employees identify tangible ways to measure their own progress daily or weekly
- **Servant Leadership Link:** The Freedom V requires clear boundaries AND clear measures of success within those boundaries. Immeasurement means the Freedom V exists but has no scoreboard. Also connects to Nesting THEREs — every SMART goal should have a metric the individual can track themselves.

### Key Insight
Lencioni places the responsibility squarely on **managers**. A manager who knows their people (eliminates anonymity), helps them see impact (eliminates irrelevance), and gives them self-assessment tools (eliminates immeasurement) can make ANY job fulfilling. This is S-L-Y in action — the misery is structural and leadership-driven, not the individual's fault.

## THE MOTIVE (Why Leaders Lead)

### Two Types of Leaders

**Reward-Centered Leaders**
- Believe leadership is the **reward** for hard work
- Expect the experience to be pleasant, prestigious, and enjoyable
- Choose what they work on and avoid anything mundane or uncomfortable
- Calculate the personal cost of uncomfortable tasks and try to skip them
- Leave their people without direction, guidance, and protection

**Responsibility-Centered Leaders**
- Believe leadership is a **responsibility**, not a reward
- Accept that leading should be difficult and challenging
- Define their role through service and sacrifice
- Put the needs of their team before their own
- Sacrifice preferences, time, and energy for the good of the team

**Servant Leadership Link:** This is the purest distillation of We There vs. Me There applied to the act of leading itself. Reward-centered = Me There leadership. Responsibility-centered = We There leadership. Maxwell's Law of Sacrifice reinforces: "A leader must give up to go up." The Transcendent There is what sustains responsibility-centered leaders through the hard parts — without purpose, leadership feels like burden; with purpose, sacrifice feels like calling.

### The Five Omissions of Reward-Centered Leaders

These are the critical tasks that reward-centered leaders avoid:

**1. Having Difficult and Uncomfortable Conversations**
- Avoiding hard feedback, letting problems fester
- **Servant Leadership Link:** The Moment of Truth process exists specifically for this. Reward-centered leaders skip Step 1 (Reality) because it's uncomfortable.

**2. Managing Direct Reports (Really Managing Them)**
- Claiming they "trust their subordinates" when actually abdicating responsibility
- True management = "helping them set direction, ensuring alignment with peers, and identifying obstacles early"
- **Servant Leadership Link:** The Freedom V is NOT hands-off leadership — it's intentional boundary-setting with active engagement. "Trust without clarity = misdirected work" (Groeschel).

**3. Running Great Team Meetings**
- Viewing meetings as drudgery rather than the central activity of leadership
- **Servant Leadership Link:** Use Lencioni's Four Meeting Types to structure this.

**4. Developing the Leadership Team**
- Neglecting team cohesion and development
- **Servant Leadership Link:** Maxwell's Level 4 (People Development) and the 5-Step Equipping Process.

**5. Communicating Constantly and Repetitively**
- Failing to over-communicate clarity
- **Servant Leadership Link:** People need to hear the purpose 7-11 times. Repetition is not redundancy — it's reinforcement.

## GETTING NAKED (Vulnerability in Service)

### The Three Fears That Sabotage Client Loyalty (and Team Trust)

**Fear 1: Fear of Losing the Business**
- Ironically, this fear HURTS your ability to retain clients/trust
- **Overcome it:** Always consult instead of sell. Give value before asking for anything. Tell the kind truth even when they won't like it.

**Fear 2: Fear of Being Embarrassed**
- The desire to avoid looking ignorant or foolish
- **Overcome it:** Enter the danger — step into uncomfortable situations. Ask "dumb" questions others are afraid to ask. Make suggestions you aren't sure of — one breakthrough idea outweighs ten misses.

**Fear 3: Fear of Feeling Inferior**
- The need to preserve importance and social standing
- **Overcome it:** "Nothing is more attractive than people who willfully and cheerfully set their egos aside."

**Servant Leadership Link:** Getting Naked is servant leadership applied to every professional relationship. All three fears are Me There impulses — protecting ego, status, and comfort. The inverted pyramid requires setting these aside. The Pillars of Referent Power are built by those willing to be naked — influence comes from authenticity, not image.

## KEY LENCIONI PRINCIPLES FOR SERVANT LEADERS

### On Teamwork
- "Not finance. Not strategy. Not technology. It is teamwork that remains the ultimate competitive advantage, both because it is so powerful and so rare."
- "Teamwork requires sacrifice up front; people who work as a team have to put the collective needs of the group ahead of their individual interests."

### On Trust and Vulnerability
- "Remember teamwork begins by building trust. And the only way to do that is to overcome our need for invulnerability."
- "When truth takes a backseat to ego and politics, trust is lost."
- "Great teams do not hold back with one another. They are unafraid to air their dirty laundry."

### On Conflict
- "When there is trust, conflict becomes nothing but the pursuit of truth."
- "If everyone is in agreement, then someone is not thinking."

### On Organizational Health
- "The single greatest advantage any company can achieve is organizational health. Yet it is ignored by most leaders even though it is simple, free, and available to anyone who wants it."

### On Leadership
- "Success is not a matter of mastering subtle, sophisticated theory but rather of embracing common sense with uncommon levels of discipline and persistence."
- "Bad meetings are the birthplace of unhealthy organizations."

### On Accountability
- "Accountability is an act of care, not aggression."
