# Servant Leadership Mental Models

Complete reference for the 18 mental models from the Servant Leadership framework (Edition 3).

---

## 1. The Three-Legged Stool

A purpose-driven, systemically integrated, self-governing enterprise stands on three legs:

- **Seat** = Organizational Purpose (the unifying THERE — mission, purpose, or vision statement)
- **Side** = Leadership (holds everything together; exists to serve the purpose)
- **Three Legs** = Strategies
  - **Operational Strategy** — how we create and deliver value
  - **Organizational Strategy** — how we structure and lead people
  - **Financial Strategy** — how we sustain resources over time

### Key Principles
- The legs are not silos — they are strands of a braided rope, interdependent
- If one leg frays, the whole stool wobbles
- Purpose sits at the top — not the leader. Leaders serve the purpose.
- When leaders put themselves at the center, it creates centralized, blame-based culture

### Quantified Systemic Understanding (QSU)
The ability to see how all three legs affect each other. Leaders with QSU can:
- **Read the Cash Story** — see how money flows through the organization
- **Feel the Culture** — understand values, trust levels, and behaviors that shape momentum
- **Track Operations** — notice rhythms, workflows, and quality patterns

### The "Flight Simulator"
A spreadsheet or predictive financial model that lets leaders test scenarios safely before committing resources. Run multiple cases (low, base, high) so the leadership team sees the same picture of reality.

### Healthy Strategy Indicators

**Operational:** Clear career ladders, training programs that build KSAs, financial understanding for all, leaders who understand the QSU model, ways to measure health (goal achievement, productivity, quality, satisfaction).

**Organizational:** Culture defined by actions not posters, hiring for values alignment, structure and accountability that prevent confusion, leadership development, ways to measure health (climate surveys, 360-degree feedback, retention, leadership pipeline strength).

**Financial:** Understanding the cash story, treating numbers as indicators not outcomes, predictive cash flow models, the ease of measurement as a discipline.

## 2. Structure Demands Behavior

**The foundational principle of the entire system.**

Structures can be physical or mental: buildings, codes of conduct, mental models, concepts, frameworks, perspectives. Mental models are the most powerful type of structure because when adopted, they create understanding and unity of effort.

"Culture is a mental model." If "culture eats strategy for lunch," it's critical to have incentive, reward, and authority structures that support purpose.

### The Overton Window
The period of time for something to move from outrage → indifference → acceptable → celebrated (or vice versa). Leaders must watch for cultural drift — behaviors that were once unacceptable becoming normalized.

**Key insight:** In the age of social media, the Overton Window can collapse from decades to days. Leaders must be vigilant about defending culture.

### Incentive Structures
- What you honor, you get more of. What you shame, you get less of.
- If incentive structures reward individual gain ("Me" thinking), they create a centralized control culture
- If incentive structures reward mission alignment ("We" thinking), they create self-governing culture
- **The principle holds: what you honor, you get more of.**

## 3. Self-Governing vs. Centralized Control Culture

Two types of organizational culture:

### Centralized Control Culture (Reactive)
- Built on external enforcement — rules, policies, authority pushing down
- Leader-focused: "Leader Elevates" → decision power → "Blame Culture" → reactive, self-preservation
- When mistakes happen: find who to blame → stories before facts → silos and "kingdoms" form
- Creates dependency and fear of punishment
- Energy consumed by reacting instead of creating

### Self-Governing Culture (Proactive)
- Built on internal ownership — shared values, servant leadership, inverted triangle
- Purpose-focused: "Servant Leadership" → process → "High-trust, aligned, empowered" → proactive, team learning
- When mistakes happen: check structure first → facts before story → shared responsibility
- Creates alignment without coercion, energy without blame, creativity without fear
- **The inverted triangle**: Leadership holds authority but chooses to apply it in service of those below

### How Cultures Are Created
- **Centralized Control:** Mistakes → blame → story before facts → silos → "kingdoms" → leader becomes more central → cycle perpetuates
- **Self-Governing:** Mistakes → SLY check → facts before story → shared responsibility → team learning → trust grows → cycle elevates

## 4. S-L-Y (Structure, Leadership, You)

When something goes wrong, check in this order:

- **Structure (85%)** — Ask questions assuming either STRUCTURE or LEADERSHIP is the cause. Every person or unit is NOT the assumption. Only after exhaustion of structure and leadership can we fully face an individual as the issue.
- **Leadership (10%)** — Look for misaligned incentives, unclear expectations, or leadership that hasn't communicated clearly
- **You (5%)** — Only a small fraction of the time is the individual directly at fault, when they either refuse to perform or act outside the organization's values

### Application
When results miss targets, don't blame — investigate:
1. Was the structure sound? Were incentives aligned?
2. Did leadership communicate clearly? Were expectations defined?
3. Only after ruling out 1 and 2: Is this genuinely an individual performance issue?

This creates **Team Learning** — where mistakes become opportunities for growth, silos break down, and trust is built.

## 5. Moment of Truth

A 4-step conflict resolution process using S-L-Y:

1. **Reality** — What are the observable facts? (Not feelings, not stories — facts)
2. **Shared Story** — Build a shared understanding from the facts. Never "my story vs. your story" — seek shared facts first.
3. **Plan** — Based on shared reality, what do we do next?
4. **Feedback** — Follow up. Did the plan work? Adjust as needed.

**Key rule:** Facts are prioritized over stories. This transparency produces Team Learning, where mistakes are treated as opportunities to grow.

## 6. HERE-Path-THERE (The Core Leadership Model)

Everything in life follows this model:

### THERE — The Destination
- Where you want to go: a project completed, a skill mastered, a culture transformed, a community renewed
- Requires **Clarity** (everyone knows what it is) and **Commitment** (everyone is aligned to it)
- People must hear the purpose 7-11 times before it truly lands
- If people do not share the same THERE, they will pull in different directions → friction, wasted energy, mission failure

### HERE — The Starting Point
- Current reality, told truthfully
- Requires **Humility** — seeing reality as it is, not as we wish it were
- Leaders often confuse wishes with facts, or substitute feelings for truth
- **Egocentric bias:** People tend to overestimate their own contributions by as much as 130%
- True HERE is supported by facts, not feelings. Feelings matter — they tell us how people experience the journey — but facts are what ground decision-making.

### Path — The Bridge
- Not a straight line but a dynamic process: **Plan → Act → Learn → Adjust → Repeat**
- Every step teaches something about ourselves, the team, or about THERE
- Progress is not guaranteed by motion alone — it comes from taking steps in the right direction, guided by clarity, tested against reality, and adjusted with wisdom

### THP vs. HTP
- **THP (There-Here-Path)** — Creative and proactive. Define a vision, assess reality honestly, build a path. This is leadership.
- **HTP (Here-There-Path)** — Reactive and escapist. Starts with "I don't like where I am" and jumps to "I want relief" with a quick fix. Leads to short-term thinking, addiction patterns, and drift.

## 7. The Transcendent There

The ultimate purpose behind all other goals. A purpose so great it can never be fully reached, but gives direction forever.

- Viktor Frankl: *"Those who have a 'why' to live can bear almost any 'how.'"*
- Mark Twain: *"The two most important days in your life are the day you are born and the day you find out why."*
- A Transcendent There integrates life — work, family, relationships, service align under a single unifying direction

### Resume Virtues vs. Eulogy Virtues
- **Resume virtues:** Job titles, salaries, awards, square footage — things the marketplace rewards
- **Eulogy virtues:** How you served, cared, loved — things people say at your funeral
- True legacy is built on eulogy virtues. Servant leadership is choosing to live not for self, but for others.

### The Cost of No Transcendent There
- Without it, life collapses into oscillating structures: work and home, family and hobbies, consuming and distracting
- Midlife crises often come from realizing our THERE was too small
- 49,500 people died by suicide in the US in 2022 — a reminder that without a compelling "why," despair can take root

## 8. Nesting THEREs and Goal Setting

Vision cascades into daily action through nesting:

1. **Transcendent THERE** — The ultimate purpose (e.g., "To serve others to the glory of God")
2. **Strategic THEREs** — Categories of activity that connect to the Transcendent THERE (Organizational, Operational, Financial strategies)
3. **Milestones** — Shorter-term markers that show progress toward strategies. Must be SMART. Revisited at least annually.
4. **SMART Goals** — Department and individual goals broken into specific, measurable, achievable, results-oriented, time-bound actions

### Key Rules
- Every strategy must have a **champion** — "If everyone owns it, no one owns it"
- Strategies are **temporary vehicles**; purpose is permanent
- Milestones are **confidence markers** — they prove the strategy is working
- Writing goals down increases success probability from 36-40% to 75-76%
- Personal SMART goals matter too — "who they were outside of work inevitably shaped who they were inside of work"
- **Annual strategy sessions** should include managers at all levels (not just executives)
- **Tactical Theres** = SMART goals that are the closest to daily action

### Accountability
- When leaders work with employees to set SMART goals monthly or quarterly, they establish clear expectations, feedback loops, and growth paths
- Accountability partners keep us anchored to the We There
- The research: *"In one study, people who wrote down their goals and shared them with others achieved success 76% of the time, compared to only 26% who had unwritten goals."*

## 9. Freedom V

Accountability through boundaries. The V shape represents:

- **Wide boundaries** at the top — for self-directed, proven people who have earned trust
- **Narrow boundaries** at the bottom — for those still learning, who need more structure and guidance
- Consequences drive behavior within the structure

**Key insight:** Freedom isn't the absence of boundaries — it's the presence of clear ones. People thrive when they know exactly where the lines are.

---

## 10. Self-Governance (Inverted Pyramid)

The servant leadership organizational model:

- Traditional hierarchy: Leader at top → authority flows down → people serve the leader
- Servant leadership: Leader at bottom → authority flows up → leader serves the people
- Leadership holds authority but chooses to apply it in service of those below
- Requires: shared vision, equipping people with tools and training, clear Freedom V's

## 11. Pillars of Referent Power (Influence)

You have the greatest influence by being an example worth following. Six pillars, all resting on **Values**:

1. **Physical** — health, energy, presence
2. **Financial** — stewardship, generosity, discipline
3. **Social** — relationships, community, service
4. **Family/Team** — investment in those closest to you
5. **Mental/Emotional** — self-awareness, learning, resilience
6. **Spiritual** — faith, purpose, transcendence

**Principle:** Influence is not about position or authority — it's about the life you model.

---

## 12. Project Mood Curve (Perseverance)

Every project follows four phases:

1. **Forming** — excitement, optimism, early energy
2. **Storming** — conflict, doubt, "the Pit" — this is where most people quit
3. **Norming** — patterns emerge, trust builds, rhythm develops
4. **Performing** — excellence, flow, high output

**The Tipping Point** is in the Storming phase. Leaders who persevere past the Pit reach Performing. Those who quit restart at Forming with a new project — and hit another Pit.

## 13. Victim Circle vs. Freedom Circle

You only control three things:
1. **Your actions**
2. **Your perspective**
3. **Whom you trust**

- **Victim Circle:** Controlled by circumstances, blames others, waits for external change
- **Freedom Circle:** Chooses response, owns perspective, selects influences intentionally

## 14. Conflict Resolution

Four styles on a grid:

|  | Low Collaboration | High Collaboration |
|---|---|---|
| **High Competition** | Competing | Collaborating |
| **Low Competition** | Avoiding | Accommodating |

**Key insight:** You cannot skip competition and jump straight to collaboration. Healthy conflict requires the courage to compete (advocate for your position) before true collaboration can emerge.

---

## 15. Proactive Use of Emotions

Two patterns:

- **Reactive:** Feel → Act → Think (emotional hijacking)
- **Proactive:** Feel → Think (LIDD) → Act

**LIDD Framework:**
- **L**isten — to the emotion and what it's telling you
- **I**nvestigate — gather facts, check assumptions
- **D**ecide — make a conscious choice
- **D**ismiss — if the emotion is not grounded in reality, let it go

Spiritual maturity adds **conscience** before action — a values check.

---

## 16. Systems Thinking

Three systems of the mind:

- **System 1** — Subconscious, automatic reaction (fast but often wrong)
- **System 2** — Deliberate investigation and analysis (slow but accurate)
- **System 3** — Values-based decision making (wisdom — the goal)

**Goal:** Reprogram System 1 through intentional System 2 and System 3 practice. Over time, wise responses become automatic.

---

## 17. Communication/Framing

"Win the framing, win the conflict."

- Good framing triggers a System 1 response in your audience (instant recognition, emotional resonance)
- Never engage with an opponent's framing — delegitimize it and elevate your own
- The leader who defines the conversation controls its outcome

---

## 18. Listening/Perspectives

- Listening = seeing what others see, not just hearing their words
- Play a movie in your head of their perspective — what does the world look like from where they stand?
- **Humility is seeing reality as it is** — not as you wish it were, not filtered through your ego
- The best leaders are the best listeners. They make others feel truly understood.

## 19. The Two Theres (We There vs. Me There)

Every moment is a choice:

### We There (Outward)
- Mission-oriented, others-focused, serving something bigger than self
- The highest form: the Transcendent There — a clearly defined higher purpose for life
- Structure demands behavior toward We: purpose, values, accountability, community, habits, rhythms

### Me There (Inward)
- Self-oriented, self-serving, protecting ego, comfort, status
- The natural human default without intentional structures
- Left to default, the Me There wins

### Key Structures That Point Toward We
- **Transcendent There** — written down, returned to daily
- **Consumption** — garbage in, garbage out; be intentional about media, books, mentors, podcasts
- **Relationships** — you are the average of your five closest friends
- **Accountability and Community** — shared accountability, truth and facts before story
- **Incentives** — reward We behavior, not Me behavior
- **Habits and Rhythms** — daily/weekly practices that reinforce purpose (morning routines, journaling, weekly reviews, church)

### The Tale of Two Sergeants
- **Sergeant Harris:** One act of extraordinary courage in crisis — pulled a fallen soldier's torso from a burning vehicle. Remembered for something he *did*.
- **Sergeant Daniels:** A lifetime of daily servant leadership. Dove into a canal to save trapped soldiers. Saved three. Lost his own life. Remembered for who he *was*.
- Harris will be remembered for a moment. Daniels will be remembered for a way of life.
- **"The We There is lived daily, in habits, in structures, in values, in service. It doesn't need one defining moment, because every moment is defined by purpose."**
