# Building Product Conviction

> Source: Andy Johns and Matt Greenberg, Mastering Product Delivery

---

## Why Conviction Matters More Than Ideas

The bottleneck is never "we don't know what to build." It is "we don't know which ideas will work, and we keep investing in wrong ones." Product conviction is evidence-backed confidence, not enthusiasm. It is a structured accumulation of customer signals, market data, and technical feasibility justifying committing scarce engineering resources.

---

## Problem Classification

### Painkillers
Acute, urgent need felt regularly. Customers articulate without prompting. Existing workarounds clearly insufficient. High willingness to change behavior. Conviction threshold: moderate (pain is self-evident, but solution still needs validation).

**your product:** "I want to engage daily but I forget and lose my streak."

### Vitamins
Nice-to-have. Customers agree it "sounds nice" but do not bring it up. No workaround because pain is not acute. Risk of building something tried once and forgotten. Conviction threshold: high -- most common source of failed big bets.

**your product:** "What if users could see artwork alongside engagement sessions?"

### Vegetables
Valued abstractly but not urgently sought. Very low emotional engagement. Often internal priorities dressed as user needs. Conviction threshold: very high, often unattainable. Reframe as painkiller or deprioritize.

**your product:** "Users should organize saved content into folders." Reframe: "Users cannot find the engagement they shared with a friend, causing them to stop sharing" -- now a painkiller.

## The Customer Signal Spectrum

### Level 1: Stated Interest
"I would use that." Very weak. People are polite. Use only for early exploration, never as basis for big bet. Strengthen by asking about past behavior, looking for emotional intensity, counting unsolicited mentions.

### Level 2: Soft Commitment
Waitlist signup, email provided, beta join. Moderate signal -- low-cost action could still be aspirational. Benchmarks: 5-15% in-app prompt signup is healthy. Any organic referral from waitlisted user is very strong.

### Level 3: Hard Commitment
Money down, LOI, plan upgrade for early access. Strong -- real sacrifice reduces the gap between stated intent and actual behavior. Use before committing engineering to a big bet.

### Level 4: Behavioral Proof
Post-launch data: actual usage, retention, engagement. Definitive. Metrics: activation rate, repeat usage within 7 days, retention impact, revenue impact.

## The Problem Framing Document

### Template

**Section 1: Customer Problem**
- Who experiences this? (Specific segment)
- How often? (Daily, weekly, situationally)
- Current workaround? (If none, is the pain real?)
- Evidence? (Signal level and strength)

**Section 2: Solution Hypothesis**
- What will we build? (One paragraph, not a spec)
- Why will this solve the problem? (Reasoning, not hope)
- Primary success metric? (One metric, not a dashboard)

**Section 3: Key Assumptions**
- What must be true? (3-5 assumptions)
- Which one, if wrong, kills the bet? (Riskiest assumption)

**Section 4: Kill Criteria**
- Under what conditions do we stop? (Specific, measurable)
- By when will we know? (Date or milestone)

### Assumption Pressure-Testing by Function

| Function | Key Question |
|----------|-------------|
| Engineering | Can we build at required quality in timeline? |
| Design | Is the interaction model learnable? |
| Support | Can we support without unsustainable ticket load? |
| Commercial | Can we price, package, and sell this? |
| Content (your product) | Editorially sound and brand-appropriate? |

---

## Kill Criteria

**Good:** "If <15% of beta users return within 7 days, pause." "If engineering prototype shows >500ms latency, deprioritize." "If 3 of 5 foundational advisors flag concerns, stop."

**Bad:** "If it doesn't feel right." "We'll know it when we see it." No criteria at all.

## Conviction-Building Timeline (2-4 weeks)

| Week | Activity |
|------|---------|
| 1 | Problem classification + existing signal review |
| 1-2 | Customer interviews (8-12) focused on past behavior |
| 2 | Soft commitment test (waitlist, beta signup) |
| 2-3 | Problem framing document + stakeholder alignment |
| 3 | Hard commitment test (if viable) |
| 3-4 | Kill criteria definition + go/no-go decision |

## Common Anti-Patterns

**"The CEO Wants It":** Enthusiasm bypasses conviction. Use idea/recommendation/command framework. Ask: "Should we explore or is this decided?"

**Survey-Driven Conviction:** 80% "Yes" on surveys = politeness, not behavior. Replace with behavioral proxies.

**Competitor-Driven Conviction:** "They launched X, so we need X." Apply conviction framework independently -- competitor launches are context, not customer signals.

**"We're Agile" Skip:** "We'll just build MVP and iterate." For big bets, this wastes months. Conviction-building is the upfront work that makes agile execution effective.
