## Part 1: The 12-Competency Growth Model

### Overview

The Growth Competency Model is a structured framework for evaluating, hiring, and developing growth practitioners. It identifies 12 core competencies organized along two axes -- execution (right side) and strategy (left side) -- and maps practitioners into three archetypes based on their competency center of gravity.

The model solves a critical hiring problem: most companies evaluate growth candidates on resume logos, past company scale, or title seniority rather than on the specific competencies the company actually needs at its current stage.

### Execution-Side Competencies (Right Side)

**1. Channel Fluency**
- Deep expertise in specific growth channels: SEO, paid acquisition, email, push, virality, ASO.
- Diagnose channel performance and identify optimization opportunities.
- Level 1: Execute within a single channel. Level 2: Manage 2-3 channels. Level 3: Evaluate and select channel mix for a business model.

**2. Instrumentation & Data Fluency**
- Define, implement, and analyze growth metrics.
- Comfort with analytics tools (Amplitude, Mixpanel) and data warehouses.
- Level 1: Read dashboards. Level 2: Define metrics, build tracking plans. Level 3: Design instrumentation systems and build growth models from raw data.

**3. Experimentation**
- Design, run, and interpret experiments.
- Know when to use A/B tests vs. pre/post vs. qualitative research.
- Level 1: Run A/B tests. Level 2: Multi-variant experiments and backlog management. Level 3: Build experimentation culture across an organization.

**4. Productizing Learnings**
- Translate experiment insights into product features and systems.
- Move from one-off tests to scalable product changes.
- Level 1: Write experiment summaries. Level 2: Ship experiment winners as features. Level 3: Architect product systems embedding growth mechanics.

**5. User Psychology**
- Behavioral psychology applied to product usage.
- Map motivations, anxieties, and decision-making processes.
- Level 1: Basic user motivations via qualitative research. Level 2: Apply psychological frameworks to experiment design. Level 3: Build user mental models informing product strategy.

**6. Prioritization & Roadmapping**
- Evaluate and rank growth opportunities by impact, effort, strategic alignment.
- Level 1: ICE/RICE scoring. Level 2: Quarterly growth roadmap with sequencing logic. Level 3: Align growth roadmap with company strategy and resource constraints.

### Strategy-Side Competencies (Left Side)

**7. Strategic Communication**
- Present growth strategy to executives, board members, cross-functional partners.
- Build narratives around growth data that drive investment and alignment.
- Level 1: Present experiment results. Level 2: Build investment cases. Level 3: Influence company strategy through growth narratives.

**8. Team Leadership**
- Build, develop, and retain a growth team.
- Create psychological safety for risk-taking in experimentation.
- Level 1: Mentor junior practitioners. Level 2: Hire and manage a team of 3-5. Level 3: Build a growth org of 10+ across sub-teams.

**9. Stakeholder Management**
- Work effectively with executives, product, engineering, design, marketing.
- Navigate organizational politics to secure resources and alignment.
- Level 1: Effective with direct partners. Level 2: Manage VP/Director relationships. Level 3: Influence C-suite and board.

**10. Growth Loop Modeling**
- Identify, map, and quantify growth loops.
- Understand loop dynamics: reinforcing loops, balancing loops, delay effects.
- Level 1: Identify existing loops. Level 2: Model loop performance and leverage points. Level 3: Design new loops and predict systemic impact.

**11. Cross-Functional Influence**
- Get things done through teams you do not manage.
- Build alignment through shared goals, data, storytelling rather than authority.
- Level 1: Collaborate on cross-functional projects. Level 2: Lead initiatives without authority. Level 3: Embed growth thinking into other teams' processes.

**12. Vision Setting**
- Articulate compelling long-term growth vision.
- Connect daily growth work to strategic direction.
- Level 1: Articulate team-level goals. Level 2: Set 1-2 year growth vision. Level 3: Shape company strategy through a growth lens.

## Part 2: The Three Growth Archetypes

### The Optimizer (Surgeon)

**Competency center of gravity:** Channel Fluency, Instrumentation, Experimentation.

Excels at squeezing maximum performance from established loops. Deep functional knowledge in specific channels and tactics. Can look at a retention curve and immediately identify three experiments to flatten the drop-off.

**Deploy when:** Growth loops exist but underperform their theoretical maximum. Clear, measurable gap between current and potential performance. Company needs to scale a proven motion.

**Do NOT deploy when:** No systematic growth process exists (need Builder). Loops are plateauing and need reinvention (need Innovator). Company has not achieved PMF.

**Typical backgrounds:** Growth marketing specialists, performance marketing managers, CRO specialists, data analysts with channel expertise.

### The Builder (Architect)

**Competency center of gravity:** Productizing Learnings, User Psychology, Prioritization & Roadmapping.

Establishes new growth systems from scratch. Designs the experimentation process, sets up instrumentation, defines metrics, and builds the first growth loops. Comfortable with ambiguity. Creates structure where none exists.

**Deploy when:** Company has PMF but no systematic growth process. Growth is founder-led and needs to transition to a team function. New product lines need growth systems built from zero.

**Do NOT deploy when:** Well-established loops need optimization (need Optimizer). Company needs strategic evolution (need Innovator).

**Typical backgrounds:** Product managers with growth experience, growth generalists from early-stage companies, entrepreneurial growth practitioners.

### The Innovator (Painter)

**Competency center of gravity:** Strategic Communication, Team Leadership, Stakeholder Management, Growth Loop Modeling.

Identifies emerging opportunities and evolves the growth model. Operates at the strategic level, looking for new growth vectors when existing loops plateau. Has influence and communication skills to get organizational buy-in.

**Deploy when:** Existing loops are plateauing. Competitive landscape is shifting. Company needs fundamentally new growth approaches.

**Do NOT deploy when:** No growth infrastructure exists (need Builder). Existing loops are healthy but not optimized (need Optimizer). Company is pre-PMF.

**Typical backgrounds:** Senior growth leaders, heads of growth, VPs of growth/product, former founders.

### Career Progression Through Archetypes

Growth careers progress by moving through archetypes:

- **Phase 1: Validating Impact** -- Early career. Demonstrate direct business results. Typically operating as Optimizer.
- **Phase 2: Demonstrating Repeatable Success** -- Mid-career. Prove results were not circumstantial. Transition from Optimizer to Builder.
- **Phase 3: Unlocking Optionality** -- Advanced career. Advisory, interim, portfolio roles. Operate as Innovator.

### Using the Model for Team Assessment

1. **Score your team** on all 12 competencies (1-5 scale: 1 = no capability, 3 = independent, 5 = can build organizational capability).
2. **Identify team profile:** Which archetype is most represented? Which is missing?
3. **Match gaps to stage:** Pre-A = execution-heavy; A-B = Builder blend; C+ = strategy-heavy.
4. **Build development plans:** Optimizer to Builder (develop Productizing, User Psych, Prioritization). Builder to Innovator (develop Strategic Comm, Loop Modeling, Vision Setting).

## Part 3: Growth Hiring by Company Stage

### The PMF Prerequisite

Growth amplifies what is already working. It cannot create PMF from scratch. Every hiring decision starts with an honest assessment of where the company stands.

**PMF signals (hire only after these appear):**
- Users return organically without prompting
- Word-of-mouth is measurable
- Retention curves flatten
- Users would be "very disappointed" if the product disappeared
- Self-serve revenue or strong organic traction

Elena Verna: "Hiring a head of growth too early" tops her list of growth tactics that never work.

### Pre-Seed & Seed: Founder-Led Growth

**Do NOT hire a growth leader.** The founder must own growth at this stage. Tight feedback loops between product and user behavior are essential.

Fishman's analogy: hiring a growth leader before PMF is "like strapping a jetpack to a unicycle."

**Founder priorities:**
1. Instrument the product (basic analytics).
2. Build a rough growth model (where users come from, activation %, retention %).
3. Stay close to customers (10+ conversations per week).
4. Identify the growth motion (product-led, marketing-led, or sales-led).

**What to hire instead:** Data/analytics person, growth-oriented PM, freelance advisor for specific tactics.

### Series A: First Dedicated Hire

**Profile:** Hands-on Builder who spikes on execution and customer knowledge.

**What to look for:**
- Product-oriented (works with engineering/design, not just marketing channels)
- Execution-heavy (right-side competency model)
- Comfortable with ambiguity
- Diagnostic mindset (understands before prescribing)
- Generalist across acquisition, activation, and retention

**Evaluation questions:**
- "Walk me through how you would diagnose our growth challenges in the first 30 days."
- "Describe a time you built a growth process from scratch."
- "How would you prioritize between improving activation and acquisition?"

**Red flags:** Talks only about one channel; wants to hire a team before understanding the problem; references big-company processes without adapting; does not ask about user data.

**Common missteps:**
1. **Celebrity growth leader** -- Senior people from big companies often cannot operate resource-constrained. Become frustrated, leave in 12-18 months.
2. **Acquisition-only specialist** -- Cannot fix activation or retention problems.
3. **Hiring too senior** -- VP expects a team; you need an IC who does the work.

### Series B: Expand to a Small Team (3-5)

**Two strategic directions:**

**Path A: Innovator path** -- when existing loops are approaching performance ceiling, competitive landscape is shifting, or product is expanding into new markets.

**Path B: Optimizer path** -- when loops are working but have significant optimization headroom.

**Critical decisions:**
1. Can the existing Builder level up to leadership? If yes, promote and hire Optimizers underneath.
2. Centralized or decentralized? At 3-5 people, centralized is almost always correct.
3. Are loops hitting ceilings? If yes, need innovation. If no, need optimization.

**Common missteps:**
1. Scaling hires before processes are dialed in.
2. Retention slipping as broader audiences arrive (acquisition quality problem masquerading as retention problem).
3. Over-focusing on a single loop or channel (diversification becomes critical).

### Series C+: Growth as Organizational Capability

Growth is no longer a team -- it is a function touching every part of the organization.

**Leadership needs shift to left-side competencies:**
- Strategic Communication (board-level)
- Vision Setting (multi-year)
- Stakeholder Management (executive alignment)
- Budgeting and Forecasting (CFO-level rigor)
- Cross-Organizational Coordination (shared goals across teams)

**Decentralization considerations:**
- Multiple product lines with distinct growth models -> decentralize
- Single core product -> maintain centralized core with embedded advocates
- Always maintain centralized analytics and experimentation infrastructure

## Part 4: Growth Team Structure

### Centralized Growth Teams

A dedicated unit where all members commit 100% to growth work. Has its own backlog, metrics, and leader.

**Typical composition:** Growth Lead, Growth PM(s), Growth Engineer(s), Growth Analyst, Growth Designer (shared), Growth Marketer (channel specialist).

**Advantages:** Velocity (no competition for resources), focus (clear mandate), deep expertise (concentrated practice), accountability (clear metric ownership).

**Disadvantages:** Siloed culture, organizational tension (changing other teams' products), limited scope, single point of failure.

**When to centralize:** Single product, early growth function, rapid experimentation needed, limited org-wide growth expertise.

### Decentralized (Embedded) Growth Teams

Growth practitioners sit within product, marketing, or engineering teams. Connected by a guild or community of practice.

**Advantages:** Culture spread, scale across surfaces, integration with feature work, resilience.

**Disadvantages:** Slower velocity, dilution risk, coordination overhead, inconsistent quality.

**When to decentralize:** Multiple product lines, mature playbook, need growth literacy everywhere, strong existing teams.

### The Velocity vs. Culture Trade-off

```
High Velocity (Centralized) <-----------> High Culture Spread (Decentralized)
```

The answer changes as the company evolves:
- **Year 1:** Centralize for velocity.
- **Year 2-3:** Begin embedding while maintaining core team.
- **Year 4+:** Fully decentralize if multiple products; centralize if single product.

### Hybrid Models

**Hub-and-Spoke:** Central growth team (hub) owns platform-level growth. Embedded practitioners (spokes) own team-specific growth. Hub provides frameworks, tools, coaching.

**Shared Services:** Central analytics and experimentation team serves all product teams. Product teams own growth metrics and roadmaps. Central team provides infrastructure and statistical consulting.

### Team Topology Mapped to Customer Lifecycle (Fishman / Patreon)

| Stage | Goal | Team | Key Metric |
|-------|------|------|-----------|
| Find | Awareness/discovery | Growth marketing, SEO, partnerships | Qualified traffic |
| Convince | Convert to action | Growth marketing, product marketing | Visitor-to-signup rate |
| Build | Setup/configuration | Growth product, design, engineering | Setup completion rate |
| Launch | First meaningful action | Growth product, engineering | Activation rate |
| Grow | Deepen engagement | Growth product, growth marketing | Engagement rate |
| Scale | Maximize efficiency | Growth product, monetization, analytics | LTV/CAC ratio |

**Sub-team mapping:**
- Acquisition Team (Find + Convince)
- Activation Team (Build + Launch)
- Revenue / Engagement Team (Grow)
- Monetization Team (Scale)

### Presenting Growth Strategy to Executives (Fishman)

8-part structure for securing buy-in:
1. Clear mission statement
2. Strategy narrative
3. Team organization mapped to customer journey
4. Impact preview for each sub-team
5. Vision told through the customer lens (most compelling section)
6. Execution roadmaps with quarterly plans
7. Investment cases partnered with Finance (3-year horizons)
8. Summary of asks, costs, and ROI

**Principles:** Present from the customer's perspective. Partner with Finance. Use 3-year horizons for subscription payback. Document assumptions. Start small, build evidence, then request larger investments.

### Aligned Incentive Design

Every sub-team needs its own metrics, but one unifying metric ties them together. At Patreon: Total Membership Value.

**Properties of a good unifying metric:**
- Connects user success to business success
- Every sub-team can see their contribution
- Prevents local optimization at system's expense
- Measured weekly or monthly

**Metric conflicts without unification:**
- Acquisition optimizes for volume, bringing low-quality users
- Monetization increases prices, reducing conversion
- Activation streamlines onboarding, cutting steps that improve retention

## Part 5: Elena Verna's Growth Frameworks

### 10 Growth Anti-Patterns

1. **Hiring a head of growth too early** -- before PMF, before meaningful data, before self-serve revenue.
2. **Hiring a head of growth to fix a declining business** -- growth amplifies what works; it cannot reverse product decay.
3. **Rebranding to drive growth** -- rebranding is a turbo boost, not an engine.
4. **Obsessing over competitors** -- competitor copying prevents finding your own growth model.
5. **Assuming your problems are unique** -- most growth challenges have been solved elsewhere.
6. **Over-prioritizing paid channels** -- paid is a turbo boost; organic/product loops are the engine.
7. **Failing to evolve the growth model every 18 months** -- every loop and channel saturates.
8. **Not hiring advisors** -- external perspective prevents blind spots.
9. **Over-experimenting without focus** -- running 50 experiments without a thesis is random activity.
10. **Pursuing low-impact tactics** -- button-color tests, single one-off emails, minor copy changes.

### The Motions x Levers Framework

Elena categorizes growth approaches along two axes:

**Growth Motions (how you reach users):**
1. Product-led -- growth driven by the product experience itself (self-serve, free trial, viral mechanics)
2. Marketing-led -- growth driven by channels and campaigns (SEO, paid, content, brand)
3. Sales-led -- growth driven by human interactions (outbound, demos, enterprise sales)

**Growth Levers (what you optimize):**
1. Acquisition -- getting new users into the product
2. Retention -- keeping existing users engaged over time
3. Monetization -- converting engagement into revenue

The 3x3 matrix creates nine intersections. A growth leader's specialization often maps to one or two (e.g., product-led retention, marketing-led acquisition). The matrix helps teams identify which intersections are strongest, which are under-invested, and where to hire next.

| | Acquisition | Retention | Monetization |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Product-led** | Viral loops, referral mechanics | Habit loops, personalization | Paywall optimization, upgrade paths |
| **Marketing-led** | SEO, paid, content marketing | Email lifecycle, re-engagement | Pricing experiments, bundling |
| **Sales-led** | Outbound, partnerships | Account management, success | Upsell, expansion revenue |

### Adjacent User Theory

Growth comes from expanding to serve users who are adjacent to your core users but face barriers to adoption.

**The framework:**
1. Identify your current core user (the persona who retains and pays best).
2. Identify who is "almost" your user -- interested in the problem but facing barriers.
3. Categorize barriers: product complexity, missing features, awareness gaps, trust gaps, price sensitivity.
4. Remove barriers without diluting value for core users.

Adjacent users represent the next growth frontier. Optimizing for existing users yields diminishing returns; expanding the addressable market through adjacency yields step-function growth.

### Four Dimensions of Growth Leader Specialization (Fishman)

1. **Business model expertise** -- B2C, B2B, B2B2C, marketplace, subscription
2. **Stage expertise** -- early-stage, growth-stage, mature
3. **Functional depth** -- product-led, marketing-led, sales-led
4. **Industry knowledge** -- vertical-specific patterns (fintech, health, media, consumer apps)

Leaders who develop clear specialization across these dimensions build compounding career value.

## Part 6: The Reverse Interview (Fishman & Verna)

A framework for growth leader candidates evaluating companies:

**About PMF:**
- How do you know you have product-market fit?
- What does your retention curve look like?
- What percentage of growth is organic vs. paid?

**About organizational support:**
- Where does growth sit in the org chart?
- Who does the growth leader report to?
- What engineering resources are dedicated to growth?
- How are conflicts between growth and product roadmap resolved?

**About data and infrastructure:**
- What analytics tools are in place?
- Can you run A/B tests today? How long does setup take?

**About expectations:**
- What are the growth targets for the next 12 months?
- How were those targets set?
- How long before the growth function is expected to show results?

**Red flags for candidates:**
- Company expects the growth hire to "fix" a declining business
- No engineering resources dedicated to growth
- Results expected in the first month
- No analytics infrastructure
- Growth leader reports to a non-product, non-executive function
- Company does not have clear PMF
