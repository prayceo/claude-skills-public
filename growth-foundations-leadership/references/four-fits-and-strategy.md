# The Four Fits Framework & Growth Process -- Deep Reference

*Sources: Brian Balfour, "Why Product Market Fit Isn't Enough" (essay series, brianbalfour.com); Brian Balfour, "Building A Growth Machine" (5-part essay series, brianbalfour.com); Erika Warren, Growth Foundations (Live)*

---

## Part 1: The Four Fits Framework

### Why Four Fits -- Not Just PMF

Product-Market Fit alone does not guarantee growth. Balfour observed dozens of companies with strong PMF signals (good NPS, solid retention, organic word-of-mouth) that still could not scale. The missing pieces were the other three fits.

Companies that achieve all four fits experience "Smooth Sailer" growth -- structural tailwinds that make growth feel easier than it should. Missing even one fit creates a ceiling that no amount of execution can break through.

### The System

```
Market <-> Product <-> Channel <-> Model <-> Market (loops back)
```

These four fits form a tightly coupled system. Changing one requires reassessing all others.

---

## Fit 1: Market <-> Product

**The question:** Does your product solve a real, frequent problem for a well-defined market?

### Three Elements of a Market

1. **Category:** What broad problem space are you in?
2. **Who:** Who specifically has this problem? (Not "everyone" -- a specific persona)
3. **Motivation:** What emotional or functional need drives them?

### Evaluating Market Quality

| Dimension | Strong Market | Weak Market |
|-----------|--------------|-------------|
| Size | Millions of potential users | Niche, thousands |
| Frequency | Daily or weekly need | Annual or rare |
| Pain severity | "Must solve" | "Nice to have" |
| Growth | Market expanding | Market shrinking |

**Frequency is the most underrated dimension.** A product solving a daily problem has structural advantages: more opportunities for habit formation, more data, more touchpoints for monetization, more natural moments for sharing.

### PMF Signals (Beyond NPS)

- Retention curves flatten (cohorts stabilize vs. declining to zero)
- Users spontaneously refer others without incentive
- Users are "very disappointed" if product disappears (Sean Ellis 40% test)
- Organic growth appears without paid acquisition

### Common Mistakes

- Defining the market too broadly ("people who want to be healthy")
- Confusing feature usage with value delivery
- Mistaking retention for PMF (retained users may still not be truly satisfied)
- Ignoring frequency: building for a problem that occurs once a year limits everything downstream

## Fit 2: Product <-> Channel

**The question:** Does the nature of your product match how your target users discover and adopt products?

**Key insight:** You do not control the rules of channels. Channels control the rules. Your product must adapt to fit the channel -- not the other way around.

### Channel Categories

| Channel type | Works for products that... |
|-------------|---------------------------|
| Viral / referral | Have a social/shared use case; users benefit from inviting others |
| Content / SEO | Solve questions people are actively searching for |
| Paid acquisition | Have high enough LTV to afford CPMs/CPCs; short consideration cycles |
| Sales | Solve expensive problems; buyers can be identified; ROI is demonstrable |
| App store / marketplace | Are discoverable by category; have clear search intent |
| Partnerships / community | Serve audiences that already exist in organized communities |

### Product-Channel Fit Test

1. Does using/completing the product naturally generate a shareable artifact or referral moment?
2. Does the problem your product solves map to a search query people actually type?
3. Is the product's value proposition communicable in the format of the channel (image, 30-second video, cold email)?

### Channel Assessment Diagnostic

For each potential channel, score (0-2) on five criteria:

1. **Natural fit:** Does the product's core value translate to this channel?
2. **Audience presence:** Is your specific persona active here?
3. **Value communication speed:** Can you convey value quickly in this channel's format?
4. **Scalability:** Can you grow volume without proportional cost increase?
5. **Defensibility:** Can competitors easily replicate your position?

Channels scoring 7+ are primary candidates. 4-6 are secondary. Below 4, do not pursue.

## Fit 3: Channel <-> Model

**The question:** Does your monetization model generate enough revenue per user to afford your channels?

### The Math That Matters

```
LTV > 3x CAC  (rough rule for sustainable paid acquisition)
Payback period < 12 months  (for most consumer subscription businesses)
```

### Channel Cost Realities

| Channel | Typical cost structure |
|---------|----------------------|
| Paid social | CPM/CPC -- cost increases as targeting saturates |
| Paid search | CPC -- cost driven by competitor bids |
| App store (paid UA) | CPI -- $1-5 casual, $5-20+ subscription |
| Content/SEO | Time + people cost, low marginal cost at scale |
| Viral/referral | Near-zero marginal cost if loop is strong |
| Sales | Salary + commissions -- typically requires $10k+ ACV |

### Model-Channel Compatibility

| Business Model | Compatible Channels | Incompatible Channels |
|---------------|--------------------|-----------------------|
| Consumer sub ($10/mo) | Paid social, content/SEO, community | Enterprise sales |
| B2B SaaS ($500/mo) | Content marketing, sales, paid search | Mass viral, TV |
| Ad-supported | Viral, content/SEO, social | Paid acquisition (economics rarely work) |
| Freemium | Viral, content/SEO, product-led | Direct sales (for free tier) |

## Fit 4: Model <-> Market

**The question:** Does your monetization model match the market's size and willingness-to-pay?

### The ARPU vs. Market Size Tradeoff

| ARPU | Users Needed for $100M Revenue | Typical Model |
|------|-------------------------------|---------------|
| $10/year | 10,000,000 | Ad-supported or micro-sub |
| $100/year | 1,000,000 | Consumer subscription |
| $1,000/year | 100,000 | Prosumer / SMB SaaS |
| $10,000/year | 10,000 | Mid-market SaaS |
| $100,000/year | 1,000 | Enterprise |

### Market Behavior Constraints

- **Consumer markets:** Price-sensitive, expect free/freemium, willing to pay for convenience and habit.
- **SMB markets:** Some willingness to pay, self-serve preferred, moderate price tolerance.
- **Enterprise markets:** Budget-driven purchasing, long sales cycles, high willingness to pay for proven ROI.

### Common Mismatches

- Freemium model in a market with no upgrade intent
- Enterprise pricing for SMBs who cannot afford it
- Consumer subscription where the market expects free (ad-supported alternatives exist)
- Low-ARPU model in a small market (cannot reach revenue goals)

## Diagnosing Which Fit Is Broken

When growth stalls, run through this checklist:

```
1. Retention curve: Does it flatten or go to zero?
   > Goes to zero: Market-Product fit problem. Users are not getting core value.

2. Acquisition cost: Are CACs rising, channels saturating?
   > Yes: Product-Channel or Channel-Model fit problem.

3. Conversion from free to paid: Is it declining?
   > Yes: Model-Market fit problem (wrong pricing) or Product-Channel fit (wrong users arriving).

4. LTV declining: Are users canceling faster?
   > Yes: Market-Product fit eroding (competition, or needs changed).
```

**Rule:** When one fit breaks, revisit ALL four. They are interdependent.

### Four Fits Evolution Over Time

- **Early stage:** Focus on Market-Product Fit above all else.
- **Growth stage:** Product-Channel Fit becomes critical.
- **Scale stage:** Channel-Model Fit determines profitability.
- **Maturity:** Model-Market Fit determines long-term viability.

### When Fits Conflict

Sometimes two fits are strong and two are weak. Example: strong Market-Product Fit and Model-Market Fit, but weak Product-Channel Fit and Channel-Model Fit.

**Resolution:** Redesign the product to improve Product-Channel Fit. Add features that make the product more distributable (shareable content, group features, community tools) without compromising core value.

### Multi-Market Strategies

Some products serve multiple markets (e.g., individual users AND communityes). Each market requires its own Four Fits analysis. The danger is trying to serve both with one product/channel/model configuration.

## Part 2: The Growth Machine -- Process

### Five Principles

| Principle | Process Implication |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Maximize Learning** | Every experiment must produce a learning, not just a result |
| **Seek Impact** | Prioritize by expected business impact, not ease |
| **Balance Art + Science** | Include both creative ideation and quantitative analysis |
| **Embrace Change** | Process must be lightweight enough to pivot quickly |
| **Process Before Tactics** | Never jump to a tactic without understanding which step in which loop it affects |

---

## Zoom Out: Strategic (Monthly / Quarterly)

Zoom Out answers: **"Are we working on the right things?"**

### Step 1: Identify Opportunity

- Map all steps in your primary growth loops
- Measure conversion rate and volume at each step
- Identify the highest-leverage constraint (bottleneck in the loop)
- Cross-reference against the four fits -- is a fit breaking?

### Step 2: Define and Set Goals

**North Star Metric (NSM):** The single metric that best captures the value your product delivers AND predicts long-term revenue. It should be a usage metric, not a revenue metric.

- Bad NSM: "Monthly revenue" (output, not driver)
- Bad NSM: "App installs" (input, not value)
- Good NSM for a engagement session app: "Daily engagement sessions completed" or "Days with streak active"

**North Star framework:**
```
Revenue < North Star < Input Metrics
              ^
      (value delivered to users)
```

**Input metrics:** The 3-5 metrics that most directly move the NSM.
**Counter-metrics:** Metrics that ensure you do not break something while chasing the NSM.

### Step 3: Understand the Problem

Before generating solutions, diagnose root cause:
- **Quantitative:** Where exactly in the loop is conversion lowest? What changed recently?
- **Qualitative:** User interviews, session recordings, support ticket patterns.
- **Benchmark:** How does this conversion compare to industry or historical best?

Common mistake: jumping from "growth is flat" directly to brainstorming solutions. Diagnosis narrows the solution space dramatically.

## Zoom In: Tactical (Weekly / Sprint)

Zoom In answers: **"Are we executing effectively?"**

### Step 1: Brainstorm

Generate ideas specifically tied to the identified opportunity:
- Ideas must map to a specific step in a specific loop
- Cover all lever types: acquisition, activation, retention, monetization, referral
- Do not filter during brainstorm -- quantity first

### Step 2: Prioritize (ICE Scoring)

```
ICE = Impact x Confidence / Effort

Impact (1-10): How much will this move the target metric if it works?
Confidence (1-10): How sure are we? (data, analogues, user research)
Effort (1-10): How much time/resource? (10 = very easy, 1 = very hard)
```

**Prioritization principles:**
- Prefer high-confidence, lower-effort experiments early (build momentum and learning base)
- Reserve high-effort bets for ideas with strong strategic alignment
- Always maintain a mix of quick-wins and strategic bets

### Step 3: Minimum Viable Test (MVT)

Design the smallest experiment that produces a real directional signal.

**MVT design questions:**
1. What is the single hypothesis being tested? (One variable = clean signal)
2. What is the success metric? (Define before running)
3. What is the minimum sample size needed?
4. What is the time box?
5. What will you do if it works? If it does not?

**MVT vs. Full Build:**
- Full build: 4-8 weeks, high sunk cost if it fails, better UX signal
- MVT: 1-5 days, cheap failure, fast learning, imperfect signal
- Rule: if MVT passes, THEN build the full thing

### Step 4: Run the MVT

- Control variables -- only change one thing at a time
- Do not look at results early (biases analysis)
- Document hypothesis, setup, and expected outcome before launching
- Assign clear ownership -- one person per test

### Step 5: Analyze

**Primary:** Did we hit the success metric? What was the effect size?

**Secondary (the real learning):**
- Why did it work / not work?
- What does this tell us about user behavior?
- What new hypotheses does this generate?
- Are there segment differences?

Do not mistake "no statistically significant result" for "no learning." At early stage, directional signals from small samples are often sufficient.

### Step 6: Systemize

- Winning test > permanent product feature or process
- Losing test > documented learnings (prevents re-running the same test)
- Ambiguous test > redesign with cleaner variable isolation, retest

**The experiment library:** Maintain a shared log of all tests, results, and learnings. One of the most undervalued assets a growth team can build.

## Operating Cadences

### Weekly
- Review live experiments: on track? Any anomalies?
- Ship completed experiments to analysis
- Prioritize and launch new experiments
- Unblock: what is preventing experiments from running?
- 30-minute meeting maximum -- focus on decisions, not updates

### Monthly
- Loop health review: are conversion rates at each step stable or declining?
- Channel performance: which channels are improving / degrading?
- Experiment velocity: are we running enough? Too many?
- Backlog review: reprioritize based on learnings from past month

### Quarterly
- Zoom Out fully: revisit North Star, input metrics, and Four Fits
- Team retrospective: process improvements
- Strategic bets: what big initiatives should we test next quarter?
- Org review: does team structure still match the primary loops?

## Experiment Brief Template

```
Experiment: [Name]
Hypothesis: If we [change X], then [metric Y] will [increase/decrease] by [Z%]
            because [underlying user behavior reason].

Target metric: [Primary] + [Counter-metric]
Secondary metrics: [What else we will watch]

Setup: [What is changing, for which segment, on which surface]
Sample: [Estimated users exposed/day] x [Duration] = [Total sample]
Owner: [Name]
Launch date: [Date]
Read date: [Date]

Success threshold: [What result = ship it]
Failure threshold: [What result = kill it]
Ambiguous zone: [What result = redesign and retest]

Learnings (fill in after): [What we learned regardless of outcome]
```

## Experimentation Velocity

The single best predictor of growth team success: how many experiments the team runs per week/month.

Why velocity matters:
1. Most experiments fail (70-90% is normal).
2. The compound effect of many small wins is enormous.
3. More experiments = faster learning = better future experiments.

**Target:** 2-3 experiments per week for a mature growth team.

**Velocity killers to eliminate:**
- Long approval cycles for experiment ideas
- Shared engineering resources that deprioritize growth work
- Over-engineering experiments (building the full feature instead of an MVT)
- Analysis paralysis (waiting for statistical perfection instead of directional signals)
- No experiment infrastructure (each test requires custom setup)

## Integrating Growth With Product and Marketing

### Friction with product teams (common):
- Product: "Growth experiments pollute the core product experience"
- Growth: "Product ships features without thinking about distribution"
- Resolution: Shared North Star metric. Growth experiments pass a "does this hurt core retention?" review. Product features answer "how does this drive the loop?"

### Friction with marketing (common):
- Marketing: "Growth is stealing our channels / budget"
- Growth: "Marketing is running one-off campaigns instead of building loops"
- Resolution: Marketing owns brand and channel strategy. Growth owns loop optimization. Both serve the same North Star.
