# GTM Motions and BizOps as Strategic Partner

**Source framework:** Business & Finance Foundations course by Matt Heist and Neil Shah. Draws on the Slack experience where both instructors built and scaled dual GTM motions simultaneously, plus Heist's three-stage pyramid for building strategic influence.

---

## Product-Led Growth (PLG) Deep Dive

### The PLG Cycle

```
Discovery (organic search, referral, content, ads)
    -> Sign Up (self-serve, no human required)
    -> Activation (experience core value quickly)
    -> Engagement (habitual use, increasing dependency)
    -> Conversion (free-to-paid, trial-to-paid)
    -> Expansion (individual -> team, basic -> premium)
    -> Advocacy (referral, reviews, word-of-mouth)
```

### PLG Metrics

| Metric | What It Measures |
|--------|-----------------|
| Signup rate | Visitor -> account creation |
| Activation rate | Signup -> key action completed |
| Free-to-paid conversion | Trial/free users -> paying subscribers |
| Time to value | Duration from signup to first value moment |
| Expansion rate | % of customers who upgrade tiers or add seats |
| Viral coefficient | New users generated per existing user |

### The Free Tier Decision

**Free trial (time-limited):**
- User gets full access for 7-30 days
- High activation (urgency to explore)
- Clear conversion moment (trial expires)
- Risk: users may not explore enough in the trial window

**Freemium (feature-limited):**
- User gets permanent access to basic features
- Lower activation urgency but longer evaluation window
- Risk: free tier may be "good enough" for many users

**Freemium (usage-limited):**
- User gets full features but limited usage (e.g., 5 engagement sessions/month)
- User experiences full value but wants more
- Risk: limit too high = no conversion; too low = no activation

## Enterprise Sales Deep Dive

### The Enterprise Sales Cycle

```
Lead Generation (inbound content, outbound prospecting, events, partners)
    -> Qualification (is this prospect a good fit? Budget?)
    -> Discovery (understand needs, pain points, buying process)
    -> Demonstration (show the product, prove value)
    -> Proposal (pricing, terms, implementation plan)
    -> Negotiation (contract terms, legal review)
    -> Close (signature, payment, onboarding handoff)
    -> Implementation (deployment, training, integration)
    -> Expansion (additional seats, departments, products)
```

### Enterprise Sales Metrics

| Metric | What It Measures |
|--------|-----------------|
| Pipeline | Total potential revenue in active deals |
| Win rate | % of qualified opportunities that close |
| ACV | Average Contract Value |
| Sales cycle length | Days from first contact to close |
| Cost per acquisition | Sales team cost / deals closed |
| Net Revenue Retention | Are enterprise customers expanding? |

## The Hybrid Model: Lessons from Slack

### How Slack Ran Both Motions

**PLG Motion (Bottom-Up):**
1. Individual discovers Slack (word-of-mouth, organic search)
2. Creates a free workspace
3. Invites team members
4. Team uses Slack daily, building dependency
5. Team hits free tier limits (message history, integrations)
6. Team admin self-serves upgrade to Pro plan

**Enterprise Motion (Top-Down):**
1. Sales identifies large organizations with Slack adoption signals
2. Outbound to IT/executive sponsors
3. Enterprise pitch: security, compliance, administration
4. IT evaluation and security review
5. Enterprise contract (Slack Enterprise Grid)
6. Organization-wide deployment

**The Bridge Between Motions:**
PLG created leads for enterprise sales. When a sales rep contacted a large company, Slack often already had dozens or hundreds of active users. The sale was not "convince them to try Slack" but "help them manage their existing usage at an enterprise level."

This bottom-up adoption created:
- **Proof of value**: IT leaders could see employees already chose Slack
- **Champions**: Active users became internal advocates for the enterprise deal
- **Reduced risk**: Product already proven inside the organization
- **Faster sales cycles**: Less evaluation needed

### Operational Separation

Running both motions requires operational discipline:

| Dimension | PLG Motion | Enterprise Motion |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------|
| Team | Growth/product team | Sales team + SE team |
| Primary metric | Free-to-paid conversion | Pipeline and win rate |
| Optimization cycle | Weekly experiments | Quarterly pipeline reviews |
| Customer touchpoint | In-product (UI, email, push) | Human (calls, demos, meetings) |
| Time horizon | Days to weeks | Weeks to months |

### Common Pitfalls in Hybrid GTM

1. **Metric conflicts**: PLG team optimizes for self-serve conversion while sales reaches out to the same prospects
2. **Resource competition**: Both motions compete for engineering resources
3. **Cultural clash**: PLG teams are data-driven experimenters; sales teams are relationship builders
4. **Pricing tension**: PLG wants low/free entry points; sales wants high ACV
5. **Attribution disputes**: Who gets credit when a self-serve user was also contacted by sales?

### Solutions to Hybrid GTM Challenges

- **Clear handoff criteria**: Define when a PLG user/account transitions to sales (e.g., >50 users, or >$X spend)
- **Unified customer view**: Both teams see the same customer data
- **Separate metrics, shared goals**: Each team has its own metrics but both roll up to the same revenue goal
- **Pricing architecture**: Design tiers that serve both (free -> self-serve paid -> sales-assisted enterprise)

## BizOps as Strategic Partner: The Three-Stage Pyramid

### The Problem

Most operational functions try to be "strategic" before earning the right. A new analyst joins and immediately wants to build a sophisticated model. The result: the ambitious work is ignored because the team has not established foundational credibility.

### Stage 1: Fundamentals (Food, Shelter, Clothing)

**Core activities:**
- Track budgets accurately and report on time
- Maintain headcount tracking and org planning
- Monitor top-line KPIs and report consistently
- Build data infrastructure to trust your numbers
- Respond to stakeholder requests reliably and quickly

**How long this takes:** 6-24 months. Rushing is the most common mistake.

**Signs you're ready for Stage 2:**
- Stakeholders trust your numbers without double-checking
- Your regular reports are expected and relied upon
- When numbers look wrong, people come to you first

### Stage 2: Trust & Partnership

**Core activities:**
- Build forecasting models that predict future performance
- Facilitate cross-functional planning processes
- Synthesize information across marketing, product, engineering, and sales
- Proactively identify risks and opportunities (not waiting to be asked)

**Signs you're ready for Stage 3:**
- You're invited to meetings proactively (not just when someone needs a number)
- Stakeholders ask for your opinion, not just your analysis
- Cross-functional teams seek your input before finalizing plans

### Stage 3: Seat at the Table

**Core activities:**
- Offering informed opinions on strategy and direction
- Challenging executive assumptions with data and first-principles thinking
- Contributing to resource allocation decisions
- Identifying strategic opportunities from pattern recognition across the business

**The mindset:** The pyramid is not a ladder you climb and leave behind -- it is a structure where the foundation must continuously support the upper levels.

### Applying the Pyramid to Any Function

**Product Teams:**
- Stage 1: Ship reliably, accurate tracking of feature adoption and bugs
- Stage 2: Connect feature work to business metrics ("This improved retention by 3 points")
- Stage 3: Propose product strategy based on business analysis, argue for investments based on LTV impact

**Engineering Teams:**
- Stage 1: Deliver on commitments, maintain system reliability
- Stage 2: Quantify business impact of technical decisions ("200ms latency reduction -> 1.2% conversion improvement")
- Stage 3: Shape product roadmap by identifying technical opportunities

**Marketing Teams:**
- Stage 1: Execute on time, report accurate metrics
- Stage 2: Provide customer insights that inform product decisions, build growth forecasting
- Stage 3: Set growth strategy based on market analysis

## Metrics Infrastructure

### The Metrics Stack

**Layer 1: Raw Data**
- Event tracking (product analytics -- Amplitude, Mixpanel)
- Financial data (billing system, accounting system)
- Marketing data (ad platforms, attribution tools)
- Customer data (CRM, support tickets)

**Layer 2: Computed Metrics**
- Retention curves (cohort analysis)
- Funnel conversion rates
- Financial ratios

**Layer 3: Dashboards**
- Executive dashboard (ARR, growth, margins, key ratios)
- Team dashboards (product, marketing, sales metrics)
- Operational dashboards (real-time monitoring, alerting)

**Layer 4: Analysis and Insight**
- Ad hoc analysis for specific questions
- Deep dives on anomalies
- Forecasting and modeling

### Dashboard Design Principles

1. **Fewer metrics, not more**: Executive dashboard should have 10-15 metrics, not 100
2. **Context over numbers**: Show trends, comparisons, and benchmarks -- not just current values
3. **Actionability**: Every metric should suggest an action when it moves
4. **Consistency**: Same definitions, same cadence, same format
5. **Accessibility**: If stakeholders cannot access the dashboard easily, it does not exist

## your product Application

### GTM Assessment

your product operates primarily as a PLG company. The most compelling enterprise-adjacent opportunity is community partnerships:

```
Community leader discovers your product -> recommends to their audience ->
Audience members download -> members subscribe ->
Community gets engagement data -> community buys "Community Plan" ->
Members get premium access -> increased retention ->
Members recommend to friends outside community -> organic growth
```

This is B2B2C distribution: the "business" (community) is the referral mechanism and the "consumer" (audience member) is the end user.

### BizOps Stage Assessment

As a post-PMF company, your product likely has some Stage 1 fundamentals (revenue tracking, basic product analytics) but may not have a formal BizOps function. Priority foundations:

1. **Revenue dashboard**: ARR, growth rate, gross margin, visible to all teams
2. **Retention analysis**: Cohort-based retention curves identifying behavioral triggers that predict long-term retention
3. **Revenue forecasting**: Monthly model based on subscriber growth, churn, and plan mix

### Stage 2 Opportunities

Once foundations are solid:
- Which content types drive the best retention? (Insight for content team)
- Which acquisition channels produce the highest-LTV subscribers? (Insight for marketing)
- What is the relationship between engagement depth and willingness to pay? (Insight for product)

### Stage 3 Questions

A mature BizOps function would shape:
- Should we invest in original content production vs. licensing?
- Is the community partnership channel worth a dedicated team?
- Should we explore international expansion (and if so, which markets)?
- How should we price a family plan or community plan?
