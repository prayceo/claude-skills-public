# Lever Dashboards, Empowering Product Specs & Feedback Management Systems
*Source: Sachin Rekhi, "Top Deliverables of Product Managers"; Michael Sippey, First Round Review; Mastering Product Management course*

---

## Lever Dashboards

**The PM's core analytical responsibility:** Build and review a consistent set of metric dashboards on a daily and weekly basis to maintain an active pulse on the product — and to build intuition for the natural ebbs and flows of your metrics.

**The three essential dashboards (Rekhi's framework):**

| Dashboard | What it tracks | Review frequency |
|-----------|---------------|-----------------|
| **Acquisition** | How well users are finding the product and signing up | Daily |
| **Engagement** | How often users are using the product on a regular basis | Daily |
| **Monetization** | How well the business is monetizing its offerings | Daily |

**Why lever dashboards differ from standard analytics:**
The goal isn't just tracking metrics — it's identifying the *input metrics* that most influence your output goals. A lever is a metric you can actually move. The dashboard is organized around the levers you have, connected to the outcomes you're targeting.

**Building product intuition through dashboards:**
Reviewing the same dashboards daily builds intuition. You begin to recognize what "normal" looks like — which means you recognize anomalies faster, form better hypotheses, and catch problems before they compound.

**For your product — Lever Dashboard candidates:**

*Acquisition dashboard:*
- App installs by source (organic, paid, referral, community partnerships)
- Account creation rate
- Trial start rate

*Engagement dashboard:*
- Daily active users / Monthly active users (DAU/MAU ratio)
- Engagement Session completion rate
- 7-day streak achievement rate
- Notification open rate
- D7 and D30 retention

*Monetization dashboard:*
- Trial to subscription conversion rate
- Monthly/annual subscription revenue
- Subscription cancellation rate
- Average revenue per user (ARPU)

## Empowering Product Specs

**What a product spec does:**
1. Forces you to think through experience details (the forcing function)
2. Communicates to designers, engineers, testers, PMM, data teams, and executives

**The required elements of every spec (Rekhi's minimum standard):**
- Detailed description of the **problem** you're solving
- The **target audience** for this specific feature
- The **rationale** behind the overall solution approach
- The **success metrics** you'll use to evaluate it
- Experience/design details on top of the above

**Sippey's perspective on specs:**
Silicon Valley has moved too far toward lightweight specs, losing the rigor that comes from specs as a forcing function. A spec that is too short doesn't force clarity of thought on the overall experience.

**Design principles in specs:**
A design principle is a brief, memorable statement that captures a key tradeoff your product makes. Principles help designers understand the guardrails, not just the requirements.

Example design principles:
- "Opinionated but flexible" (Notion)
- "Fast and focused" (Notejoy)
- "For the creator, not the audience" (Substack, early)

When you articulate design principles explicitly in specs, you give the design team context for making the hundreds of small decisions that don't make it into requirements.

## Feedback Management Systems

**The most important rule (Michael Sippey):**
Talk to at least one customer a day. Sippey has used this rule since his first product job at Advent in the early 1990s and considers it the single most important discipline in product management.

**The three-step customer feedback framework (Sippey, First Round Review):**

**Step 1: Set up at least 30 meetings before writing a line of code**

Line up 30 meetings with the people you'll be selling to. Talk to them about their problem and your potential solution. Pattern recognition only starts emerging around meeting 10. Segmentation only becomes clear around meeting 20. You only truly understand what to build around meeting 30.

**The 55-minute meeting structure (Sippey's deck, unchanged for 22 years):**
```
5 min:   Introductions
30 min:  Problem discovery
         -> "We think you have this problem. Do you?"
         -> "How are you solving it today?"
         -> "How much are you spending/how much do you care about this?"
         -> "How does this problem impact your business?"
10 min:  Proposed solution (brief!)
10 min:  Feedback and next steps
```

Note: More than half the time is spent on the problem, not the solution. This is intentional.

**Favorite ending questions:**
- "If you had $100 for our development budget, how would you spend it?"
- "Did we hit a home run or a base hit?"
- "Would you be willing to beta test?"
- "What would you be willing to pay?"

**Step 2: Get in the van**

Bring your entire team — lead engineer, lead QA, lead support, salesperson. Everyone needs to hear customers describe the problem they're about to tackle. After the meeting, debrief immediately:
- How sophisticated was this business/person?
- What were their top 3 problems?
- Would they beta test?
- Any surprises?

**Step 3: Focus on their problem, not selling your solution**

The jukebox story: Sippey's team built a digital jukebox product and went to talk to bar owners. The bar owners liked the idea — but weren't the actual buyers. Jukeboxes are owned by third-party distributors. By "getting in the van," they discovered they had the wrong target customer entirely, before shipping.

**Scott Cook (Intuit) principle, cited by Rekhi:**
The goal of customer discovery isn't to confirm what you already believe. It's to find surprises. Quantity and quality of valuable surprises is what a PM optimizes for in discovery.
