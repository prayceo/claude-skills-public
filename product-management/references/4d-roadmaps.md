## Why Traditional Prioritization Fails

### The Problems with RICE

RICE (Reach x Impact x Confidence / Effort) has real advantages — it's systematic, quantifiable, and creates a sense of rigor. But it has structural biases that produce low-leverage roadmaps:

**Bias 1: Incrementalism**
RICE favors work with high confidence and high reach. High confidence = things we've seen work before. High reach = features for the largest audience. This systematically deprioritizes strategic and vision work, which has higher investment requirements and less certain returns.

**Bias 2: No top-down constraint**
RICE is a bottom-up ranking exercise. You brainstorm initiatives, score them, and take the top scorers. This produces a roadmap where the items improve different objectives and different metrics — optimized locally but unfocused globally.

**Bias 3: Leadership buy-in is harder**
An unfocused roadmap is an unfocused story. When every initiative is the "best" at something different, there's no coherent narrative to take to leadership.

### The Problems with Voting

Team voting has similar problems. Ideas that are popular with the team win, which means:
- Ideas that feel tangible and shippable get prioritized
- Abstract but important strategic work loses votes
- The loudest advocates for their pet ideas have outsized influence

## The 4D Framework in Full

### How It Works

1. **Define target KPIs** — What 2-3 metrics are you specifically trying to move this quarter?
2. **For each lens, brainstorm objectives** — What objectives from this lens would move those KPIs?
3. **For each objective, brainstorm initiatives** — What product work would achieve that objective?
4. **Prioritize across objectives and initiatives** — Constrained by the target KPIs, pick the highest-leverage set

The constraint is the key differentiator. Every initiative must connect to a target KPI. This forces focus and creates the coherent story.

## Lens 1: Strategy

**What it asks:** What initiatives further the product along its strategic dimensions?

**How to use it:**
Look at your Product Strategy Map (6 PMF hypotheses). For each hypothesis, ask: where is the gap between the current product and what the strategy requires?

Strategic brainstorming questions:
- If we're serving our current audience well, what adjacent audience should we expand to next?
- How can we better fulfill our core value proposition?
- What would strengthen our competitive moat?
- What are we promising customers that the product doesn't yet fully deliver?

**The LinkedIn Sales Navigator example:**
Customers kept requesting more search filters (customer lens). But the strategy lens asked: "How do we better fulfill the 'targeting the right buyer' value proposition?" This revealed that search itself was friction — the right answer wasn't more filters, it was an entirely different targeting paradigm. Strategy lens found what customer lens missed.

**Characteristics of strategy lens work:**
- Generally longer time to implement (weeks to months, not days)
- More needle-moving when it works (fundamentally improves the product)
- Higher investment uncertainty (harder to predict exact impact)
- Often left out of RICE/voting because of this uncertainty

**Signs of over-indexing on strategy lens:**
- Steady, unanticipated decline in engagement metrics (users feel unheard)
- Long droughts with no customer-facing improvements
- Customer feedback is consistently ignored in favor of "strategic initiatives"
- Team morale declining among customer-facing people (support, success)

## Lens 2: Vision

**What it asks:** What initiatives develop the product closer to the vision narrative you've articulated?

**How to use it:**
Take your vision narrative (the 1-2 page prose document describing the future state). Break it into milestone capabilities — what foundational work has to happen to make the vision achievable?

Vision lens brainstorming questions:
- What does the product need to be able to do in 2-3 years that it can't do today?
- What groundwork needs to be laid now for those future capabilities?
- Is there anything we're currently building that moves us farther from the vision?
- What are we NOT doing that future us will wish we had started now?

**Why this lens is critical:**
Without the vision lens, it's easy to avoid investing in groundwork that doesn't yield immediate results. The quarterly pressure to show measurable wins pushes this work out continuously. The vision lens is the forcing function that ensures you're building toward something.

**Characteristics of vision lens work:**
- Often more speculative (further from today's known problems)
- May not show immediate metric improvement
- Creates optionality for future product directions
- Highest risk of deprioritization in short-term-oriented orgs

**Signs of over-indexing on vision lens:**
- Significant investment in new initiatives despite core product having clear improvement opportunities
- Existing customer requests going unaddressed for multiple quarters
- New features going underutilized because core product experience is still broken
- High churn among customers who say "great vision but the basics don't work"

## Lens 3: Customer

**What it asks:** What initiatives solve problems that customers have explicitly surfaced through feedback?

**How to use it:**
Pull your Feedback Management System data. What are the top recurring themes from customer conversations, support tickets, NPS verbatims, and surveys? Translate these into product objectives.

Customer lens brainstorming questions:
- What's the #1 pain point customers mention in support conversations?
- What's the most common feature request in surveys or interviews?
- What do churned users say they didn't get from the product?
- What do highly satisfied users say they wish worked better?

**Characteristics of customer lens work:**
- Generally clearer scope (user has described what they want)
- Faster to implement (more incremental)
- Good for engagement and retention (users feel heard)
- Creates trust and goodwill

**The risks of over-indexing on customer lens:**

*Risk 1: Long-term lack of differentiation*
Building everything customers ask for produces a product that looks increasingly like competitors who also built everything customers asked for.

*Risk 2: Power user optimization trap*
The most vocal feedback comes from power users. Over-serving power users at the expense of the experience for casual and new users is one of the most common ways products stagnate.

*Risk 3: Complexity spiral*
Each new customer request adds a niche feature. Over time, the product becomes increasingly complex. New user onboarding gets harder.

**Signs of over-indexing on customer lens:**
- Steady decline in the impact of new features (diminishing returns)
- Product getting increasingly complex and hard to explain to new users
- Features that most users don't use appearing more frequently
- The roadmap is essentially a feature request list

## Lens 4: Business

**What it asks:** What initiatives improve the critical input metrics that drive business outcomes most directly?

**How to use it:**
Start with your target KPIs (the output metrics you're trying to move). Work backward to the input metrics most likely to influence them. Brainstorm initiatives that move those input metrics.

Business lens brainstorming questions:
- What input metrics most directly drive our target output KPIs?
- What's the single biggest leverage point in the acquisition funnel?
- Where in the activation funnel is the largest drop-off?
- What's causing our highest-value users to churn?

**Characteristics of business lens work:**
- Short feedback loops (experiments can run quickly)
- Often measurable in days or weeks
- Keeps the business moving while strategic work matures
- Familiar territory for data-driven teams

**Signs of over-indexing on business lens:**
- Features that improve metrics in week 1 but decline in weeks 2-4 (optimization without value creation)
- Growing A/B test factory with diminishing experiment lifts
- Heavy investment in conversion optimization while product quality stagnates
- Metrics up, satisfaction down (gaming the metrics)

## The Balance Principle

**"Keep the lights on" lenses (incremental, immediate):**
Customer + Business — maintain engagement, keep the business moving, users feel heard

**"Something big" lenses (needle-moving, longer-term):**
Strategy + Vision — build toward the future, create competitive advantage, make meaningful product progress

A healthy roadmap has work from all four. Tracking the distribution of your roadmap across lenses is a diagnostic tool: if you're all customer+business, you're optimizing without strategy. If you're all strategy+vision, you're ignoring real users today.

## The 4D Process Step-by-Step

**Step 1: Define 2-3 target KPIs for the quarter**
These are your constraints. Every objective and initiative must connect to at least one. Examples: D30 retention, trial to subscription conversion rate, monthly subscriber growth.

**Step 2: Strategy lens brainstorm**
Review your Product Strategy Map. Where are the gaps? What initiatives would close the biggest strategic gap while moving your target KPIs?

**Step 3: Vision lens brainstorm**
Review your Vision Narrative. What progress can you make toward it this quarter? What foundational work would enable next year's vision work?

**Step 4: Customer lens brainstorm**
Review the top 3-5 themes from customer feedback (support, interviews, NPS, surveys). What product work addresses the most important ones?

**Step 5: Business lens brainstorm**
Review your Lever Dashboards. Which input metrics are furthest from target? What initiatives would move them?

**Step 6: Prioritize across all four lens outputs**
You now have a constrained set of options — all must connect to your target KPIs. Prioritize for the greatest total expected impact. Look for the combination that best moves your KPIs while maintaining balance across the four lenses.

**Step 7: Build the leadership narrative**
Because every item connects to your target KPIs, you now have a coherent story: "This quarter we're focused on [target KPIs]. Here's what we're doing across each lens, why, and how it connects."

## Roadmap Diagnostic Questions

**Is your roadmap working?**
- Can you trace every initiative to a target KPI? (if no, add constraint)
- Is there work from all four lenses? (if no, identify which lenses are missing)
- Can you tell the roadmap as a single coherent story? (if no, objectives are disconnected)
- Are you making strategic bets, not just quick wins? (if no, vision/strategy lenses underweighted)
- Does leadership understand the narrative? (if no, buy-in will be harder)

**Red flags:**
- All items are customer requests or A/B tests (all customer + business lens)
- You can't explain why the top items are the top items
- Every item has a different owner with a different objective
- The roadmap is essentially a ranked feature list
- Nothing on the roadmap would excite you to share with users

## OOKRS: The Full Framework

### Outputs vs. Outcomes

**Output OKR:** "Ship redesigned onboarding flow by March 15"
- Measurable: yes/no
- Tells you: did you ship it?
- Doesn't tell you: did it work?

**Outcome OKR:** "Increase D7 retention from 32% to 40%"
- Measurable: yes/no plus degree
- Tells you: did user behavior change?
- Creates the question: why did it move (or not)?

The OOKRS shift focuses teams on the impact they're creating, not just the work they're doing.

### The Output/Outcome Spectrum

Not everything can be purely outcome-based. Sometimes the right KR is a deliverable:
- When you're building foundational infrastructure with no immediate measurable outcome
- When the outcome is genuinely unknowable (first-time product experiments)
- When the deliverable is the commitment stakeholders need (launch date)

The rule: default to outcomes. Use output KRs only when you can't define a meaningful outcome KR, and be explicit about why.

## The OKR Learning Loop

### Step 1: Set OOKRS with Explicit Predictions

Before the quarter begins, document:

**For each Objective:**
- Why is this objective the right priority right now?
- What strategy or vision lens does it serve?
- What happens if we don't make progress here?

**For each Key Result:**
- What specific number are we committing to move?
- What's our starting baseline?
- What's our target?

**The prediction (most teams skip this):**
- Which initiatives on our roadmap do we believe will move which KRs?
- By how much do we predict each initiative will contribute?
- What's our confidence level?

**Example prediction document:**
```
OKR: Increase D7 retention from 32% to 40% (+8pp)

Prediction by initiative:
- New onboarding engagement session series: +4-5pp (high confidence)
- Streak notification improvements: +2-3pp (medium confidence)
- Content calendar refresh: +1pp (low confidence)

Total predicted: 7-9pp. Target: 8pp.
```

### Step 2: Execute the Roadmap

Standard execution. One addition: track which initiatives ship when, so the postmortem can be clean. If the onboarding redesign ships in week 6 of an 8-week quarter, its impact will be partial.

### Step 3: Run the Postmortem

At quarter end, for each OKR:

**The core questions:**
1. Did the KR move? By how much vs. target?
2. Which initiatives contributed to the movement?
3. For initiatives that didn't move the KR: why not?
4. What surprised us? What did we predict wrong?
5. What do we now believe about how this metric moves?

**The "update your model" step:**
After answering these questions, explicitly update your mental model:
- "We now believe onboarding content quality matters more than onboarding length"
- "We were wrong that notification frequency was the lever — it was notification timing"

### Step 4: Feed Learning into the Next Roadmap

The postmortem findings update three things:

1. **The Product Strategy Map** — If results revealed your value proposition isn't landing, update the hypothesis.
2. **The Next Roadmap** — Double down on approaches that worked. Deprioritize approaches that didn't.
3. **Future Predictions** — Your confidence intervals should get tighter as you run more loops.

## OKRs as Stakeholder Communication

OKRs are the primary vehicle for keeping senior leadership informed.

**The weekly OKR update format:**
- Current KR status (on track / at risk / off track)
- Why (one sentence each)
- What's happening next week to address any at-risk KRs

**The quarterly OKR review format:**
- Final KR results vs. targets
- The postmortem findings (what we learned)
- How those learnings inform next quarter's priorities

The goal is never to look good — it's to build credibility through honest communication. Teams that communicate bad results with good analysis earn more trust than teams that only surface good news.

## Decision Architecture

### The PM's Decision Role

The job isn't to make perfect decisions. It's to create a system where the best ideas win, decisions get made efficiently, and the team trusts the process.

### The Critical Discipline: Communicate Decision Rationales

PMs often share decisions but rarely share rationales. The rationale is what:
- Validates that you heard and considered input
- Makes the assumptions explicit (tells the team when to revisit)
- Forces clarity of thought
- Creates organizational learning

### Decision Types and Velocity

**Decision matrix:**

| | Reversible | Irreversible |
|---|---|---|
| **Low stakes** | Decide quickly; one person is enough | Small group; document rationale |
| **High stakes** | Test it; A/B if possible; bias toward action | Full stakeholder alignment; careful deliberation |

**The most common mistake:**
Applying high-stakes irreversible process to low-stakes reversible decisions. This creates organizational slowdown without adding value.

### Sippey on the Art of Decisions

The hardest decisions in Sippey's career at Twitter weren't "what to build" — they were decisions about what to change or remove from a product that users had strong feelings about. Features that had vocal advocates. Behaviors that had become culture.

The key skills frameworks don't teach:
- **Knowing what signals to trust** when data and intuition conflict
- **Managing the emotional dimension** of decisions that affect vocal users
- **Holding conviction** while remaining genuinely open to being wrong
- **Knowing when to move fast** and when to slow down despite pressure

## OKR Templates

### Setting OKRs

```
QUARTER: [Q]

OBJECTIVE 1: [Inspiring, qualitative direction]

Why this objective: [One paragraph explaining why this is the right priority]
Strategy/vision connection: [Which lens — strategy, vision, customer, business?]

  Key Result 1.1: [Outcome metric] from [baseline] to [target]
  Key Result 1.2: [Outcome metric] from [baseline] to [target]
  [Optional output KR if needed]: [Deliverable] by [date]

PREDICTIONS:
  Initiative A -> Expected to contribute +[X] to KR 1.1 because [hypothesis]
  Initiative B -> Expected to contribute +[X] to KR 1.2 because [hypothesis]
  Confidence: [High/Medium/Low] based on [evidence]
```

### OKR Postmortem

```
POSTMORTEM: [Quarter]

KR RESULTS:
  KR 1.1: Started at [X], ended at [Y], target was [Z]
           -> Outcome: [Hit / Missed / Exceeded]
  KR 1.2: Started at [X], ended at [Y], target was [Z]
           -> Outcome: [Hit / Missed / Exceeded]

INITIATIVE ATTRIBUTION:
  Initiative A: Did it ship? [Yes/No/Partial]
               Did it move KR 1.1? [Yes/No/Unknown]
               Predicted contribution vs. actual: [+Xpp predicted / +Ypp actual]

SURPRISES:
  What moved more than we expected: [List]
  What moved less than we expected: [List]
  What didn't move at all: [List]

UPDATED BELIEFS:
  We now believe: [New understanding about how metrics move]
  We were wrong about: [Hypothesis that didn't hold]
  We're still uncertain about: [Open questions]

FEED FORWARD:
  Strategy Map updates: [Any changes to the 6 hypotheses]
  Next quarter roadmap implications: [What to do differently]
  Next quarter prediction calibration: [What we know more/less confidently now]
```
