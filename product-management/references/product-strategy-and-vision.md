# Product Strategy & Vision — Deep Reference
*Source: Sachin Rekhi, "Top Deliverables of Product Managers"; Mastering Product Management course syllabus; Sachin Rekhi course launch essay*

---

## The Product Strategy Map in Full

### Why Treat Strategy as Hypotheses

A product strategy is not a static document. It's a set of hypotheses you're constantly testing against market reality. The reason to call them hypotheses:

1. **They evolve** — as you learn from customers, competitors, and market shifts, each hypothesis should be refined or pivoted
2. **They're testable** — you can design customer conversations and experiments to specifically validate or invalidate each one
3. **They're ranked by risk** — the riskiest hypothesis is the one to validate first (not the one that feels most interesting)
4. **They create learning culture** — teams that treat strategy as hypotheses learn faster than teams that treat strategy as decisions

## The 6 PMF Hypotheses in Depth

### Hypothesis 1: Problem to Solve

**What it is:** The acute pain point your product addresses.

**The critical constraint:** Do NOT define the problem in terms of the solution you're proposing. Focus solely on the customer pain. Require yourself to describe the full context in which this pain arises.

**Wrong:** "Customers need a better way to track their daily engagements." (solution-framed)
**Right:** "consumers who want to maintain a daily daily habit consistently fail to do so because there's no structured experience that makes it as easy as checking Instagram — they know what they want to do but the habit doesn't stick." (customer-pain-framed)

**The depth test:** Can you describe the experience of the problem from the customer's perspective without mentioning your product once? If not, you're still in solution-space.

**How to evolve this hypothesis:** Customer discovery conversations. Listen for unprompted descriptions of the problem. When customers describe a version of the problem you didn't anticipate, your hypothesis needs updating.

**your product problem hypothesis evolution:**
- Initial: "People want to engage more"
- After discovery: "consumers who want a daily daily habit fail to maintain it because there's no structured experience that makes engagement as habitual as checking social media"
- After data analysis: "adults 25-55 who have tried and failed to maintain a daily engagement practice need a guided, streak-based experience that makes consistency feel achievable rather than aspirational"

Each refinement makes every downstream decision clearer — what content to produce, what notifications to send, what the onboarding should emphasize.

### Hypothesis 2: Target Audience

**What it is:** The best-fit customer segment for your product.

**The precision requirement:** Be as specific as possible — who your current MVP is targeted toward TODAY, not who you could eventually serve.

**Rekhi's example:** LinkedIn Sales Navigator started with "B2B sales professionals." After customer discovery, refined to: "B2B sales professionals in large enterprises in the technology and financial services industries working in account executive or sales development roles."

That level of precision:
- Gave the product team clarity on what use cases to optimize for
- Gave the sales team a clear ICP for prospecting
- Made every tradeoff decision easier ("would a Director of Sales Dev at a fintech enterprise use this?")

**How to evolve this hypothesis:** As you discover which users get the most value from your product (highest retention, highest NPS, highest LTV), tighten the definition around them. As you expand, broaden it — but always know the tightest version.

**The audience coherence check:** Does the problem you're solving resonate most strongly with this specific audience? If your best-retained users aren't the ones you thought they'd be, your target audience hypothesis needs updating.

---

### Hypothesis 3: Value Proposition

**What it is:** How your solution uniquely addresses the customer's problem in a compelling way.

**The framing requirement:** A value proposition is a "promise of value" phrased in the customer's terms. How will it improve their life or business? Not what features will it have.

**Wrong:** "your product provides a curated library of engagement sessions, engagements, and premium contents with streak tracking."
**Right:** "your product makes it as easy to maintain a daily daily habit as it is to check your email — so you finally become the person you've always wanted to be personally."

**The differentiation question:** Your value proposition must explain not just what value you provide but why you provide it in a way that's meaningfully different from alternatives. "Better" is not a value proposition. "The only [specific differentiating quality] because [mechanism]" is closer.

**How to evolve this hypothesis:** When customers say "I love it because..." — that's your value proposition being validated. When they say "I tried it but..." — that's it being invalidated for a segment. Collect these moments intentionally.

### Hypothesis 4: Competitive Advantage

**What it is:** Why you will win vs. existing and future alternatives.

**The sustainable moat question:** Is your advantage defensible? Common but weak advantages:
- First-mover advantage (rarely lasts)
- Feature advantage (competitors can copy features)
- Price advantage (a race to the bottom)

Stronger advantages:
- Network effects (product becomes more valuable as more people use it)
- Data advantage (your dataset creates insights competitors can't replicate)
- Brand and trust (particularly powerful in sensitive categories like wellness)
- Distribution (relationships or channels others can't replicate)
- Switching costs (deep integration into workflows or habits)

**How to evolve this hypothesis:** Monitor competitor moves and customer reasons for choosing you vs. them. When customers choose a competitor, understand why. When they switch to you, understand what tipped the balance.

**your product competitive advantage assessment:**
- Network effects: Limited today (solo product), but community features would create them
- Data advantage: Content engagement patterns across millions of users inform what engagement sessions to produce
- Brand and trust: Strong in the product category — trust is the moat. Users choosing a mobile app need to trust the product foundation
- Distribution: Community partnerships create a distribution channel competitors without expertal relationships can't easily replicate
- Switching costs: Streak history, bookmarked engagement sessions, and personal journal entries create habit-based switching costs

The strongest current moat is likely brand trust + distribution through community partnerships. The question for strategy: which additional moat layer should be invested in next?

---

### Hypothesis 5: Growth Strategy

**What it is:** How you will attract a sufficient number of your target customers to your product.

**The growth loop requirement:** A vague growth strategy ("word of mouth," "great product") doesn't qualify. A growth strategy should specify the loop:
- What's the acquisition channel?
- What user behavior drives viral/organic growth?
- What's the conversion funnel?
- What paid channels (if any) are in the mix?

**Example:** "We grow through community partnerships where experts recommend your product to their audience, combined with word-of-mouth from streaks being shared socially, with App Store optimization for 'consumer engagement session' search capturing organic intent."

**How to evolve this hypothesis:** Track attribution carefully. As channels mature and saturate, new loops need to be identified. The growth strategy is one of the fastest-evolving of the six hypotheses.

---

### Hypothesis 6: Business Model

**What it is:** How you will monetize.

**The willingness-to-pay question:** This hypothesis requires understanding what your target audience is actually willing to pay — not what you'd like them to pay. The validation for this hypothesis often requires direct conversations asking "what would you pay?" (Sippey's advice: ask the question and ride out the uncomfortable silence.)

**How to evolve this hypothesis:** Pricing experiments, conversion rate analysis, LTV modeling. As you grow, you'll discover willingness-to-pay varies by segment — and you may find opportunities for differentiated pricing tiers.

**Common pricing pitfalls for subscription apps in sensitive categories:**
- Gating personal content behind a paywall can create a values misalignment ("should engagement cost money?"). The business model hypothesis needs to address this tension explicitly.
- The free tier must deliver enough value that users experience the core benefit before encountering the paywall. If the free experience is too good, conversion suffers. If too limited, retention suffers. The balance point is the business model hypothesis in action.
- Annual subscription pricing often outperforms monthly for habit-based products because users who commit annually are more likely to build the habit (commitment consistency effect).

## The Three Coherence Questions

Once all six hypotheses are documented, test the strategy's coherence:

**Question 1: Does the problem you're solving resonate strongest with your target audience?**
If your target audience doesn't feel the problem acutely, the rest of the strategy is built on a shaky foundation. Signs of failure: low conversion from target audience outreach, high conversion from a different segment than expected.

**Question 2: Does your value proposition adequately solve the problem?**
The value proposition must actually close the gap between the problem's pain and the customer's desired state. Signs of failure: users try the product but don't retain; users say "interesting" but don't recommend; high trial, low conversion.

**Question 3: Does your business model enable your growth strategy?**
If monetization creates friction that interrupts the growth loop, the business model and growth strategy are misaligned. Example: a model that requires payment before any value is delivered may work in a high-intent purchase context but kill viral growth loops. Signs of failure: conversion rates drop when introducing payment friction; referral rates decline after monetization changes.

## Vision Narratives in Full

### Why Vision Statements Fail

A vision statement like "We'll be the #1 personal growth app in the world" is:
- Unmemorable
- Non-specific (doesn't tell you what to build or what tradeoffs to make)
- Not inspiring (it's about the company, not about the impact on users)
- Not actionable (a designer can't use this to make decisions)

### The Vision Narrative Structure

A vision narrative is 1-2 pages of prose, written as a story:

**Part 1: The world today**
Describe the current state — the problem that exists, how people are living with it, and what's missing. Be specific. Name real behaviors, real frustrations, real workarounds.

**Part 2: What the product currently delivers**
How does the product address that reality today? What value does it provide? Be honest about where it falls short.

**Part 3: The future state (the vision)**
If the product achieves its vision, how will the world be different? Focus on the customer's life, not the company's success. Write it in the present tense, as if you're describing a world that already exists. Be specific.

**Part 4: The path**
What has to be built or achieved to get from Part 2 to Part 3? This is where the vision connects to strategy — the stepping stones that make the vision achievable.

### Why Prose Specifically

Prose forces narrative structure. When you write in prose:
- You have to decide what to say first (ordering = prioritization of what's most important)
- You have to make transitions (revealing what the logical connections are)
- You have to say things in full sentences (forcing specificity)
- You can iterate on the "how" of the telling (not just the content)

Bullets don't force any of this. You can have ten vision bullets that don't connect to each other and nobody notices.

### The Three Canonical Examples

**Jeff Bezos, 1997 Amazon Shareholder Letter:**
Sets up the vision of being "Earth's most customer-centric company" by walking through the current reality of Internet commerce, Amazon's positioning in it, the investment being made for long-term value creation, and what success looks like.

**Page and Brin, "The Anatomy of a Large-Scale Hypertextual Web Search Engine" (1998):**
Describes the problem with existing search engines (spam, low quality), the technical architecture Google was building to solve it, and the implications. Written as a research paper but functions as a vision document.

**Stewart Butterfield, "We Don't Sell Saddles Here" (Slack, 2013):**
Describes not just what Slack is, but what it's for. The narrative explains what problem is being solved, why the current solutions don't work, what a successful Slack would enable, and what the team needs to believe to get there.

All three are prose. All three are persuasive arguments, not lists. All three focus on why the world will be better, not on what features will be built.

### Writing a Vision Narrative — Practical Guide

**Step 1: Write "the world today" section first.**
Interview 5-10 users and ask: "Describe your daily engagement/engagement session practice. What works? What doesn't? What do you wish were different?" Use their language, not yours. This section should feel like a documentary — observation, not judgment.

**Step 2: Write "the future state" section second.**
Describe a specific user's day 3-5 years from now if the product achieves its vision. Be concrete: What does their morning look like? What happens when they face a hard day? How does the product fit into their life? Write in present tense.

**Step 3: Write "current product value" third.**
Be honest about what the product delivers today and where it falls short relative to the future state. This creates the tension that makes the vision compelling — it shows the gap.

**Step 4: Write "the path" last.**
What major capabilities need to be built? In roughly what order? Each step should feel achievable and connected to the one before it. This is where the vision connects to strategy and roadmap.

**Common vision narrative failures:**
- Too abstract: "We'll transform personal growth" — no one can build this
- Too tactical: "We'll add community features, then AI personalization, then..." — this is a roadmap, not a vision
- Company-centric: "We'll become the #1 consumer app" — this inspires investors, not builders
- Timeless: "Our vision is timeless excellence" — if it could have been written 10 years ago, it's too vague

## Stakeholder Buy-In: Substance + Style

Sachin Rekhi's framework for getting buy-in on strategy and vision.

### The Substance

Substance is the quality of the argument itself — the 6 PMF hypotheses, the coherence test, the evidence from customer discovery. Strong substance includes:
- Clear problem statement (not solution-framed)
- Specific target audience
- Compelling coherence across all six hypotheses
- Evidence from real customer conversations or market data

### The 6 Elements of Style

Style is how you convey the argument to make it most convincing to your target audience. Even a great argument can fail if the style is wrong for the audience.

**1. Framing**
Present a particular perspective to steer the audience toward your desired conclusion. How you frame what your audience sees sets the tone for the entire conversation. Before presenting, decide: What frame do you want them to bring to this?

**2. Social Proof**
Leverage the shared opinion of others to convince key stakeholders. Rekhi's example: At LinkedIn Sales Navigator, he brought quotes and signals from credible, recognizable companies (JP Morgan, Apple) to leadership, creating social proof that moved the decision.

**3. Logic**
Make the argument airtight. Every step should follow from the previous. Anticipate objections and address them. Don't rely on enthusiasm to cover logical gaps.

**4. Storytelling**
Use narrative structure — character (the customer), conflict (the problem), resolution (the product) — to make the argument emotionally resonant, not just intellectually sound. This is where Wellington's story arc (hook, context, tension, proposal, ask) provides the tactical structure.

**5. Visuals**
Show, don't just tell. A mockup, a customer quote on a screen, a chart of a market trend — these make abstract arguments concrete and memorable.

**6. Context**
Help the audience understand why this matters now. What's changing in the market, with customers, or with competitors that makes this the right moment?

The best presentations combine strong substance (the six hypotheses, coherence, evidence) with thoughtful style (the right framing for this specific audience, social proof they'll respect, a narrative structure that sticks).

## The Strategy Review Cadence

### Quarterly Strategy Map Review

The Product Strategy Map should be reviewed quarterly. For each hypothesis:

1. **What did we learn this quarter?** New customer insights, market shifts, competitive moves, metric surprises
2. **Does the hypothesis still hold?** If not, what needs to change?
3. **What's the riskiest hypothesis right now?** This should receive the most validation effort next quarter
4. **What experiments would test our weakest hypothesis?** Design customer conversations or product experiments specifically to validate or invalidate

### Annual Vision Narrative Refresh

The vision narrative should be updated annually:
- Rewrite "the world today" with current reality
- Update "current product value" with where the product actually is
- Adjust "the future state" based on what you've learned is possible and desirable
- Revise "the path" based on which steps have been completed and which need to change

The vision narrative is a living document. A vision that never changes is a vision that never learns from reality.

### Strategy Map + Vision Alignment Check

Quarterly, verify that the strategy map and vision narrative point in the same direction:
- Does the target audience in the strategy map match the user described in the vision narrative?
- Does the value proposition connect to the future state in the vision?
- Does the growth strategy create a path to the scale described in the vision?
- Does the competitive advantage hypothesis support the moat needed to achieve the vision?

If strategy and vision have drifted apart, reconcile them before the next roadmap cycle.

## Customer Feedback as Strategy Input

### Sippey's Feedback System and Strategy

Customer feedback is not just a source of feature ideas — it's the primary mechanism for validating and evolving strategy hypotheses. Sippey's rule of "one customer conversation a day" isn't about feature prioritization. It's about maintaining a real-time connection to the market that keeps the strategy honest.

**How feedback maps to strategy hypotheses:**

| What you hear | Which hypothesis to update |
|---------------|---------------------------|
| "I didn't know you existed" | Growth strategy — your channels aren't reaching target audience |
| "I tried it but it wasn't for me" | Target audience or value proposition — either wrong segment or wrong promise |
| "I love X but wish you had Y" | Value proposition — the promise may be right but the fulfillment is incomplete |
| "I switched to [competitor]" | Competitive advantage — your moat has a gap |
| "I'd pay more for..." | Business model — willingness to pay varies by value delivered |
| "I told my friend about this" | Growth strategy validation — organic loop is working |

**The Scott Cook surprise principle applied to strategy:**
When a customer says something that surprises you — upside or downside — that surprise is almost certainly revealing a gap between one of your strategy hypotheses and market reality. Track surprises explicitly. Review them quarterly alongside the strategy map. The highest density of surprises points to the hypothesis most in need of revision.

### The 30-Meeting Sprint as Strategy Validation

Sippey's 30-meeting sprint isn't just for pre-launch discovery. It can be applied quarterly to any hypothesis under stress. If D30 retention is declining and you don't know why, schedule 15-20 customer conversations specifically focused on the retention problem. Around conversation 10, patterns will emerge. By conversation 20, you'll know which hypothesis needs updating.
