## OKRs — Setting Goals That Actually Drive Behavior

*JZ's framework from Lenny's Podcast and Stanford course*

### What Makes an OKR Actually Work

Most OKRs fail because they measure output instead of outcome, or because they're set conservatively to guarantee hitting them. Neither is useful.

**The two failure modes:**
- **Output OKRs:** "Ship 4 new features this quarter" — measures activity, not impact
- **Safe OKRs:** Goals set to guarantee 100% attainment — signals sandbagging, not ambition

**The JZ OKR test:**
1. Does this OKR make you slightly uncomfortable? (If not, it's not ambitious enough)
2. Does it end with user or business outcome, not product output?
3. Would hitting this 70% be genuinely meaningful?
4. Does it force trade-offs? (If everything passes the OKR filter, the OKR is too broad)

### OKR Structure

**Objective:** Qualitative, inspiring, directional. Should be memorable enough to repeat without looking it up.
- Bad: "Improve the core experience"
- Good: "Make your product the first app users open every morning"

**Key Results (3–5 per objective):** Quantitative, measurable, time-bound.
- Bad KR: "Improve retention" (no number, no timeframe)
- Bad KR: "Ship streak feature" (output, not outcome)
- Good KR: "Increase 30-day retention from 42% to 55% by end of Q2"
- Good KR: "Achieve 7-day streak completion rate of 40% among new users"

### The 70% Rule

If you're hitting 100% of your OKRs every quarter, your goals aren't ambitious enough. The right calibration:
- 70% attainment = success (you aimed high and nearly got there)
- 100% attainment = goals were too easy (raise them next quarter)
- 40% attainment = goals were disconnected from reality (recalibrate)

**JZ's framing:** OKRs are tools for focus and alignment, not performance review criteria. Teams that treat OKRs as performance goals set them conservatively. Teams that treat them as focus tools set them ambitiously.

### OKR Cascading

Company OKRs → Team OKRs → Individual priorities

**The right way:** Company sets direction (objective). Teams write their own OKRs that ladder to it. Individuals understand how their work maps to team OKRs. Nobody has OKRs written for them.

**The wrong way:** Leadership writes OKRs and hands them down. Teams don't own the goals. Accountability evaporates.

**Alignment check:** Can every team member draw a line from their current work to the team OKR to the company objective? If not, there's an alignment gap.

## Roadmaps as Storytelling

*JZ's framework from Lenny's Podcast*

### The Narrative Principle

A roadmap is not a list of features and dates. It's a story about how a series of bets adds up to a destination.

The reader of a good roadmap should finish understanding:
1. What user problem are we ultimately solving?
2. Why this sequence? Why not a different order?
3. What does success look like when we get there?
4. What are we NOT doing, and why?

If your roadmap doesn't answer these questions, it's a feature list, not a strategy.

### Roadmap Architecture

**Three layers of a roadmap:**

**Now (this quarter):** High confidence, well-defined. Specific features, specific success metrics. Should have active specs.

**Next (1–2 quarters):** Medium confidence, directional. Problem areas defined, solutions still forming. Should have validated problems, not finalized solutions.

**Later (beyond 6 months):** Low confidence, aspirational. Strategic bets. Should be held loosely — don't over-specify things you can't predict.

**Common mistake:** Being equally specific across all three layers. "Later" items that are as specific as "now" items signal overconfidence in your ability to predict the future.

### Roadmap as Commitment Device

A roadmap is a public statement of what you're choosing. It says:
- We're doing these things
- Therefore we're NOT doing these other things

**The explicit no is as important as the yes.** A roadmap without clear deprioritization allows everyone to believe their priorities are on it.

**JZ's stakeholder test:** When you share a roadmap, stakeholders should be able to clearly see what they wanted that you didn't include. If everyone's happy with the roadmap, it's probably too vague to force any trade-offs.

### Roadmap Communication

**What to include in every roadmap presentation:**
- The user problem we're building toward (connects roadmap to why)
- The sequence logic (why is B after A, not before?)
- What we're not doing (and why, briefly)
- What signals would cause us to reprioritize

**Frequency:** Share roadmaps broadly and update them. A roadmap that's never updated becomes a fiction. Update it quarterly or when significant new information changes your priorities — and communicate the changes proactively.

## First 90 Days — The JZ Playbook

*From JZ's Lenny's Podcast episode and public talks*

### Phase 1: Weeks 1–4 — Listen and Map

**Goal:** Build a complete map of the landscape before drawing any conclusions.

**What to do:**
- Talk to users (at least 10–15 structured interviews)
- Talk to every cross-functional partner: engineering leads, design leads, data, marketing, sales, support
- Read everything: past specs, postmortems, strategy docs, OKRs
- Use the product obsessively — as a new user, as a power user, as an edge-case user
- Understand what the team currently believes and why

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't propose solutions
- Don't opine on what's wrong
- Don't ship anything (except possibly small, obviously good fixes)
- Don't assert your past experience as a template for what should happen here

**The trust bank:** Listening builds trust before you need it. The calls you make in weeks 5–12 will require trust you're building now. Spend these weeks earning it.

### Phase 2: Weeks 5–8 — Form Hypotheses

**Goal:** Develop a point of view based on what you've learned.

**The synthesis questions:**
- Where is the highest-value user problem that the team isn't solving yet?
- What does the team currently believe that doesn't match what you observed in user research?
- Where is the biggest misalignment between what's being built and what users actually need?
- What assumptions is the team making that haven't been validated?

**Communicate selectively:** By week 5–6, start sharing observations — framed as questions, not conclusions. "I noticed X — help me understand why that's the current approach" is better than "X is wrong, here's what we should do instead."

### Phase 3: Weeks 9–12 — Act

**Goal:** Ship one small thing that demonstrates your judgment.

**Criteria for the first ship:**
- Unambiguously good for users (not a controversial call)
- Validates that you can execute, not just think
- Shows your judgment about what matters, not just your ability to move fast

**JZ's principle:** The first thing you ship in a new role signals what you care about. Choose it deliberately.

### Working With Your CEO

*JZ's advice for product leaders working at the exec level*

**Understand their mental model first.** CEOs have strong intuitions about the product. Before pushing back, understand where those intuitions come from. Often they're based on user conversations, board feedback, or investor pressure you don't have full context on.

**Bring data, not opinions.** "Users prefer X" without supporting evidence is just another opinion. "In 15 user interviews, 12 users expressed this specific frustration" is evidence.

**Predict their reactions.** Before sharing a proposal, think through how your CEO will receive it. What will they challenge? Prepare for those challenges rather than being surprised by them.

**Make the ask explicit.** "I'm sharing this for your information" vs. "I need a decision by Friday" vs. "I'd like your input before I finalize" are three completely different requests. Be clear which one you're making.

## Stakeholder Management

*From JZ and Anand's Stanford PM course and public talks*

### The PM's Authority Paradox

PMs have significant responsibility (what gets built, when, for whom) but limited formal authority (can't direct engineers, designers, or other functions to do what they say). This gap is the defining challenge of the PM role.

**Authority comes from:**
- **Clarity:** You make the problem and solution crystal clear so teams can align
- **Credibility:** You've been right before; people trust your judgment
- **Process:** You run the decision-making process fairly, so even people who disagree respect the outcome
- **Trust:** You've listened, understood other perspectives, and represented them fairly

Authority does NOT come from:
- Title ("I'm the PM, so you do what I say")
- Escalation ("I'll get my manager to tell your manager")
- Urgency ("We need this by Thursday, period")

### Managing Cross-Functional Partners

**Engineering:**
- Bring them into problem space, not just solution space. Engineers who understand the user problem make better implementation decisions.
- Respect their technical judgment on "how." Assert your product judgment on "what" and "why."
- Protect them from context-switching. Every interruption costs more than it appears to. Batch your asks.
- Over-communicate priority. Engineers facing competing demands need clear signals on what matters most.

**Design:**
- Design is a peer in product, not a service function. Great PMs treat designers as co-owners of the problem.
- Critique on "does this solve the user's problem" — not "I don't like how this looks."
- Bring designers into user research. They see things PMs miss.
- Don't pre-solve design problems before bringing them to design. Bring them the problem, not your solution sketch.

**Data/Analytics:**
- Define success metrics before building, not after shipping. Post-hoc metrics are rationalization, not measurement.
- Build dashboards early so you're not flying blind in beta.
- Know the difference between correlation and causation in your data. "Users who use feature X retain better" doesn't mean feature X causes retention.

**Marketing/Growth:**
- Loop them in during design, not at launch. The best go-to-market strategies are built into the product, not bolted on.
- They have user insight you don't. Especially for acquisition-stage users.
- Help them understand the user's why — they'll craft better messaging.

**Support/CX:**
- Support hears from users before your analytics do. They're an early warning system.
- Brief them before every launch. A support team that learns about a feature from user complaints is a broken process.
- Review support tickets regularly — they're unfiltered user feedback.

### The "Managing Up" Discipline

Managing up means keeping leaders informed, aligned, and positioned to help — without losing your own judgment in the process.

**JZ's three-part managing-up framework:**
1. **Keep them informed** — no surprises. Leaders shouldn't learn about problems from people other than you.
2. **Ask for what you need** — be specific. "I need more engineering headcount" is less useful than "I need one more senior engineer for 6 weeks to hit our Q2 goal — here's the trade-off if we don't get it."
3. **Disagree through conversation, not action** — if you disagree with a direction, say so with evidence. Don't passive-aggressively not execute on it.

## PM Career Advice

*JZ's career framework from multiple talks and podcast appearances*

### Treat Your Career Like a Product

Apply the same rigor to your career that you apply to the products you build.

**The career PM questions:**
- Who are you building for? (Your future self — not others' expectations of you)
- What problem are you solving? (What energizes you? What would you do even if unpaid?)
- What's your north star? (Most PMs never articulate this — do the work)
- What's your current hypothesis about the path? (It will change — that's fine)

**The constraint of "doing what seems right":** Many PMs optimize for what looks impressive on a resume or what their peers are doing. These are local maxima. The global maximum is what's right for your specific skills, values, and energizers.

### The Three Career Phase Skills

**Contributor PM:** Master the craft of understanding what to build and shipping it well. This is the foundation everything else builds on. Don't rush past this phase.

**Manager of PMs:** Shift identity from builder to enabler. Your job is now to make other PMs better. The hardest part: stopping doing the work yourself when you see it being done imperfectly.

**Product executive:** Treat the product organization as your product. Who is the user? (Your team and cross-functional partners.) What is the problem? (Organizational friction, misalignment, lack of clarity.) What does success look like? (Leverage: the org generates more output per person than expected.)

### The Skill That Transforms PM Careers

*JZ's answer when asked by Lenny what single thing transformed her career:*

Learning to articulate bold goals and then make them a reality — and getting others to believe in those goals alongside you. Not just vision-setting, but the combination of inspiring ambition and practical execution. PMs who can do both at high level are rare and extremely valuable.

## Common PM Mistakes (From JZ and Anand's Teaching)

**Starting with solutions:** The most common and most costly mistake. Forces all subsequent work to justify a pre-decided answer.

**Building for the business, not the user:** Letting business metrics drive feature decisions without grounding them in user value. Short-term metrics can be optimized by hurting users (dark patterns, spam, friction reduction that reduces quality). Long-term value always comes from genuine user value.

**Conflating correlation with causation in data:** "Feature X users retain better" can mean feature X causes retention — or that retained users are more likely to discover feature X. Know which you're measuring.

**Not saying no:** PMs who say yes to everything are not PMs — they're feature request processors. The discipline is saying no (or "not now") clearly and with good reasoning, consistently.

**Mistaking motion for progress:** Shipping features is not the same as delivering user value. Track outcomes, not output.

**Skipping the user's why:** Building something technically correct that solves the wrong problem. Happens when PMs trust their instincts over user research.

**Launching to GA too fast:** Skipping or shortcutting the beta phase because of schedule pressure. The trust you burn with a bad GA launch is more expensive than the time you saved.

**Managing by escalation:** Using authority or hierarchy to force decisions rather than building alignment. Creates a culture of compliance, not ownership.
