## 1. Executive Performance Design (10-Question Framework)

Before writing a single word, answer these 10 questions. They form the strategic brief that governs every decision about structure, devices, tone, and close. (Adapted from Lynda Jackson's executive coaching framework.)

1. **What is the GOAL of this performance?** (Not "what is the topic" — what is the goal?)
2. **What do I want to DO with this audience?** (Persuade, inspire, challenge, comfort, sell, teach, unite?)
3. **What do I want to GIVE to this audience?** (Not what you want to take — what are you giving them?)
4. **What does this audience NEED to hear from me?** (Not what you want to say — what do they need?)
5. **What do I want them to REMEMBER?** (One sentence. If you can't answer this, you're not ready.)
6. **What EMOTIONAL EFFECT do I want to have on them?** (Anger? Hope? Urgency? Relief? Pride? Fear?)
7. **What do I want the audience to DO after hearing me?** (Specific action. Not "think differently.")
8. **What THREE WORDS do I want the audience to use describing this experience?** (This is your brand filter — every line of the speech should pass through these three words.)
9. **What is the TITLE of this performance?** (A strong title is a discipline. If you can't title it, the thesis isn't sharp enough.)
10. **Is this performance part of a SERIES?** (Is this one movement in a longer campaign to educate or persuade?)

**The Critical Reframe:** The greatest speakers don't write thinking "What's the next point I have to make?" They think "What's the next question I want the audience to be asking themselves?" Audiences are problem-solvers. The best presentations are mysteries that slowly unfold. Effective communication happens when brains engage and process — not when eyes passively scan slides.

## 2. The 12-Step Preparation Workflow

This is the full professional preparation sequence from speech brief to stage-ready delivery.

1. **Define the TITLE and AUDIENCE** — Who are they and what is this called?
2. **Define the GOAL** — What must this performance accomplish?
3. **Define the OUTCOME** — What do I need to accomplish with this audience?
4. **Define the LEGACY** — Answer:
   - a. What do I want them to remember?
   - b. What effect do I want on them?
   - c. What do I want them to take away?
   - d. How do I want them to describe my performance?
   - e. Am I taking a partisan or non-partisan approach?
5. **BRAINSTORM** — Generate all possible content without editing
6. **Determine STRUCTURE** (1st draft) — Select a framework from structures.md
7. **DRY RUN #1** — Talk it out aloud (not silently). Keep answers to Steps 1-3 in mind the entire time. This is where you discover what works spoken vs. written.
8. **ADJUST STRUCTURE** — Cut, reorder, tighten based on what you learned speaking it
9. **DRY RUN #2 — Add timing.** Time each section. Note where you're running long.
10. **HIGHLIGHT OMITTABLE SECTIONS** — Mark points you could cut if running overtime on the actual day. This is your "flex budget." Never cut the opening or closing — cut middle points.
11. **PREPARE MEDIA and HANDOUTS** (if used) — Visual aids should be sparse. If the audience can read everything on your slides, they don't need you.
12. **FINAL RUN** — Full rehearsal with media, handouts, timing, and delivery marks. This should feel like a dress rehearsal, not a practice.

## 3. The Public Speaking Voice (Volume, Pace, Pause)

Your public speaking voice is fundamentally different from your conversational voice. This section covers the three vocal instruments every speaker must master.

### The Core Principles

- Your voice must ALWAYS be projected. You are breathing from the diaphragm and pushing sound outward. This requires significant physical energy.
- You speak much more SLOWLY than in conversation. The audience needs time to receive and process each idea.
- You open the mouth WIDER than normal to improve clarity and word-by-word comprehension.
- You use different volumes to highlight words and add meaning — this is called **"light and shade."**
- The ONLY way the audience receives meaning is if the speaker COMMUNICATES that meaning through their voice. The voice is the instrument. Everything else is secondary.

### The Three Vocal Scales

Think of each dimension on a 1-10 scale. Your job is to move consciously along each scale throughout the speech.

**VOLUME (1-10)**
- 1-3: Intimate, confessional, drawing the audience in
- 4-6: Conversational authority, normal projected speech
- 7-8: Emphasis, urgency, key declarations
- 9-10: Peak moments, climactic lines, rallying cries

**PACE (1-10)**
- 1-3: Slow, deliberate, weighty — used for key revelations, emotional moments, gravitas
- 4-6: Normal speaking pace (but still slower than conversation)
- 7-8: Building energy, creating urgency, narrative momentum
- 9-10: Rapid-fire lists, building to a crescendo, controlled urgency

**PAUSE (1-10)**
- 1-3: Brief beat between sentences (natural breathing pause)
- 5: Section transition pause (audience resets)
- 8: Dramatic pause after a key line (let it land)
- 10: Long silence after the most important moment of the speech (5+ seconds — uncomfortable but powerful)

### "Light and Shade" — The Master Technique

Light and shade means varying volume, pace, and pause WITHIN a single passage to create contrast and meaning. A speech delivered at one volume and one pace is monotone — regardless of how good the words are. The contrast IS the meaning.

**Practical Patterns:**
- Go QUIET before you go loud (the drop creates anticipation)
- Go SLOW before a key line, then deliver the key line at normal pace (creates weight)
- PAUSE after your most important line, not before it (let the audience absorb)
- Speed UP during lists and narratives, slow DOWN for conclusions and emotional beats
- Use a WHISPER for the most intimate, vulnerable moment — it draws the audience forward physically

### Writing Delivery Marks Into Scripts

When drafting speeches, embed delivery instructions directly into the text:
- **[PAUSE]** — Standard pause (2-3 seconds)
- **[LONG PAUSE]** — Extended silence (4-6 seconds, let it sit)
- **[SLOW]** — Reduce pace significantly for this passage
- **[BUILD]** — Gradually increase volume and pace through this section
- **[QUIET]** — Drop volume to intimate level
- **[LOUD]** — Project with authority and volume
- **[SMILE]** — Physical cue for warmth or humor
- These marks are for the speaker, not the audience. They're the score to the music.

## 4. The Confident Speaker Checklist

Use this as a pre-flight checklist before any speech. Every item is a behavior to embody.

- Maintains easy eye contact by breaking the audience into SECTIONS (not darting between individuals)
- Projects voice at ALL TIMES — never drops below the volume floor
- Speaks slowly and deliberately
- Varies the pace — speed up for energy, slow down for weight
- Uses pauses AT ANY TIME — including mid-sentence for emphasis
- Has a strong, interesting start (no throat-clearing, no "thank you so much")
- Has a strong, interesting finish (no "so... yeah," no trailing off)
- Moves easily but does NOT pace (movement is purposeful, not nervous)
- Is NOT concerned when something goes wrong — pauses, fixes it, continues (never apologize to the audience for technical issues)
- Does NOT apologize to the audience (for nervousness, for the topic, for anything)
- Varies volume to communicate important words and points ("light and shade")
- Is organized SIMPLY — the audience should never feel lost in the structure
- Has a back-up set of notes easily read from several feet away (not a phone, not tiny text)
- Asks the audience questions to INVOLVE them
- Is patient and respectful with the audience
- Is not concerned when challenged — handles pushback with composure
- Enjoys the experience of public speaking (the audience can tell)
- **ALWAYS GIVES THE IMPRESSION OF BEING IN CONTROL** — This is the master rule. Everything else serves this.

## 5. Audience Psychology & The Speaker-as-Leader

### The Fundamental Insight

Drawing from crowd psychology (Elias Canetti, Sigmund Freud, Gustave Le Bon), the speaker's position relative to the audience is the position of a LEADER relative to a crowd. This is not optional — it is the structural reality of public speaking.

**Key principles from crowd theory applied to speaking:**

1. **The individual behaves differently in a group.** Your audience members are not thinking as individuals — they are thinking as a collective. Emotions, reactions, and energy are contagious within the room.

2. **The crowd expects and WANTS to be led.** Audiences do not want democracy at the podium. They want authority, clarity, and direction. A speaker who projects confidence earns trust. A speaker who projects uncertainty creates anxiety.

3. **The speaker must be comfortable with power.** Many speakers resist exercising authority over an audience — they fear seeming arrogant or controlling. But the audience's desire for the speaker to maintain order and direction is deep and real. An audience where the speaker has lost control is an uncomfortable, even frightening experience for everyone.

4. **Self-interest is suspended in a crowd.** When individuals join an audience, their personal agendas partially dissolve into the collective experience. This is why shared emotion in a room is so much more powerful than reading the same speech alone.

5. **A speaker's job is to CHANGE the audience.** A performance should leave the audience changed in some way — changed in how they think about a topic, changed in what they feel, changed in what they're willing to do. No performance should leave the audience unchanged.

### The "Performance" Mindset

A speech is not a conversation. It is a PERFORMANCE. This does not mean it is inauthentic — it means it is deliberately crafted and delivered with skill, energy, and intention. The speaker needs to decide:
- What is their ROLE with the audience? (Persuade, raise doubt, entertain, resolve, lecture, teach, sell, seduce?)
- What FEELINGS should the audience experience? (Anger, excitement, confusion, joy, fear, hope?)
- The decision dictates the style of performance and the type of persuasion to deploy.

## 6. Impromptu Speaking Framework

For those moments when you are asked to "say a few words" without preparation.

### The Quick Framework:

1. **Decide your ONE message quickly.** You have not been asked to give a speech — you've been asked for impromptu remarks. One clear idea is enough.

2. **Write down up to 3 points** on whatever is available (note card, napkin, phone). Points only — NOT full sentences. Full sentences lead to memorization, which leads to rigidity and nervousness.

3. **Do NOT try to memorize.** Memorization makes you think about words instead of communicating meaning. Trust your knowledge of the subject.

4. **Open STRONG.** Even impromptu remarks need a hook. Use a rhetorical device (question, bold statement, short anecdote). Your voice needs to project with confidence — start at volume 6/10 so you sound commanding, not tentative.

5. **If you have 3 points, SAY you have 3 points.** "I'd like to make three observations..." This gives the audience (and you) a roadmap. Numbered points are easier to deliver and easier to follow.

6. **Use rhetorical questions as transitions.** Moving from point to point is the hardest part of impromptu speaking. A rhetorical question buys you time and resets the audience's attention. "So what does this mean for us?" then pause, glance at your next point, and continue.

7. **Maintain eye contact.** This is easier when you haven't written too many words. After finishing a point: pause → look down at your next note → think about what to say → look up → speak with energy.

8. **Trust your instinct.** If a thought pops into your head mid-delivery that connects, use it. The best impromptu remarks feel spontaneous because they ARE — but within a planned structure.

9. **Close STRONG.** Even 60 seconds of impromptu remarks need a clean ending. A short callback, a one-sentence summary, or a direct statement of your core message. Never trail off.

### The "Napkin Method" — What to Write Down:
```
OPEN: [Hook — question, bold claim, or one-liner]
1. [Point one — 2-3 words]
2. [Point two — 2-3 words]
3. [Point three — 2-3 words]
CLOSE: [One sentence — callback or call to action]
```

## 7. Argument Architecture (Inductive vs. Deductive)

Two fundamental methods for structuring persuasive arguments within a speech. Choose based on audience skepticism level.

### Inductive Method (Evidence → Conclusion)
**How it works:** Present a body of specific facts, then draw a general conclusion.
**When to use:** When you have strong evidence and need the audience to arrive at the conclusion themselves. Works well with analytical, skeptical audiences (e.g., boards, investors, scientists).
**Structure:** Fact 1 + Fact 2 + Fact 3 + ... → "Therefore, [conclusion]."
**Power:** The audience feels they've been led to the conclusion by evidence, not told what to think.
**Pro tip:** Although logically the conclusion follows the evidence, in presentations it usually works better to STATE the conclusion first, then support it with evidence. The conclusion becomes a forecast; the evidence becomes proof.

### Deductive Method (Principle → Specific Application)
**How it works:** Start with a general principle the audience already accepts, then apply it to a specific case.
**When to use:** When you can anchor to a shared belief or accepted truth. Particularly powerful when the audience already agrees with the principle but hasn't connected it to your specific case.
**Structure:** Major Premise (accepted truth) + Minor Premise (specific case) = Conclusion (inevitable).
**Power:** If the audience accepts both premises, they are COMPELLED to accept the conclusion. The logic is airtight.

### Choosing Between Them:
- **Audience is skeptical?** → Inductive (let evidence do the convincing)
- **Audience shares your values but not your conclusion?** → Deductive (connect shared values to your case)
- **Complex, data-heavy topic?** → Inductive (build the evidence base)
- **Simple, values-driven topic?** → Deductive (anchor to shared principles)
- **Rule of thumb:** When you have enough evidence to build an inductive argument, do so. Well-constructed inductive arguments are the strongest use of evidence.

## 8. Political Speaking Techniques — "Claptraps"

Claptraps are rhetorical devices specifically designed to signal to the audience that a response (applause, agreement, laughter) is appropriate. They work because audiences want to respond but need a clear signal for WHEN.

### Claptrap 1: Projecting a Name
**Structure:** Identify the person (On your marks) → Say a few words about them (Get set) → Name them (Go!)
**How it works:** The build-up gives the audience time to realize applause is coming. The name is the release point.
**Usage:** Introductions, award announcements, acknowledging dignitaries. Always pause slightly before the name.

### Claptrap 2: Lists of Three
**Why three works:** Three-part lists create a sense of completeness. Lists of two feel incomplete (we instinctively add "and so on"). The most common place for an audience to respond is AFTER the third item — because they perceive completion.
**Key insight:** By "packaging" content in threes, speakers signal to audiences that a change of mood or tempo is occurring — that something worthy of attention (and possibly applause) is arriving.

### Claptrap 3: Contrastive Pairs (Antithesis)
**How it works:** The first part poses a puzzle or sets up an expectation. The second part resolves it with a contrasting idea. The audience recognizes the resolution and responds.
**Requirements for effectiveness:**
- Both parts must be SIMILAR in length
- Both parts must be SIMILAR in grammatical structure
- The second part must be a slightly modified MIRROR IMAGE of the first
- If the parts aren't balanced in length and structure, the audience loses the thread

## 9. The Acknowledge & Overcome Strategy

When you know your audience is aware of counterarguments, you have two choices: ignore the opposition or address it head-on. Research strongly supports the second approach.

### Why Acknowledge & Overcome Works:
1. It suggests fair and thorough consideration of the issues
2. It enhances YOUR credibility and may diminish the opposition's
3. It preempts objections before they solidify in the audience's mind
4. Research shows it is the most effective approach with skeptical audiences — including those who oppose your position before you speak

### How to Execute:
1. **State the counterargument fairly** — Don't strawman it. If you misrepresent the opposition, you lose credibility with anyone who knows the real argument.
2. **Acknowledge its appeal** — "I understand why some would argue..." This shows intellectual honesty.
3. **Pivot to your rebuttal** — Present evidence, reasoning, or values that override the counterargument.
4. **Return to your position strengthened** — The audience now sees your position as having survived challenge.

### Where to Place It in a Speech:
- AFTER your main argument (so the audience has context)
- BEFORE your conclusion (so you close on your strongest note, not on the opposition's argument)
- In the Classical 5-Part structure, this maps to the "Refutatio" section
