## 1. Monroe's Motivated Sequence

**Best for:** Persuasive speeches, calls to action, fundraising, policy advocacy
**Origin:** Psychologist Alan H. Monroe, 1930s

### The 5 Steps:

**Step 1 — ATTENTION** (~10% of speech)
Grab the audience with a hook (see openings-closings.md). Establish why they should listen NOW.

**Step 2 — NEED** (~25% of speech)
Demonstrate a problem that demands attention. Make the audience feel the pain.
- State the problem clearly
- Illustrate with examples, stories, statistics
- Show how it affects the audience personally
- Make the consequences of inaction vivid

**Step 3 — SATISFACTION** (~25% of speech)
Present your solution. Show how it directly addresses the need.
- State the solution clearly
- Explain how it works
- Provide evidence it works (case studies, data, testimonials)
- Address potential objections (prolepsis)

**Step 4 — VISUALIZATION** (~25% of speech)
Paint a vivid picture of the future — both positive (with solution) and negative (without).
- Positive visualization: Describe the world after adoption
- Negative visualization: Describe the world if nothing changes
- Contrast method: Toggle between both for maximum impact

**Step 5 — ACTION** (~15% of speech)
Tell the audience exactly what to do. Be specific and immediate.
- Give one clear, concrete next step
- Make it easy (low friction)
- Create urgency (why now, not later)
- End with an emotional or memorable closing line

### Example Mapping (MLK "I Have a Dream"):
- Attention: Opens with "I am happy to join with you today in what will go down in history..."
- Need: Describes the "shameful condition" of racial injustice using metaphor of a bad check
- Satisfaction: The promise of the Constitution and Declaration of Independence
- Visualization: The "I have a dream" sequence — vivid images of a just future
- Action: "Let freedom ring" — a call to persistent, nonviolent action

## 2. Duarte Sparkline

**Best for:** Inspirational/motivational speeches, vision-casting, leadership addresses
**Origin:** Nancy Duarte (Resonate, TED Talk)

### The Structure:

The speech oscillates between two states:
- **"What Is"** (Status Quo) — The current reality, its pain points, its limitations
- **"What Could Be"** (New Bliss) — The future vision, the possibility, the transformation

### The Pattern:
```
What Is → What Could Be → What Is → What Could Be → ... → NEW BLISS (ending)
```

The toggles accelerate as the speech progresses, creating rising tension and excitement.
The speech ALWAYS ends on "What Could Be" — the aspirational future.

### Key Principles:
- Start by meeting the audience where they ARE (status quo)
- Each toggle should create emotional contrast (tension → release)
- The gap between "what is" and "what could be" is where persuasion lives
- End with a vivid, elevated vision of the transformed future
- The speaker should model the emotion they want the audience to feel

### Example Mapping (Steve Jobs iPhone Launch):
- What Is: Current smartphones are hard to use, stylus-based, clunky
- What Could Be: A revolutionary device that combines phone + iPod + internet
- What Is: Every existing solution has compromises
- What Could Be: "Are you getting it?" — a single device that changes everything
- New Bliss: "Today, Apple reinvents the phone"

## 3. TED 4-Step Formula

**Best for:** Thought leadership, idea-sharing, educational talks, conference presentations
**Origin:** Analysis of top 100 most-viewed TED talks

### The 4 Steps:

**Step 1 — HOOK** (~5-10% of speech)
Open with one of three proven hooks:
1. Surprising statement/statistic (pattern interrupt)
2. Provocative question (activates problem-solving brain)
3. Personal story (creates immediate emotional connection)

**Step 2 — BIG PROMISE** (~5% of speech)
Tell the audience what they will gain. Frame from THEIR perspective.
- Use "you" language, not "I" language
- Be specific about the transformation or takeaway
- Example: "Today, you'll discover the three-step framework that helped us double retention in 90 days."

**Step 3 — BODY** (~70-75% of speech)
Deliver 2-3 main points. For each point:
- State the point clearly
- Explain WHY the audience should know it
- Illustrate with a story or example
- Provide evidence or proof
- Transition smoothly to the next point

**Step 4 — CLOSING** (~10-15% of speech)
- Recap 1-3 key takeaways (not 10)
- End with an inspiring closing statement
- Optional: Circle back to the opening hook (the "callback close")
- Give one specific action the audience can take immediately

## 4. Classical 5-Part Argument (Aristotelian)

**Best for:** Debate, policy speeches, legal arguments, academic addresses, board presentations
**Origin:** Aristotle's Rhetoric

### The 5 Parts:

1. **Exordium (Introduction)** — Establish credibility and goodwill. State the thesis.
2. **Narratio (Background)** — Provide context. Give the audience the information needed to understand.
3. **Confirmatio (Proof)** — Present arguments and evidence supporting your position.
4. **Refutatio (Refutation)** — Acknowledge and dismantle counterarguments (prolepsis).
5. **Peroratio (Conclusion)** — Summarize, make emotional appeal, call to action.

### Aristotle's Three Appeals (use throughout):
- **Ethos** (Credibility) — Why should they trust you? Experience, character, shared values.
- **Logos** (Logic) — Data, evidence, reasoning, cause-and-effect.
- **Pathos** (Emotion) — Stories, vivid imagery, appeals to values and identity.

**Pro tip:** The strongest speeches LEAD with ethos (earn trust), BUILD with logos (earn belief), and CLOSE with pathos (earn action).

## 5. Hero's Journey (Narrative Arc)

**Best for:** Personal stories, commencement speeches, brand narratives, memoir-style talks
**Origin:** Joseph Campbell (The Hero with a Thousand Faces), adapted for speechwriting

### Simplified Speech Version:

1. **Ordinary World** — Establish the status quo before the transformation
2. **Call to Adventure** — Something disrupts the ordinary (a problem, an insight, a loss)
3. **Resistance/Struggle** — The difficulty of change (vulnerability moment)
4. **Mentor/Catalyst** — A person, event, or idea that shifts everything
5. **Transformation** — The breakthrough, the lesson, the change
6. **Return with the Gift** — What you now know, and what the audience can learn from it

### The Vulnerability-to-Victory Framework:
- Acknowledge the challenge (builds credibility)
- Share the struggle (builds connection)
- Reveal the breakthrough (builds hope)
- Universalize the lesson (builds relevance)

## 6. Problem-Agitate-Solve (PAS)

**Best for:** Sales pitches, fundraising, product launches, short persuasive talks
**Origin:** Copywriting (adapted for speechwriting)

### The 3 Steps:

1. **Problem** — Name the pain clearly. Make the audience nod in recognition.
2. **Agitate** — Make it worse. Show consequences, costs, emotional toll. Turn up the heat.
3. **Solve** — Present your solution as the natural, inevitable answer. Show proof. Give a clear next step.

### Variant — PASTOR Framework (for longer speeches):
- **P**roblem — Identify the pain
- **A**mplify — Make consequences vivid
- **S**tory — Tell a story that illustrates
- **T**estimony — Show proof/social proof
- **O**ffer — Present the solution
- **R**esponse — Tell them exactly what to do

## 7. Situation-Complication-Resolution (SCR)

**Best for:** Business presentations, consulting recommendations, storytelling within speeches
**Origin:** Barbara Minto (The Pyramid Principle), used by McKinsey

### The 3 Parts:

1. **Situation** — Set the scene. What's the context? What does the audience already know?
2. **Complication** — What went wrong? What changed? What's the tension?
3. **Resolution** — What's the answer? What did we learn? What should we do?

This framework works as the MACRO structure for an entire speech, or as a MICRO structure for individual stories within a speech.

## 8. Sermon / Faith Framework (Text-Tension-Truth-Transformation)

**Best for:** Sermons, faith-based talks, devotionals, retreat addresses
**Origin:** Homiletics tradition, adapted

### The 4 Movements:

1. **Text** — Ground in scripture, story, or foundational truth. Read or reference the anchor passage.
2. **Tension** — Name the struggle. What makes this hard? Where does the human condition collide with this truth?
3. **Truth** — Illuminate the insight. What does this mean for us? What does God/wisdom reveal?
4. **Transformation** — Cast the vision. How does this change how we live, love, lead, or serve?

### Variant — The Lowry Loop (Eugene Lowry):
1. Upsetting the equilibrium (create tension)
2. Analyzing the discrepancy (explore the problem)
3. Disclosing the clue to resolution (the "aha" moment)
4. Experiencing the gospel (the good news)
5. Anticipating the consequences (what now?)

## Choosing the Right Structure: Decision Matrix

| Your Goal                          | Primary Structure         | Secondary Layer        |
|------------------------------------|---------------------------|------------------------|
| Get audience to ACT                | Monroe's Motivated Seq.   | PAS for urgency        |
| Inspire a vision                   | Duarte Sparkline          | Hero's Journey stories |
| Share an insight/idea              | TED 4-Step                | SCR for stories        |
| Win a debate/argument              | Classical 5-Part          | Rule of Three          |
| Tell a transformational story      | Hero's Journey            | Duarte for toggles     |
| Sell a product/idea quickly        | PAS                       | TED Promise            |

| Explain to executives              | SCR                       | Classical 5-Part       |
