# Product Positioning Framework

**Source framework:** Meghan Keaney Anderson and Mary Sheehan's Mastering Product Marketing course, supplemented by Anderson's writing on positioning at HubSpot, Jasper, and Watershed, Sheehan's positioning frameworks from Adobe and Google, and the blog post "How to Write a Product Positioning Statement."

---

## Why Positioning Is the Foundation

Every other product marketing activity -- messaging, launches, competitive strategy, sales enablement -- flows from positioning. If the positioning is wrong, everything built on top of it is wrong.

**Positioning determines:**
- How customers discover you (search terms, category, comparison sets)
- How customers evaluate you (what criteria they use to judge you)
- How customers describe you (word of mouth, reviews, recommendations)
- Whether they stay (do expectations set by positioning match the reality?)

## The Positioning Research Process

### Phase 1: Customer Discovery Research

**Who to talk to:**
- 5-7 recent customers (converted in last 90 days) -- understand what drove them to choose you
- 3-5 churned customers (left in last 90 days) -- understand where positioning broke down
- 3-5 prospects who did not convert -- understand what alternatives they chose and why
- 3-5 power users -- understand the deepest value they get

**Core research questions:**

**Understanding the problem:**
- "What were you trying to accomplish when you started looking for a solution like ours?"
- "What were you using before? What was not working about it?"
- "Walk me through the moment you decided to look for something new."

**Understanding the decision:**
- "What other options did you consider?"
- "What was most important to you when choosing?"
- "What almost stopped you from choosing us?"
- "What tipped the decision in our favor?"

**Understanding the language:**
- "How would you describe [product] to a friend?"
- "What is the one thing you would tell someone about [product]?"
- "If [product] disappeared tomorrow, what would you miss most?"

**Understanding the value:**
- "What has changed since you started using [product]?"
- "What can you do now that you could not do before?"
- "How does [product] fit into your daily routine?"

### Phase 2: Competitive Landscape Mapping

**Step 1: Define the consideration set**

The consideration set is not just direct competitors -- it is everything a customer might use instead of your product.

**your product's full consideration set:**
- Direct competitors: Competitor A, Competitor E, Competitor F
- Adjacent competitors: Competitor B, Competitor C, Competitor D
- Substitute categories: Secular meditation (Headspace, Calm), podcasts (community premium contents), books (daily engagements)
- Inaction: "I just try to engage on my own"

**Step 2: Map the positioning landscape**

Create a 2x2 positioning map using the two dimensions that matter most to customers:

```
                    CATEGORY-SPECIFIC
                         |
               Competitor A    |   your product
               (Catholic)|   (multi-format, multi-denomination)
                         |
              Competitor B |
              (content-only)|
SINGLE-FORMAT -----------|------------ MULTI-FORMAT
                         |
               Competitor C     |
               (Audio    |
               Content Library)    |
                         |
                         |   Headspace/Calm
                         |   (multi-format, secular)
                    GENERAL WELLNESS
```

**Step 3: Identify your distinctive space**

your product's unique positioning territory: Multi-format, category-specific, daily habit product with community features. No other major player occupies this exact intersection.

## Building the Positioning Statement

### The Positioning Statement Template

```
For [target audience]
who [situation or need],
[Product Name] is a [category]
that [key benefit / differentiation].
Unlike [primary alternative],
[Product Name] [proof of differentiation].
```

### your product Positioning Statement Options

**Option A: Habit-Focused**
```
For consumers who want to make personal growth a daily practice,
your product is a daily engagement companion
that turns personal growth into a simple, sustainable habit.
Unlike other engagement or content apps,
your product combines audio engagement sessions, guided sessions,
and community in one app designed for daily use.
```

**Option B: Community-Focused**
```
For consumers who want to grow in the product with others,
your product is a engagement session community
that connects users through daily personal content and shared engagement.
Unlike standalone content or meditation apps,
your product pairs personal engagement with a community
of people supporting and encouraging each other.
```

**Option C: Accessibility-Focused**
```
For busy consumers who struggle to maintain a daily practice,
your product is a daily daily companion
that delivers 5-minute engagement sessions, engagements, and content
in the format that fits your life -- audio, text, or video.
Unlike traditional engagement sessions or community resources,
your product meets you where you are, every day.
```

### Evaluating Positioning Options

| Criterion | Option A (Habit) | Option B (Community) | Option C (Accessibility) |
|-----------|-----------------|---------------------|-------------------------|
| True (can we deliver?) | Strong | Medium (community is newer) | Strong |
| Relevant (do customers care?) | Strong (habit is top desire) | Medium (some users want solo) | Strong |
| Distinctive (is it unique?) | Medium (Competitor E also daily) | Strong (unique combo) | Medium (convenience is generic) |
| Scalable (can we grow into it?) | Strong | Strong | Medium |
| Actionable (can we message it?) | Strong | Strong | Medium |

**Recommendation:** Option A with community as a secondary positioning pillar. The daily habit is the strongest positioning territory because it is true, relevant, and measurable.

## Positioning Evolution

### When to Revisit Positioning

Positioning is not permanent. Revisit when:

1. **The product has changed significantly.** Major new capabilities may warrant repositioning.
2. **The market has shifted.** New competitors, category evolution, or customer needs have changed.
3. **The data says so.** Win rates are declining, messaging is not resonating, churn reasons reference unmet expectations.
4. **You are entering a new market.** Expanding to a new audience may require positioning adjustment.

### your product Positioning Evolution Path

**Today:** "Daily engagement session companion for consumers seeking a daily personal habit"
**Near-term (as community matures):** "Daily daily companion connecting users through engagement, engagement, and community"
**Long-term (as platform expands):** "The home for your daily life -- daily engagement, engagement, community, and personal growth"

Each evolution expands the positioning territory without abandoning the core.

## Positioning Validation Checklist

Before committing to positioning, validate it against these tests:

- [ ] **Customer language test:** Does the positioning use words customers actually use (from interviews)?
- [ ] **Differentiation test:** Can you name what makes this different without mentioning features?
- [ ] **Clarity test:** Can a new team member explain the positioning after reading it once?
- [ ] **Believability test:** Can you prove every claim in the positioning statement?
- [ ] **Durability test:** Will this positioning still be true and relevant in 18 months?
- [ ] **Messaging derivation test:** Can you naturally create ads, emails, and landing pages from this positioning?
- [ ] **Internal alignment test:** Does every team (product, marketing, sales, support) agree?
- [ ] **Competitive test:** Does a competitor already own this positioning territory?
- [ ] **Expansion test:** Does this positioning allow the product to grow (new features, new audiences)?
- [ ] **Emotional resonance test:** Does this positioning make customers feel something?
