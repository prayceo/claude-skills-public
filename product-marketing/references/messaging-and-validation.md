# Messaging Architecture and Validation

**Source framework:** Meghan Keaney Anderson and Mary Sheehan's Mastering Product Marketing course. Covers the translation from strategic positioning into tactical messaging for specific audiences and channels, plus frameworks for validating that messaging resonates.

---

## From Positioning to Messaging

Positioning is the strategic foundation. Messaging is the tactical expression of positioning for specific audiences and channels.

**Messaging hierarchy:**

| Level | Description | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| Positioning statement | The strategic foundation (internal) | "Daily engagement session companion for personal growth as a daily habit" |
| Brand narrative | The story-form expression (homepage, about page) | "In a world of noise, your product gives you 5 minutes of peace..." |
| Value propositions | The 3-4 key benefits (feature pages, ads) | "Daily engagement sessions," "Engagement community," "Multi-format" |
| Proof points | Evidence for each value prop | "5M+ users," "500+ engagement sessions," "4.8 star rating" |
| Channel messaging | Adapted for specific contexts | App Store description, Facebook ad copy, email subject lines |

## Channel-Specific Messaging Adaptation

### App Store (ASO)

- Lead with the category and primary benefit in the title/subtitle
- Use the first line of the description for the strongest value prop
- Screenshots should show the daily experience, not feature lists
- Ratings and review count are the primary social proof

### Paid Social (Meta/Instagram)

- Lead with emotional connection (imagery of peaceful morning routine, community activity)
- Use testimonial-style copy from real users
- Short-form video (15-30 seconds) showing the daily engagement experience
- CTA: "Start free" or "Try 7 days free"

### Email Marketing

- Subject lines should reference the daily habit or the emotional benefit
- Personalize based on user behavior (new vs. lapsed vs. active)
- Include one clear CTA per email
- For re-engagement: lead with empathy, not features

### Push Notifications

- Content-driven: "Today's engagement session: [Title]" outperforms generic "Open your product"
- Timing: Match to user's historical engagement time
- Frequency: 1 per day maximum; more creates opt-out risk
- Tone: Warm and inviting, never urgent or salesy

## Messaging Validation Methods

### A/B Testing Messaging

**What to test:**
- Headlines (emotional vs. functional)
- Value prop order (which benefit leads?)
- Social proof type (numbers vs. testimonials vs. ratings)
- CTA language ("Start free" vs. "Try it free" vs. "Download now")

**Where to test:**
- App Store: Test subtitle and first screenshot
- Paid social: Test ad copy and creative
- Email: Test subject lines and preview text
- Paywall: Test headline and value prop hierarchy

**How to evaluate:**
- Measure downstream metrics, not just click-through
- A headline that gets more clicks but lower retention is not winning
- Run tests for statistical significance (minimum 1,000 exposures per variant)

### Customer Language Mining

Extract actual customer language from:
- App Store reviews (yours and competitors')
- Customer support tickets (how do users describe their experience?)
- Social media mentions (what words do people use when talking about you?)
- Customer interview transcripts (literal phrases, not your paraphrasing)

Use this language directly in messaging. Customer-sourced language consistently outperforms internally generated copy because it matches how real people think and talk about the product.

### The "Tell a Friend" Test

Ask 10 current users: "How would you describe your product to a friend who has never heard of it?"

Record their exact words. Look for patterns:
- What do they mention first? (This is your primary value prop)
- What words do they use? (This is your messaging vocabulary)
- What do they NOT mention? (Features you emphasize that users do not value)
- What emotion do they convey? (This is your brand tone)

If users consistently describe the product differently from how you position it, the users are right and the positioning needs to change.

## Messaging Anti-Patterns

**Anti-pattern 1: Feature Listing**
- "500+ engagement sessions, 200+ community groups, learning plans, streaks..."
- Fix: Lead with the outcome ("Start every day with God") not the inventory

**Anti-pattern 2: Category Confusion**
- Messaging that positions you in a category customers do not recognize
- Fix: Use the category language customers use in their own words

**Anti-pattern 3: Emotional Mismatch**
- Messaging tone that does not match the product experience (e.g., hype language for a contemplative product)
- Fix: The tone of the messaging should preview the tone of the product experience

**Anti-pattern 4: One Message for All**
- Using the same messaging for new users, lapsed users, power users, and community leaders
- Fix: Build audience-specific messaging frameworks that share positioning but vary in emphasis
