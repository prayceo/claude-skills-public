# Product Strategy, Portfolio Balance, and Power Users — Deep Reference

*Sources: "The Product Strategy Stack" (Ravi Mehta, Mar 2021); "Product Work Beyond Product Market Fit" (Fareed Mosavat et al., Jun 2020); "The Power User Trap" (Bangaly Kaba, Mar 2021); Product Strategy course; "Building Your Product Strategy Stack" (Lenny's Newsletter interview with Ravi Mehta)*

## The Product Strategy Stack — Layer by Layer

### Layer 1: Company Mission

**Definition:** The world your company sees and the change it wants to bring to that world.

**Characteristics:**
- Aspirational and emotionally motivating
- Not limited by current execution capabilities
- Durable: typically lasts years before becoming outdated
- Only changes when the company's fundamental view of the world changes

**What makes a great mission:**
- Specific enough to guide strategy but ambitious enough to motivate
- Internally motivating AND externally orienting
- Not about products or features -- about a desired state of the world

**Anti-patterns:**
- Mission defined as a product capability ("build the best X")
- Mission changed every year in response to business conditions
- Mission so vague it provides no strategic guidance ("delight users")

### Layer 2: Company Strategy

**Definition:** The logical plan you have to bring your company's mission into being.

**Characteristics:**
- Rigorously logical -- clear sequence of steps with documented reasoning
- Accounts for company position, strengths, and situational risks
- Granular enough to be executable (can derive product strategy from it)
- Changes every 1-3 years; reassessed annually
- NOT a goal -- it is the plan by which goals will be achieved

**The strategy vs. goals distinction:** Strategy = HOW you win. Goals = WHAT winning looks like. "Increase revenue by 20%" is a goal. "Increase revenue by 20% by expanding into two underserved markets" includes a strategy.

**Components:**
1. Target market -- who are we serving? What is the addressable market?
2. Competitive advantage -- why will we win against alternatives?
3. Growth model -- how does the business grow? (acquisition, retention, monetization)
4. Key bets -- what are the 2-3 strategic bets we are making this year?

**When company strategy should change:**
- Market conditions fundamentally shift (new technology, new competition)
- The company has made significant progress and needs to pursue the next phase
- A major strategic assumption has been invalidated

**Anti-patterns:**
- Strategy defined as a quantitative goal without a plan
- Strategy is a list of initiatives, not a logical argument
- No one in the company can articulate the strategy from memory
- Strategy changes every quarter in response to business conditions

### Layer 3: Product Strategy

**Definition:** The logical plan for how the product will drive its part of the company strategy.

Product strategy is the connective tissue. Neither the roadmap nor the goals can be well-defined without it.

**What a great product strategy articulates:**
- Which users we are building for (and which we are NOT)
- What problems we are solving (and what we are explicitly NOT solving)
- What our unique product advantage is (why us, not another product)
- How the product creates and captures value
- What the product will intentionally NOT do

**The Quibi vs. TikTok case:** Both had similar missions (bite-sized mobile entertainment). Radically different product strategies. Quibi: enable Hollywood creators to bring premium content to mobile. TikTok: enable users to create and share snackable content. Quibi's product strategy failed to capitalize on the social nature of mobile and the creator economy.

**The Stitch Fix case:** Product strategy (UI, personalization) was not enough. They needed to align with operational strategy (ML for inventory management, logistics). Product strategy must be defined in awareness of how other functional strategies complement it.

**Anti-patterns:**
- Product strategy = "build the best product"
- Product strategy defined without reference to company strategy
- Product strategy that is actually just a list of features
- Product strategy changed every quarter

### Layer 4: Product Roadmap

**Definition:** The sequence of features that implement the product strategy over time.

**The sequence principle:** Sequencing is the most undervalued skill in product. Well-sequenced features compound -- 1+1=3. Apple's Apple Pay sequence: Wallet, Touch ID, NFC-enabled hardware, Apple Pay. Netflix: Partnership-based catalog (2007), then original content (2013). The sequencing created compounding capabilities.

**The roadmap as commitment device:** A roadmap is a statement of what you are prioritizing AND what you are not. A roadmap without clear exclusions is not a roadmap -- it is a wish list.

**Roadmap vs. goals relationship:** Goals should flow FROM the roadmap. When companies set goals first and ask teams to build roadmaps to meet them, teams optimize for near-term metrics at the expense of focused product development.

**Roadmap anti-patterns:**
- Feature list with no sequencing rationale
- Changes every month to reflect sales/marketing priorities
- All feature work with no scaling or growth investment
- Exists but is not shared outside the product team
- Organized by ship date rather than strategic theme
- Every stakeholder got one item (political compromise, no coherent strategy)

### Layer 5: Product Goals

**Definition:** The outcomes that measure strategic progress.

**A good product goal:**
- Objectively measurable
- Defined BEFORE the roadmap is executed (not rationalized after)
- Connected to product strategy, not defined in isolation

**The goals are not strategy trap:** When you achieve a goal, you have not necessarily achieved progress on strategy. Kodak met their Ofoto goals while completely failing their digital photography strategy.

**Goal setting framework per initiative:**
1. Primary metric: the single metric that defines success
2. Guardrail metrics: metrics that should not regress
3. Learning metric: what we want to understand even if the primary metric does not move
4. Timeline: when we expect to see the metric move

**What to do when you miss a goal:**
1. Diagnose whether the roadmap was wrong (something needs to change upstream)
2. Or whether the goal was defined incorrectly (strategy is right, but we measured the wrong thing)
3. Do not assume missing a goal = strategy is wrong

### Stack Diagnostics

**Symptom: Team cannot agree on priorities**
Check product strategy: Is it clear which users and problems are in scope?

**Symptom: Teams across the company building incompatible things**
Check product strategy and company strategy alignment.

**Symptom: Product keeps getting more complex and harder to use**
Check roadmap: Is the team managing feature shadows? Is there capacity for simplification?

**Symptom: Goals are consistently hit but growth is slowing**
Check goals vs. strategy alignment: Are the goals measuring the right things?

**Symptom: Product has great features but users do not adopt them**
Check product strategy: Is there clarity about which users this is for?

## The Four Types of Product Work — Full Reference

### The Foundational Insight

After initial PMF, all product work falls into four categories. Each requires a different process, measure of success, and strategy. Applying a single development process and metric to all four types is the most common source of strategic failure in product organizations.

### Feature Work

**Definition:** Creating and capturing value by extending functionality into incremental and adjacent areas.

**The Feature Shadow Framework:**

Every feature has three categories of shadow costs that persist indefinitely after launch:

1. **Maintenance costs:** No feature has zero maintenance. Features must be continuously maintained, evolved, and supported.

2. **User costs:** Every feature adds cognitive complexity. This impacts discovery, onboarding, and the experience of all users -- even those who never use the feature. Pinterest removed Likes because new users were confused about whether to Pin or Like. Removing it did not impact retention.

3. **Killing costs:** "We can always kill it if it is not popular" is almost never true. Power users disproportionately use features on the periphery, making killing underused features even more costly than it appears.

**Feature Shadow downstream problems:**
- Distraction and thrashing: teams churn between strategic initiatives and follow-on work
- Product complexity enabling disruption (Yahoo to Google, MySpace to Facebook, SharePoint to Slack)
- Friction for new users: more features = steeper onboarding = lower activation

**Feature evaluation -- three dimensions:**
1. **Adoption:** Are users finding and using this feature?
2. **Retention:** Are users who use this feature more retained?
3. **Satisfaction:** Are users who use this feature satisfied with it?

Features can fail on any one dimension independently. High adoption + low retention means the feature gets tried but does not deliver value. Low adoption + high retention (for adopters) means a discoverability problem. High adoption + low satisfaction means the feature is used but frustrating.

**Feature strategy best practices:**
- Leave buffer for shadow work in every roadmap
- Evaluate existing features on all three dimensions before building new ones
- Sequence features to tell a coherent story (1+1=3)

### Growth Work

**Definition:** Creating and capturing value by accelerating adoption and usage within the existing market.

**The Growth Model Principle:** Before any growth work, answer: "How does our product grow?" This means understanding acquisition channels, retention behaviors, monetization model, and how these three interact as a system. Once you have the growth model, identify the largest constraint, diagnose the constraint type, and select the right method.

**Growth work process:**
1. Understand the growth model
2. Identify the largest constraint
3. Diagnose the constraint type (friction, awareness, value gap, technical limitation)
4. Select the right method (optimization, feature, new channel, new message)
5. Design a test with clear success metrics
6. Analyze and iterate

**When growth work becomes the wrong answer:**
- Optimizing acquisition when retention is 0% -- CAC without LTV
- Optimizing retention when activation is broken
- Running experiments on a product without good PMF

### Scaling Work

**Definition:** Focusing on bottlenecks to ensure the team can continue to move forward across all other work types.

**Three categories:**
1. **Technical scaling:** Infrastructure, engineering/design debt, capacity
2. **Process scaling:** Organizational coordination, alignment systems, how multiple teams work together
3. **User scaling:** Fraud detection, spam filtering, content moderation, security

**The Goldilocks timing framework:**

Too late indicators:
- High percentage of work is unplanned (fire-fighting mode)
- Opportunities being passed up because they are too hard to implement
- Roadmap items consistently delayed due to engineering debt
- Feature shipping velocity declining quarter-over-quarter

Too early indicators:
- Resources spent on scaling problems you have not yet hit
- Engineering time going to premature optimization
- Building for the 10M user product when you have 100K users

**Success metric:** ROI measured by impact across the entire portfolio of other work. Feature work ships faster, growth experiments can be run more quickly, PMF expansion is not blocked by tech debt.

### PMF Expansion Work

**Definition:** Increasing the ceiling on PMF in a non-incremental way by expanding into adjacent markets, adjacent products, or both.

**Why the PMF threshold keeps rising:**
1. Product grows, more users, faster expectations rise
2. Competitors respond and improve
3. New entrants build against your current level

**Three expansion modes:**

| Mode | Description | Risk Level |
|------|-------------|-----------|
| Same product, adjacent market | Same value prop, new segment/geography | Lower |
| Same market, adjacent product | Same users, new product area | Medium |
| Diversification | New product, new market | Highest |

**The "Why us?" test:** Before pursuing any expansion, articulate the unique asset giving you an advantage: brand and trust, distribution, proprietary data, technology, existing user relationships. Is this asset actually an advantage in the expansion, or just a comfort?

**The resource paradox:** More resources hurt PMF expansion. The discovery loop requires fast decision-making, clear communication, ability to change direction without consensus overhead, and low coordination cost. Start with 4-6 people. Add resources only as ambiguity reduces and hypotheses are validated.

**Using existing distribution (the double-edged sword):** Existing distribution is an asset -- but shoving a new product to your existing distribution before it is ready depletes goodwill, creates premature scale that hides poor PMF, and tempts teams to optimize for distribution rather than actual fit. Existing distribution cannot replace the PMF loop.

## The Portfolio Balance Framework

### The Five Common Portfolio Mistakes

**Mistake 1: All Feature, All Growth.** Teams do feature and growth work continuously, never investing in scaling or PMF expansion. Signs: shipping velocity declining, features taking longer, growth metrics plateauing.

**Mistake 2: Premature PMF Expansion.** Starting expansion before the core product has reached scale. Resources split, neither succeeds.

**Mistake 3: Scaling Too Late.** Technical and process debt accumulates until it blocks everything.

**Mistake 4: Growth Work on a Product That Needs Feature Work.** Optimizing conversion when the product does not deliver enough value to retain users.

**Mistake 5: Feature Work When You Need Growth Work.** Building new capabilities when existing ones are not fully adopted. The Pinterest lesson: changing "Pin It" to "Save" (growth work) got 15% activation improvement. Building Maps and Q&A products (feature work) had zero material impact.

### Portfolio Balance by Product Stage

**Early post-PMF (scaling fast):**
- High: Feature Work + Growth Work
- Moderate: Scaling Work (Goldilocks timing)
- Low: PMF Expansion (too early)

**Growth stage (primary market saturating):**
- Moderate: Feature Work (shadow costs rising)
- High: Growth Work (maximize current market capture)
- High: Scaling Work (debt catching up)
- Beginning: PMF Expansion (start while momentum exists)

**Mature product (primary market saturated):**
- Low-Moderate: Feature Work (maintenance and targeted)
- Moderate: Growth Work (optimizing within mature loops)
- Moderate: Scaling Work (ongoing)
- High: PMF Expansion (essential for continued growth)

**The most common failure:** Companies stay in "high Feature + high Growth" mode long after they should have shifted toward PMF Expansion. When growth slows, the instinct is to ship more features or optimize harder -- when the real answer is to expand the market.

### Structural Alignment -- Leading a Product Strategy

The hardest part of product strategy is not designing it -- it is getting buy-in and translating it into execution.

**The product leader's strategic job:** Senior PMs and product leaders must go from depth in one type of product work to managing breadth across all four types. This requires diagnosing which type a problem belongs to, understanding what process and metric each type requires, building a portfolio view, and communicating clearly enough for teams to make autonomous decisions.

**Communicating and getting buy-in:**
1. Start at the company mission level -- ensure everyone agrees on the foundation
2. Trace the logical chain from mission through strategy to roadmap to goals
3. Use case studies to illustrate what happens when layers diverge
4. Present portfolio balance explicitly -- show that all four types of work are represented

**The T-shape principle:** The goal is not expertise in all four types. It is diagnostic vocabulary -- identify what's wrong, communicate it clearly, and course-correct effectively.

## Power User Field Guide by Product Type

### Consumer Social Power Users

**Power Lurkers:** Passive content consumers (Instagram scrollers, Twitter readers). Value: ad impressions, potential to convert. Risk: over-investing to convert them into creators.

**Power Engagers:** Active users who both create AND consume heavily. Value: drive growth loops, reinforce platform culture. Risk: easy to overlook -- small in number, less visible than influencers.

**Power Influencers:** Celebrities, professional creators, large audiences. Value: content flywheel, feature ambassadors. Risk: very vocal complainers; alienating them can pull audiences to alternatives.

**Power Spammers:** Users whose content degrades the ecosystem. Metrics resemble Power Influencers but content is lower quality. Risk: degrade ecosystem, drive away quality users.

### Marketplace Power Users

**Demand side:**
- Power Whales: big, consistent high-margin spenders. Risk: lessons do not generalize.
- Power Minnows: consistent small spenders, high frequency. Risk: may be unprofitable long-term.

**Supply side:**
- Power Suppliers: professionalized multi-unit suppliers (multi-property Airbnb hosts). Risk: can crowd out smaller but higher-quality supply.
- Power Gems: high-quality unique suppliers. Risk: low ceiling, hard to scale.

### Direct-to-Consumer Power Users

**Power Loyalists:** Near-ceiling subscribers/customers spanning the full product portfolio. Value: highest LTV. Risk: needs do not generalize to broader market.

**Power Evangelists:** Outlier referral drivers. Value: drive viral loops, potential to convert to Loyalist. Risk: diminishing returns as product matures.

**Power Savers:** Discount-driven buyers (only buy on sale). Value: top-line volume. Risk: low margin, may indicate lack of PMF for full-price product.

### B2B Power Users

**Organizational level:**
- Power Monetizers: large, low-maintenance, required-usage accounts. Risk: stable revenue can hide individual unhappiness.
- Power Divas: large revenue potential but outlier support demands. Risk: product bloat from niche requirements.
- Power Feature Users: outlier feature breadth, early adopters. Risk: overlap with Divas; may request non-generalizable features.

**Individual level:**
- Power Champions: influential individuals who drive internal adoption. Risk: alienating them causes advocacy for replacement.
- Power Builders: admin users who configure the product. Their quality determines experience for the entire organization.
- Power Engagers: outlier daily users across diverse actions. High engagement does not equal satisfaction.
- Power Specialists: outlier users of less popular features. Surface new use cases but can create bloat if prioritized.
- Power Decision Makers: low usage, high purchasing influence. Preferences may conflict with actual user needs.
