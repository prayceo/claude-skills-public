# Coaching, Team Building, and Leading Through Influence — Deep Reference

*Sources: Ravi Mehta, "What's Your Shape?" (ravi-mehta.com); Product Leadership course; Product Leadership Accelerator framework; "The Secrets to Maximizing the Performance of Your Product Managers" (Blog)*

## The Product Leader's Dual Mandate

> "Product Managers build products. Product Leaders build products, people, and companies."

The transition from PM to Product Leader requires a fundamental shift in how you measure success. A PM's output is a great product. A Product Leader's output is a great team that builds great products. The corollary: if you remove the Product Leader and the team falls apart, the leader was managing, not leading. If the team thrives without the leader, the leader has built something durable.

## The Exponential Feedback Model

Traditional PM development relies on annual reviews -- infrequent, backward-looking, and largely useless for real-time growth. Mehta advocates for exponential feedback -- a multi-frequency system that compounds development over time.

### Level 1: Real-Time Feedback (Daily)

Delivered immediately after a specific moment -- a meeting, a decision, a deliverable.

**Format:**
```
"I noticed [specific behavior]. The impact was [what happened].
Next time, consider [specific alternative]."
```

**Examples:**
- "I noticed you presented the data first and the recommendation last. The VP lost attention by slide 3. Next time, lead with the recommendation and use data as backup."
- "I noticed you asked the engineer about timeline without sharing the context of why it mattered. They committed to a date but did not understand the stakes. Next time, share the business context before asking for commitments."

**Rules:**
- Deliver within 2 hours of the event
- Be specific -- name the behavior, not the person
- Include both the impact and an alternative
- Keep it to 30 seconds
- Praise in public, coach in private

### Level 2: Weekly Feedback (1:1s)

The weekly 1:1 is the primary coaching vehicle.

**1:1 Agenda (30 minutes):**

```
PART 1: THEIR AGENDA (10 min)
  What is on your mind?
  Where do you need my help?
  What blockers can I remove?

PART 2: COACHING MOMENT (10 min)
  Pick one moment from the past week
  "Walk me through what happened when you [specific situation]"
  "What went well?"
  "What would you do differently?"
  Connect to a specific competency:
    "This relates to your [Stakeholder Management] growth area.
     Here is what I would suggest for next time..."

PART 3: LOOKING AHEAD (10 min)
  What is coming next week that we should prepare for?
  Any high-stakes meetings or decisions?
  Progress on growth goals?
```

**1:1 Anti-Patterns:**
- Status updates (use async for this)
- Only tactical, no coaching
- Leader talks more than PM
- No connection to growth goals
- Canceled frequently

### Level 3: Monthly Feedback (Career Development)

Monthly career conversations go deeper than weekly 1:1s. They connect daily work to long-term professional growth.

**Monthly Session Agenda (45 minutes):**

```
1. GROWTH GOAL REVIEW (15 min)
   - What progress have you made on your growth goals this month?
   - What specific examples demonstrate growth?
   - What obstacles are you encountering?

2. COMPETENCY SPOTLIGHT (15 min)
   - Pick one competency to deep-dive
   - Review specific instances from the month
   - Compare to the Over-Performing description
   - Set specific practice activities for next month

3. CAREER PATH (15 min)
   - Where do you want to be in 12 months? 3 years?
   - What skills or experiences do you need to get there?
   - What stretch opportunities should we pursue?
   - Is there a mentorship or sponsorship opportunity?
```

### Level 4: Quarterly Feedback (Formal Assessment)

The quarterly review uses the full 12-competency model to evaluate progress and recalibrate growth plans.

**Quarterly Process:**

```
PREPARATION (2-3 hours):
1. Rate the PM on all 12 competencies with specific evidence
2. Identify the PM's archetype/shape
3. Compare to last quarter's ratings -- where did they grow?
4. Draft growth goals for next quarter

CALIBRATION (30 min with peer leaders):
1. Share assessments across the leadership team
2. Normalize ratings (is one leader's On Track another's Needs Focus?)
3. Identify team-level patterns

DELIVERY (60 min with the PM):
1. Share the competency assessment
2. Celebrate growth areas
3. Discuss areas needing focus with empathy and specificity
4. Co-create the next quarter's growth plan
5. Align on support needed from the leader
```

## Developing Specific Competencies

### Business Outcome Ownership (Most Critical)

**The Problem:** Many PMs default to feature ownership rather than metric ownership. They celebrate shipping features rather than measuring impact.

**Development Activities:**
1. **Metric ownership assignment:** "Your job is to improve engagement session completion rate, not to ship engagement session features."
2. **Weekly metric review:** Every 1:1 includes "What happened to your metric this week? Why?"
3. **Impact prediction:** Before any feature ships, the PM must predict expected metric impact. After ship, compare prediction to reality.
4. **Post-mortem discipline:** For every shipped feature, conduct a 30-day post-mortem: "Did this move the metric? By how much? Why or why not?"

### Voice of the Customer (Most Impactful for Consumer Products)

**The Problem:** PMs in consumer products may project their own beliefs onto users or rely on assumptions about what religious people want.

**Development Activities:**
1. **Monthly conversation minimum:** Require 4+ user conversations per month. Track and discuss in 1:1s.
2. **Research share ritual:** PM presents one user insight per week to the team. Must include a verbatim quote.
3. **Segment rotation:** Each month, the PM talks to a different user segment (new users, churned users, power users, free users, paid users).
4. **Assumption audit:** Before any feature decision, list assumptions about user behavior. Star the ones that have evidence vs. the ones that are untested.

### Managing Up (Most Neglected)

**The Problem:** PMs are often coached on building products and leading teams but not on managing their relationship with their own leadership.

**Development Activities:**
1. **Pre-brief preparation:** Before every executive meeting, prepare together. "What is the one thing you want leadership to walk away knowing?"
2. **Headline practice:** Write the email subject line version of every recommendation. If you cannot summarize in one sentence, you are not clear enough.
3. **Executive simulation:** Role-play leadership meetings. The leader plays the executive; the PM practices framing, defending, and adapting.
4. **Post-meeting debrief:** After every executive interaction, discuss: "What worked? What question caught you off guard? How would you handle it next time?"

### Product Vision & Roadmapping

**Development Activities:**
1. Write the press release for the next feature before building it
2. Practice articulating the "why" behind every roadmap item
3. Challenge: "If we could only ship one thing next quarter, what and why?"

## Building Complementary Teams

### The Team Composition Process

**Step 1: Define Product Needs by Context**

| Product Context | Primary Archetype | Secondary | Why |
|----------------|-------------------|-----------|-----|
| 0-to-1 (new product) | Strategic Visionary | Customer Champion | Need vision and user understanding to find PMF |
| Growth phase (scaling) | Product Executor | Customer Champion | Need reliable delivery and user feedback loops |
| Mature product (optimizing) | Customer Champion | Product Executor | Need deep user insight for incremental gains |
| Turnaround (fixing) | Strategic Visionary | People Leader | Need new direction and team alignment |
| Platform product | Product Executor | People Leader | Need technical reliability and cross-team coordination |
| New market entry | Customer Champion | Strategic Visionary | Need deep understanding of new users and market |

**Step 2: Map Current Team Strengths**

Create a competency coverage map showing which competencies are covered by at least one strong team member:

```
TEAM COVERAGE MAP

Competency                 Coverage    Gap Risk
-------------------------------------------------
Feature Specification      ######      Low
Product Delivery           ########    Low
Quality Assurance          ####        Medium
Fluency with Data          ######      Low
Voice of the Customer      ##          HIGH
User Experience Design     ####        Medium
Business Outcome Ownership ##          HIGH
Vision & Roadmapping       ########    Low
Strategic Impact           ####        Medium
Stakeholder Management     ######      Low
Team Leadership            ####        Medium
Managing Up                ##          HIGH

CRITICAL GAPS: Voice of Customer, Business Outcome Ownership, Managing Up
HIRING PRIORITY: Customer Champion with strong business orientation
```

**Step 3: Hire for Complementarity**

The instinct is to hire people like yourself. Resist it. If the team is execution-heavy, hire a Strategic Visionary. If the team is strategy-rich but delivery-poor, hire a Product Executor. Complementarity beats similarity.

When evaluating PM candidates, score them against your team's gaps -- not just against the role requirements in isolation.

**Step 4: Design Team Rituals for Cross-Pollination**

Even a well-composed team needs rituals that ensure strengths are shared:

| Ritual | Purpose | Cadence | Duration |
|--------|---------|---------|----------|
| Research share | Customer Champions share insights with team | Weekly | 15 min |
| Strategy review | Strategic Visionaries present and invite critique | Monthly | 60 min |
| Ship review | Product Executors walk through what shipped and learned | Bi-weekly | 30 min |
| Stakeholder roundtable | People Leaders coach team on upcoming dynamics | Bi-weekly | 30 min |
| Competency pairing | Cross-archetype pairs for specific projects | Per project | Ongoing |

## Product Leader Time Allocation

### By Team State

**Team is new or struggling (low trust, unclear roles):**
- 60% on TEAM DEVELOPMENT: weekly 1:1s, team rituals, monthly retros
- 30% on STRATEGY: establish the strategy stack
- 10% on EXECUTION SUPPORT: unblock critical items

**Team is functional but plateauing (good execution, limited growth):**
- 40% on STRATEGY: refresh the strategy stack, identify next growth levers
- 40% on INDIVIDUAL DEVELOPMENT: competency assessments, stretch assignments, career paths
- 20% on EXECUTION SUPPORT

**Team is high-performing (strong execution, clear strategy):**
- 50% on STRATEGIC IMPACT: company-level strategy, new market/product opportunities, org design
- 30% on INDIVIDUAL DEVELOPMENT: coaching high-performers for next role, maintaining culture
- 20% on ORGANIZATIONAL INFLUENCE: cross-functional partnerships, executive alignment

## Leading Through Influence

Product leaders rarely have direct authority over those they need to influence.

### The Trust Equation

```
TRUST = (Credibility + Reliability + Empathy) / Self-Interest

Credibility: Do stakeholders believe you know what you are talking about?
  Build by: sharing data, demonstrating domain expertise, being right often

Reliability: Do stakeholders believe you will follow through?
  Build by: delivering on commitments, communicating early when things change

Empathy: Do stakeholders feel you understand their perspective?
  Build by: listening first, acknowledging constraints, incorporating feedback

Self-Interest: Do stakeholders think you are optimizing for yourself or the team?
  Reduce by: giving credit, sharing wins, supporting others' priorities
```

### Influence by Stakeholder Type

| Stakeholder | What They Care About | How to Influence |
|-------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| Engineering | Technical quality, feasibility, team autonomy | Involve early, respect constraints, share context not just requirements |
| Design | User experience, craft, coherent vision | Co-create, bring user evidence, protect design time |
| Marketing | Story, positioning, launch impact | Share the narrative early, involve in product decisions |
| Sales | Customer requests, competitive positioning | Share roadmap context, explain prioritization rationale |
| Leadership | Business outcomes, risk, strategic alignment | Lead with impact, frame in business terms, be direct |
| Finance | Unit economics, ROI, cost | Quantify impact, present trade-offs clearly |

### The Disagree and Commit Framework

When alignment fails:

```
1. STATE YOUR POSITION CLEARLY
   "I believe we should invest in community features because..."

2. LISTEN TO THE OPPOSING VIEW
   "I understand the concern about resource allocation..."

3. IF OVERRULED, COMMIT FULLY
   "I disagree with this direction, but I commit to executing it well.
    Here is how I will make this succeed."

4. SET A REVIEW POINT
   "Can we agree to review the data in 6 weeks and revisit if needed?"

5. EXECUTE WITH FULL EFFORT
   Do not sabotage or half-commit. If you committed, deliver.
```

## Coaching Decision Tree

### Diagnosing an Underperforming PM

```
START: Identify the competency gap

EXECUTION gap (missing deadlines, unclear specs, quality issues)
|
+-- Is it a skill gap or a will gap?
|   +-- SKILL: Pair with a strong executor; provide templates; review PRDs together
|   +-- WILL: Direct conversation about expectations; set 30-day improvement plan
|
+-- Check: Are there organizational blockers? (unclear priorities, too many projects)
    +-- YES: Fix the system first, then reassess the PM
    +-- NO: The PM needs development or a different role

CUSTOMER INSIGHT gap (shipping without evidence, ignoring data)
|
+-- Require minimum user conversations per sprint
+-- Co-attend user interviews; debrief together
+-- Assign a "metric of the week" to investigate and present
+-- Timeline: Expect improvement within 6-8 weeks

STRATEGY gap (reactive roadmap, no clear vision, disconnected from outcomes)
|
+-- Walk through the strategy stack together
+-- Ask them to write a one-page product strategy
+-- Review and iterate weekly for 4 weeks
+-- Assign a strategic initiative with coaching support

INFLUENCE gap (poor stakeholder relationships, team friction, cannot get buy-in)
|
+-- Attend their meetings; provide real-time feedback after
+-- Role-play difficult conversations
+-- Assign a cross-functional project with clear relationship goals
+-- Timeline: Influence is the slowest to develop; expect 3-6 months
```

### When Development Is Not Working

After a reasonable investment period (typically 2-3 months for execution and insight gaps, 3-6 months for strategy and influence gaps):

1. **Reassess the diagnosis:** Is the gap correctly identified? Sometimes a strategy gap masks an insight gap, or an influence gap masks a confidence gap.
2. **Check for system issues:** Is the role set up for success? Are priorities clear? Does the PM have the authority to act on their growth areas?
3. **Consider role fit:** A PM struggling as a Strategic Visionary may thrive as a Product Executor. The archetype framework suggests the PM may be in the wrong seat, not the wrong profession.
4. **Have the direct conversation:** Share the data, the investment made, and the gap remaining. Co-create either a final improvement plan with specific milestones or an honest discussion about alternative roles.
