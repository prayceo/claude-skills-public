# Naval's Decision-Making, Learning, and Mental Models

## Judgment

"My definition of wisdom is knowing the long-term consequences of your actions. Wisdom applied to external problems is judgment."

"In an age of leverage, one correct decision can win everything."

"The direction you're heading in matters more than how fast you move."

"'Clear thinker' is a better compliment than 'smart.'"

Judgment is the most important quality in an age of leverage. When a single decision can be amplified a million times through code, capital, or media, the quality of your thinking matters more than the quantity of your effort.

## Mental Models Collection

Naval advocates collecting mental models from multiple disciplines and applying them fluidly. The core models he references most:

- **Evolution** — the most powerful mental model. Explains biology, business, culture, and ideas. Everything that persists has survived selection.
- **Inversion (Munger)** — instead of asking how to succeed, ask how to fail and avoid that. Solve problems backwards.
- **Complexity Theory** — simple rules produce complex emergent behavior. You cannot predict complex systems, but you can understand their properties.
- **Microeconomics / Game Theory** — supply, demand, incentives, Nash equilibria. The grammar of business and human interaction.
- **Principal-Agent Problem** — "If you want it done, Go. If not, Send." The agent never cares as much as the principal. "Almost all human behavior can be explained by incentives."
- **Compound Interest** — applies to money, relationships, knowledge, and reputation. Small consistent advantages compound into enormous gaps.
- **Black Swans** — rare, unpredictable events with massive impact. Most of life's outcomes are determined by a few Black Swan moments.
- **Calculus Principles** — thinking in terms of rates of change, not static snapshots. The derivative matters more than the value.
- **Falsifiability** — if a theory cannot be proven wrong, it is not a useful theory. Applies to business hypotheses as much as science.

### Principal-Agent Deep Dive

"If you want it done, Go. If not, Send."

"Almost all human behavior can be explained by incentives."

This is one of Naval's most-used models. Whenever you delegate, you lose fidelity. The agent has different incentives than the principal. The solution is either to align incentives perfectly (equity, skin in the game) or to do the most important things yourself.

### Kelly Criterion and Ergodicity

Never bet everything. The Kelly Criterion tells you the optimal bet size given your edge and bankroll. But the deeper insight is about ergodicity: the time average and the ensemble average are not the same.

Russian roulette example: the expected value of playing Russian roulette for $1M is positive in the ensemble average (5/6 chance of $1M). But the time average — what happens to you personally across repeated plays — converges to death. Never play games where the downside is ruin, regardless of expected value.

### Schelling Point

A Schelling Point is the solution people converge on without communicating. Rational coordination without communication. In business, this explains why standards emerge, why people cluster in the same cities, and why certain deal structures become default.

## Decision Frameworks

### If You Cannot Decide, the Answer is No

"If you can't decide, the answer is no."

Modern society offers abundant options. The cost of saying yes to something mediocre is missing something great. Only say yes when you are certain. Anything less than "hell yes" is a no.

### Run Uphill

"Run uphill" — when evenly split between two options, take the path that is more painful in the short term. The short-term pain option almost always leads to long-term gain. Easy choices, hard life. Hard choices, easy life.

### Discard Memory and Identity

"Almost all biases are time-saving heuristics. For important decisions, discard memory and identity, and focus on the problem."

Your past experiences and your sense of who you are both distort your judgment. For the decisions that truly matter, try to evaluate the situation as if you had no history and no identity. What would a rational agent with your information choose?

### Classical Virtues as Heuristics

"The classical virtues are all decision-making heuristics to optimize for long term over short term."

Honesty, courage, temperance, justice — these are not abstract ideals. They are practical algorithms for making decisions that your future self will thank you for. Every virtue is a shortcut that says "choose the long-term benefit over the short-term temptation."

## Clear Thinking

Understand the basics at a fundamental level. Do not memorize advanced concepts you cannot rederive from first principles. If you truly understand the foundations, you can reconstruct anything built on top of them.

Feynman standard: if you cannot explain something to a child, you do not truly understand it. Complexity is often a sign of incomplete understanding, not deep knowledge.

"What we wish to be true clouds our perception of what is true."

"A contrarian isn't one who always objects — that's a conformist of a different sort. A contrarian reasons independently from the ground up and resists pressure to conform."

True contrarian thinking is not reflexive opposition. It is independent reasoning that sometimes agrees with the consensus and sometimes does not. The defining quality is that the reasoning comes first, and the position follows.

---

## Shedding Identity

"Any belief you took in a package (Democrat, Catholic, American) is suspect."

Package-deal beliefs are inherited, not reasoned. If you identify as part of a group, you have imported their entire belief system without evaluating each piece independently. This is intellectual laziness disguised as belonging.

"To be honest, speak without identity."

When you speak from identity ("as a CEO," "as a liberal," "as an engineer"), you are not reasoning — you are performing a role. Strip away the identity and see what remains.

"Tension is who you think you should be. Relaxation is who you are."

The gap between your self-image and your actual nature is the source of most psychological suffering. Close the gap by dropping the self-image, not by forcing yourself to match it.

---

## Radical Honesty

"The moment you tell somebody something dishonest, you've lied to yourself."

Every external lie requires an internal lie first. You must convince yourself that deception is acceptable. Over time, this erodes your ability to see reality clearly. Honesty is not just a moral virtue — it is an epistemic one.

Buffett's rule: "Praise specifically, criticize generally."

When praising, be precise about what was good. When criticizing, speak about patterns and principles, not individuals. This preserves relationships while still communicating truth.

"Charisma is the ability to project confidence and love at the same time."

Charisma is not manipulation. It is the rare combination of believing in yourself (confidence) and genuinely caring about others (love). People follow those who make them feel both safe and inspired.

## The Reading Operating System

"Read what you love until you love to read."

The entry point does not matter. Comics, science fiction, business books, history — whatever gets you reading. The habit of reading is more important than what you read at first.

**No obligation to finish any book.** Read 10-20 books simultaneously. Pick up whatever calls to you. Put down whatever bores you. Life is too short for books that do not grip you.

"Reading a book isn't a race — the better the book, the more slowly it should be absorbed."

"The number of books completed is a vanity metric."

"If they wrote it to make money, don't read it."

Books written to make money are optimized for sales, not truth. Look for authors who wrote because they had to, not because they wanted a bestseller.

**Read originals.** Read Darwin, not Dawkins. Read Adam Smith, not modern interpretations. The original thinkers are clearer and more nuanced than the people who summarize them.

"When solving problems: the older the problem, the older the solution."

Ancient problems have been solved. If your problem is about human nature, motivation, or meaning, the answers are in texts thousands of years old.

"Study logic and math, because once you've mastered them, you won't fear any book."

Logic and math are the foundation of all rigorous thinking. Once you can follow a proof, no subject is intimidating.

## What to Study

"Study microeconomics, game theory, psychology, persuasion, ethics, mathematics, and computers."

"There is no skill called 'business.' Avoid business magazines and business classes."

Business is an applied combination of other skills. Study the components directly rather than the packaged version.

"Reading science, math, and philosophy one hour per day will likely put you at the upper echelon of human success within seven years."

**Five most important skills:** Reading, writing, arithmetic, persuasion, and computer programming. These are the leverage skills that amplify everything else.

---

## The Perpetual Learner

"The most important skill for getting rich is becoming a perpetual learner."

In a rapidly changing world, the ability to learn new things quickly is more valuable than any specific knowledge. Your current expertise has a half-life.

New model for careers: come up to speed in a new field in 9-12 months, then your knowledge becomes obsolete in roughly 4 years. The cycle of learning never ends.

"The best jobs are neither decreed nor degreed."

The most valuable roles in the economy are not created by government decree or credentialed by university degrees. They are invented by people who see an opportunity and build the skills to capture it.

---

## The Three Big Decisions

"Where you live, who you're with, and what you do."

These three decisions determine more of your life outcomes than almost everything else combined. If you will be in a place, relationship, or career for 10 years, it is worth spending 1-2 years deciding carefully.
