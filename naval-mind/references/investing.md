# Naval's Investing Mental Models

## Power Law Distribution

The power law governs venture investing. The #1 winner returns as much as #2 through the rest combined. This is why portfolio construction matters more than individual picks.

"Smart money is just dumb money that's been through a crash."

## Angel Investing as Asymmetric Bet

Angel investing is the lottery with some of the numbers revealed in advance. The key asymmetry: you can only lose 1x your investment, but you can win 1000x. This asymmetry means the rational strategy is to take many informed bets.

"You get paid for being right first, and to be first, you can't wait for consensus."

## Diversification

Diversification hedges against lack of knowledge. If you truly knew which company would win, you would concentrate. Diversification is an honest admission that you do not know.

## Judgment Over Due Diligence

Judgment is the preparation done BEFORE a deal arrives. It is not due diligence. Judgment is your accumulated mental models, pattern recognition, and taste built over years. It takes 5-15 years to know if you have good judgment in investing. You cannot shortcut this.

"Investing favors the dispassionate. Markets efficiently separate emotional investors from their money."

## The Best Deals Look Weird

The best investments have characteristics that make most people uncomfortable. Naval uses the one fatal flaw framework: give every deal one reason to pass. If there is more than one fatal flaw, that is a real problem. But every great deal has at least one thing that looks wrong.

"Best investing attitude is contrarian, patient, informed optimism."

## Immunity to FOMO

The best investors are immune to FOMO (fear of missing out). Naval uses a 24-hour cooling-off period before committing to any deal. If the excitement fades in 24 hours, it was not conviction — it was emotion.

## Buffett's Three-Part Test

Warren Buffett's hiring framework applies to founders: look for high energy, high intelligence, and high integrity. If you are missing integrity, the first two will destroy you.

## Coachability is Overrated

Coachability is overrated as a signal. Great founders follow very little advice. They listen to many people but ultimately make their own decisions. The best founders are polite but stubborn.

## Reputation and Returns

Your reputation as an investor is built by the companies that do poorly — how you treat founders when things go wrong. But your returns come from the winners. These are different games.

## First-Time vs. Repeat Founders

First-time founders tend to take market risk — they chase bigger, unproven markets, which leads to bigger outcomes. Repeat founders tend to take execution risk — they pick known markets and execute better. Both are valid, but the biggest outcomes come from market risk.

## References Barely Work

References barely work for evaluating creative founders. Truly original founders are often seen as difficult, weird, or wrong by the people around them. Naval passed on Twilio and Ethereum because of references. The reference check is a conformity filter that screens out the most original thinkers.

## VC is a Bundle

VC is a bundle of three things: Advice, Control, and Money. Smart founders unbundle these — they get advice from advisors, minimize control given away, and take money from the most efficient source.

## Valuation vs. Control

Valuation is temporary; control is forever. A high valuation means nothing if you gave away board seats and veto rights. Founders should optimize for control over valuation in every negotiation.

## Funding Market Development

Funding markets develop in reverse order: public markets came first, then mezzanine financing, then venture capital, then angel investing, then seed. Each new layer makes the previous layer more efficient.

## Keynesian Beauty Contest

Private investors are rational herd animals. You are not picking the best company — you are picking the company that the next round of investors will think is the best company. This is the Keynesian Beauty Contest applied to venture capital.

## Blockchains and Coordination

Blockchains solve the problem of coordination without a state. They address the Byzantine Generals' problem — how to reach consensus among parties who cannot fully trust each other, without a central authority.

## Cryptography as Asymmetric Defense

Cryptography is the first asymmetric advantage for the defender since the invention of the castle wall. For the first time in the digital age, defense is cheaper than attack. This has profound implications for individual sovereignty.
