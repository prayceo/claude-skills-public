## FOCUS & PRIORITIZATION

### Protect Your MITs (Most Important Things)

> "Every 'quick favor' you say yes to is a 'no' to something we already committed to."

Side quests are the silent killer of high-performing organizations. They look small — "just a quick call," "can you review this?" — but they compound into massive priority drift.

**The Rule:** If a company only accomplishes 2–5 things that matter, it can miss many others and still hit a bigger goal. What appears to be speed from the outside is not "lots of activity." It is making the right decisions on few things.

**Application:**
- Before saying yes to anything, ask: "Is this one of our MITs?"
- If no, it's a side quest. Decline or defer it.
- High-performing organizations don't win by doing more things. They win by doing fewer things exceptionally well, in the right order.

### Elimination Creates Clarity

> "Most hard goals aren't actually hard. They're crowded."

The reason most goals take a year isn't because they're complex — it's because they're surrounded by competing priorities that dilute focus.

> "Once you eliminate competing priorities, the same outcome often collapses from a year into a month of concentrated effort."

**The Formula:** Clarity = Speed = Momentum = Success

**Application:**
- Ask: "If I could only focus on ONE thing for the next 30–60 days, what would it be?"
- Ask: "What am I doing out of habit or obligation that, if eliminated, would dramatically increase my speed?"
- Don't stretch into a year-long effort what could be achieved with 30–60 days of concentrated focus.

## TIME MANAGEMENT

### Monday Hour One

> "Your calendar should look like Tetris. Every slot filled. Every hour accounted for."

Spend 1 hour planning on Monday morning to save 10–12 hours executing all week.

**The System:**
1. Block your ENTIRE week by Monday morning. Every hour accounted for.
2. Block breaks FIRST — lunch, walks, personal time. If you don't schedule them, they won't happen.
3. No vague "project work" — name the specific project for each time block.
4. Email and Slack get designated windows, not all-day access.
5. Add overflow time for ad-hoc requests and things taking longer than expected.
6. Color-code your calendar for instant visual clarity on where your time goes.

> "Constraints create freedom. When you give yourself too much time, work expands to fill it."

## MAKERS VS. MANAGERS

> "Task-switching can cost up to 40% of someone's productive time."

This is one of the most underappreciated operational frameworks. Most companies schedule everyone the same way, which destroys maker productivity.

### The Two Modes

**Makers** (creators, designers, developers, writers) need half-day or full-day uninterrupted blocks. Even one meeting in the middle of a maker's day kills their deep work. A 30-minute meeting doesn't cost them 30 minutes — it fragments their entire block.

**Managers** work in 30–60 minute blocks naturally. Meetings and decisions ARE their workflow. Context-switching is built into the job.

### The 10x Cost Rule

> "When managers schedule meetings with makers, it costs the maker 10x what it costs the manager."

A manager puts a 30-min meeting on a maker's calendar and thinks "no big deal." But the maker loses the 30 minutes PLUS the setup/teardown time PLUS the momentum they had on their deep work. Net cost: 2–3 hours of productive output for a 30-minute meeting.

### Solutions

- **Hybrids** should theme their days: maker mornings, manager afternoons. Or block 1 full no-meeting day per week.
- Before scheduling a meeting with a maker, ask: "Is this worth 10x the time it takes?"
- Batch maker meetings into one day/block so the other days stay clear.
- Task-switching costs up to 40% of productive time — that's real revenue lost.

## LEADERSHIP & DELEGATION

### Make Yourself Less Necessary

> "If a leader being unavailable causes everything to break, that isn't leadership; that's dependency."

This is the central thesis of Leila's leadership philosophy. The goal of leadership is not to prove how essential you are — it's to build people and systems that operate without you.

> "Being constantly needed is not the goal. Building people and systems that can operate without you is."

### The Helicopter Parent Trap

> "We don't create leaders by doing the work for them — we disempower teams when we 'helicopter parent' their tasks."

When you jump in to fix things, you get a short-term win (the task gets done right) but a long-term loss (your team never learns, never owns, never grows). The leader who is always saving the day is actually holding the organization back.

### The Delegation Script

When someone brings you a question, instead of answering it, ask:

> "What would you do if I weren't here?"

This does three things:
1. Forces them to think before defaulting to you
2. Reveals whether it's a communication, training, or motivation issue (Managerial Triangle)
3. Builds their decision-making muscle over time

### Practical Steps

- Identify one "default task" you do by habit and intentionally hand it off this week
- Notice where being indispensable FEELS good — those moments are traps
- Don't be the first answer. Let others own, decide, and lead.
- Not every problem is yours to solve. Leadership requires restraint, not just effort.

> "You cannot help someone who won't participate in helping themselves."

## THE FOUR TYPES OF CAPACITY

> "Capacity is not the output of success. It's the input."

Most leaders think capacity is something you build AFTER you grow. Leila argues it's the prerequisite — you must have capacity in place BEFORE growth arrives, or the growth will crush you.

> "If we wait until we 'need' capacity, we're already late."

### The Four Types

1. **Financial Capacity** — Cash reserves, credit lines, runway. Can you afford to invest ahead of revenue?
2. **Human Capacity** — Bench strength, training pipeline, succession planning. Can your team handle 2x load?
3. **Operational Capacity** — Systems, processes, infrastructure. Can your ops scale without breaking?
4. **Cognitive Capacity** — Leadership bandwidth, decision-making energy, strategic thinking time. Is the CEO drowning in tactics?

### Application

Score each of the four on a 1–10 scale. Your lowest score is your constraint. You need all four to scale — a weakness in any one becomes the ceiling.

## MINDSET & GROWTH REFRAMES

### Every Excuse Is a Skill Deficit

> "Money doesn't discriminate who puts in the work. Neither does talent."

When someone says "I can't because..." — reframe it. Every "I can't" is actually "I don't have the skill YET." This reframe turns external excuses into internal skill gaps that can be trained.

### Don't Wait for Permission

> "Don't confuse time with difficulty. Don't let tradition dictate when you improve."

Don't wait for a calendar date, a new quarter, or a ritual to change the plan. If it's broken, fix it now. If it needs to change, change it now.

## ACQUISITION.COM STRATEGIC ANCHORS

These are the three strategic anchors that guide all decisions at Acquisition.com. They emerged from applying inversion thinking (via Munger) to the question: "What 3 decisions would absolutely kill our business?"

### The Three Business-Killers (Inverted)

1. **Stay hidden → Give massive free value publicly.** The market rewards generosity. Free content, free frameworks, free tools — the more you give, the more trust and goodwill compounds.

2. **Work with everyone → Have a clear avatar and value proposition.** Selective partnerships only. Work with founders who have competitive greatness, unimpeachable character, and sincere candor. Saying no to bad fits is as important as saying yes to good ones.

3. **Use legacy tech → Build on modern, scalable infrastructure.** Standardize technology across the portfolio. Enduring infrastructure is a competitive moat.

## JOB ADS AS AD COPY

> "The job description is for employees what ad copy is for customers." — Leila

Most job postings are boring, soulless, ChatGPT-generated templates. They read like legal documents, not opportunities. This is a massive missed opportunity.

**The fix:** Rewrite job ads with personality, soul, and a compelling vision. One Acquisition.com portfolio founder went from 12 to 35 inbound applications per day with zero additional ad spend — just better job copy.

**The parallel:** If you wouldn't run an ad that says "We sell stuff. Buy it." — why would you post a job that says "We have an opening. Apply."?

## SALES AVAILABILITY INSIGHT

> "The #1 correlation with increased sales wasn't close rates — it was increased availability."

More open calendar slots = more booked calls = fewer cancellations = more sales. The business with the most availability wins. It's not because they're better or smarter — they just made it easier to become the obvious choice.

**Tactical application:** Staff shifts from 6am EST to 9pm PST, 7 days/week. Maximize the window where someone can talk to your team.

## THE SEASON OF SUCKING

> "You become successful the moment you decide you are. The moment you go all in on you, you already won. The game's in motion. You just gotta give time, time."

Alex made hundreds of videos sleeping at his gym, took shots before filming because he was so nervous. 12+ years later, nobody remembers the bad ones. The process made him who he is.

> "The output of the video wasn't the likes and comments I got. It was who I became."

The season of sucking is mandatory. Consistency before exceptionalism. You can't skip the reps. Learning requires action, not consumption — if you just read/watch and don't implement, you learn nothing.

> "If you just consume it, you learn nothing. You only learn if you take action."
