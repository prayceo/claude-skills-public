# Extended Sales Canon — Consultative, SaaS, LinkedIn, Rhetoric

Companion to `sales.md` and `sales-workshop.md`. Where Hormozi is volume / direct-response / one-call-close, this reference covers the **consultative, multi-stakeholder, high-ACV motion** — the motion the company runs for B2B partnerships with churches, enterprise deals with media companies, and content-licensing sales. It layers four bodies of work on top of the Hormozi system:

1. **SaaS Academy Master Sales Playbook** (Dan Martell) — the SaaS sales operating system.
2. **LinkedIn Prospecting Technique — The LINK Method** (SaaS Academy) — warm outbound on LinkedIn.
3. **BUNT / F3 / ARM** (sales-desk objection handling) — every objection in five letters.
4. **Lynda Jackson — Persuasion & Executive Presence** — rhetoric, voice, and "close" architectures as used in high-stakes pitches, boardrooms, and keynotes. (Note: Jackson is a communications coach, not a sales trainer. Her "closes" are speech closes — they work as the verbal architecture that lands a sales meeting, keynote pitch, or investor talk.)

**The premise:** Hormozi sells to a pipeline. Consultative sellers sell to a **person inside a buying committee**. The mechanics change. This file is the mechanics.

## PART 1 — CONSULTATIVE DISCOVERY (The Real Sale)

### The Principle

**In consultative sales, the sale is made in discovery, not in the demo.** The demo is confirmation. Discovery is the sale.

Hormozi says "label the problem." Consultative sellers go further: they identify the **pain, the cost of inaction, the impact on the business, and the impact on the person** — then position the solution as the bridge across that gap.

Your job in discovery is to uncover three things:

1. **Compelling Event** — Why now? What happens if they do nothing?
2. **Economic Impact** — What is the cost in dollars of the status quo? (Revenue missed, cost incurred, time wasted × fully loaded cost.)
3. **Personal Impact** — What does this mean for the buyer personally? Their bonus? Their promotion? Their reputation?

No compelling event → no deal this quarter. No economic impact → price objection at the end. No personal impact → internal champion won't fight for you.

### The Discovery Call Toolkit (SaaS Academy)

**Pre-Call Research** — Do not show up cold. Review the prospect's:
- LinkedIn (role, tenure, recent posts, mutual connections)
- Company (funding, headcount trajectory, recent news, tech stack)
- Pre-call survey responses (if you sent one)
- Their competitors' positioning

If during this research you realize they are not qualified — cancel the call with a polite email. Do not waste a discovery slot.

**Intro (first 2 minutes)** — Use this script exactly:

> "My job today is to determine if the challenges you are facing in your business are things we can actually address with our solution. At the end of the call, if there's a fit we can move forward. If not, we can part ways. Does that make sense?
>
> Here's what we're going to do during the call:
> - Assess the challenges you are facing
> - Discuss your vision
>
> Then I'll share examples of customers who have faced similar issues and how they found success through our solution. How does that sound?
>
> I've got us scheduled for 15 minutes — does that still work?
>
> If we find out we're a good fit for you, we can roll straight into a demo or schedule one for later. If not, I'll give you your time back. Does that sound fair?"

This script does four things in under 90 seconds:
1. Sets a mutual disqualification frame (takes pressure off both sides)
2. Agendas the call (you're in control)
3. Confirms time box (respects them)
4. Sets the "demo or no-demo" close as the next step (forces a decision)

### The Discovery Question Ladder

Open → Broad → Specific → Impact → Vision. Do not skip steps.

**1. Situation Questions (current state)**
- "Walk me through how you're currently doing [X] today."
- "What tools / team / process do you use?"
- "How long has it been set up this way?"

**2. Problem Questions (what's broken)**
- "What's not working about that?"
- "Where are the bottlenecks?"
- "What have you tried so far?"
- "What worked and what didn't?"

**3. Implication Questions (cost of inaction) — THE MONEY QUESTIONS**
- "What does that cost you in lost revenue each month?"
- "What does your team spend in time on this?"
- "If this isn't solved by [timeframe], what happens?"
- "What's the downstream impact on [related metric]?"
- "How is this affecting [their boss / team / customer]?"

**4. Need-Payoff Questions (vision of the future)**
- "If we could fix this, what would that unlock for you?"
- "What would success look like in 90 days?"
- "If you had our solution deployed, how would that impact your role? Your team? Your company?"
- "Why is now the right time to solve this?"
- "Why us?"

**Rule of the ratio:** The prospect should be talking 70% of the time. You should be talking 30%. If you're talking more than that, you're selling — not discovering.

### BANT vs MEDDIC — Pick the Right Qualifier

| Framework | Use when |
|---|---|
| **BANT** (Budget, Authority, Need, Timeline) | SMB, shorter cycles, single-decision-maker, under $25K ACV |
| **MEDDIC** (Metrics, Economic Buyer, Decision Criteria, Decision Process, Identify Pain, Champion) | Enterprise, multi-stakeholder, $50K+ ACV, 60+ day cycles |
| **MEDDPICC** (adds Paper process, Competition) | Enterprise with procurement / legal / security review |

**For the company's enterprise + church-partnership motion — use MEDDIC.** Churches and media companies both have committees. Never assume one person decides.

**MEDDIC in plain English:**
- **M** — Metrics: What are the hard numbers they'll measure? ("Reduce support tickets by 30%.")
- **E** — Economic Buyer: Who signs the check? Have you talked to them, or just to their deputy?
- **D** — Decision Criteria: What are they comparing on? (Usually the wrong list — your job is to shape it.)
- **D** — Decision Process: What are the steps between now and "yes"? Who approves? How long?
- **I** — Identify Pain: The specific pain, in their words, with cost attached.
- **C** — Champion: Someone inside who will sell for you when you're not in the room. Not just a fan — a **fighter**.

**A deal without a Champion is not a deal. It's a wish.**

## PART 2 — THE SAAS DEMO (Vehicle, Not Destination)

### Demo Structure (10-20-15-5)

Source: SaaS Academy Demo Call Script. Total: ~50 minutes.

| Step | Duration | Objective |
|---|---|---|
| Demo slide deck + Q&A | 10-15 min | Show how your service is a vehicle for THEIR growth, not a feature list |
| Explore how THEY would use the tool | 20 min | Identify the specific plan / features as their entry point |
| Pricing and packaging discussion | 15 min | Build the timeline for signing, onboarding, scaling |
| Review outstanding decision criteria + incentive-based timeline | 5 min | Agree next contact. Map remaining stakeholders. Subscription type. |

### Demo Rules

1. **Never show features — show outcomes.** Every click should map to a line item in their discovery pain list. "Remember when you told me X was costing you Y hours a week? Watch this."

2. **Personalize the demo environment.** Load their logo, their data, their use case. A generic demo = a generic decision ("we'll think about it").

3. **Use the feature/benefit battle card.** For every feature you show, state: Feature → 3 Benefits → Proof. Features describe the plane. Benefits describe the destination. Sell the destination (Hormozi's Maui Rule applies here).

4. **Trial close every 10 minutes.** "Does this solve the bottleneck you mentioned?" "How does this compare to what you're using now?" If they haven't nodded three times by minute 25, you're losing.

5. **End with an incentive-based timeline**, not "let me send a proposal." Time-bound the close: "If we sign by [date], we can get you onboarded before [compelling event]."

### Demo Close Options (in ascending pressure)

- **Soft close:** "What would you need to see to move forward?"
- **Assumed close:** "Shall I send the agreement over today so you can review with [name]?"
- **Alternative close:** "Do you want to start with Pro this month and add seats next quarter, or go straight to Premium?"
- **Calendar close:** "Let's get the kickoff on the calendar for the 15th — that gives us time to onboard before your [event]."
- **Takeaway close:** "Honestly, based on what you told me, I'm not sure this is the right fit right now. Am I missing something?" (Only use when you truly believe it, or when they've been non-committal through the demo.)

## PART 3 — LINKEDIN PROSPECTING: THE LINK METHOD

Source: SaaS Academy LinkedIn Prospecting Technique.

```
FIND  →  CONNECT  →  ENGAGE  →  BUILD  →  CONVERT
```

This is warm outbound. Cold outbound is a different game (see `references/leads.md` for Hormozi's cold outreach). LINK is for **consultative deals where the buyer checks your profile before they respond.**

### Step 0 — Your Profile Is Your Landing Page

Before you message anyone, the LinkedIn Prospecting Checklist says your profile must have:

- **Attractive Headline** — Not "CEO at the company." Try: "Helping faith organizations reach 100M+ people through digital audio." (Outcome-focused.)
- **Professional Photograph** — Hi-res, smiling, plain background.
- **Background Image** — Your brand, your product shot, or an event photo.
- **Memorable Summary** — Written in first person. Opens with the problem you solve, not your resume.
- **Appealing Media** — Pinned posts, videos, customer stories.
- **Crisp Experience** — Bullet outcomes, not job descriptions. Use numbers.
- **Good Recommendations** — 5+ from customers, not just colleagues.
- **Initial Network** — 500+ connections in your target industry before you start outbound.

**If your profile is weak, every DM you send is wasted.** Build the profile first. Then prospect.

### Step 1 — FIND (Targeting)

Build the list using LinkedIn Sales Navigator. Filter by:

- **Location** — Where are your best customers based?
- **Industry** — The industry your case studies are from.
- **Job Titles** — The primary titles / roles you sell to (3-5 max).
- **Company Size** — Based on headcount ranges where your product fits.
- **Other** — Role tenure (2+ years = stability), function, years of experience.

**Rule:** 200 targeted prospects per week is the floor. 1,000 per month is the goal. Week 1 = 200, Week 2 = 200, ..., Week 5 = 200. Five overlapping cadences running in parallel.

### Step 2 — CONNECT (The 5-Touch Message Cadence)

Never connect and pitch. Ever. LinkedIn is a social network first, a prospecting tool second.

**Touch 1 — Connecting (Value):** "Hi [Name], saw your post on [topic]. I run [company] — we help [persona] solve [problem]. Thought I'd connect." *No pitch. Just a reason.*

**Touch 2 — Resource (7 days later):** Share a specific resource. A webinar, a checklist, a case study relevant to their role. "Since we connected, thought you might find this useful — [link]."

**Touch 3 — Discussion (14 days later):** Share or tag a high-quality discussion, article, or post relevant to their industry. Ask their take.

**Touch 4 — Request (21 days later):** Offer something of value in a call — a benchmark, a free audit, an intro to a relevant peer. Not a pitch for your product. A pitch for a conversation.

**Touch 5 — Follow Up (30 days later):** Use your UPS (Unique Positioning Statement) + social proof. "We helped [similar company] achieve [specific outcome]. Worth 15 minutes to see if we could do the same for you?"

### Step 3 — ENGAGE (Value Bombs)

Between touches, engage publicly. LinkedIn's algorithm rewards creators, and your prospects are watching.

- **Group / Community** — Start or join a named group: "[Industry / Expertise] Experts." Post weekly.
- **Webinar** — 3 valuable strategies, taught live, 30 minutes. Invite your prospect list.
- **Lead Magnet** — A checklist / list / resource that solves a real problem. Gated by email capture.
- **Resources** — Share other people's content when it's useful. You're curating, not just publishing.

### Step 4 — BUILD (Relationships Before Pitches)

- **Comment on their posts weekly.** Substantive comments, not "Great post!" — add a perspective or ask a question.
- **Endorse their skills.** Costs nothing, gets noticed.
- **Share their content.** They will see it.
- **Send voice messages.** Stand out from text. 30 seconds. Casual. Human.

**The LinkedIn Sell-By-Chat rule:** Do not book calls until they've engaged with you 3+ times (liked, commented, replied). A call booked off cold-pitch converts at 5%. A call booked off warm-chat converts at 40%.

### Step 5 — CONVERT (The Ask)

When they're warm: "I've enjoyed our back-and-forth. Based on [specific detail they shared], I think there's a real fit here. Would you be open to a 20-minute call next week? Tuesday 2pm or Thursday 10am work?"

**Alternative close. Specific times. No "let me know if you're interested" — that puts the burden on them.**

### Best-Practice Rules

- Keep messaging relevant to their world, not yours.
- Add value every touch — a resource, a question, an intro, a insight.
- Remember: they are people, not prospects. The language you use shapes the outcome.
- Use conversational language — lowercase, short sentences, first-person.
- Ask relevant questions, don't lecture.
- Block 10-30 min/day for LinkedIn engagement. Consistency beats blitz.

## PART 4 — EVERY OBJECTION DISTILLS TO BUNT

Source: SalesTips.png.

```
B  —  Budget   — "Too expensive"
U  —  Urgency  — "Not right now"
N  —  Need     — "Don't need it"
T  —  Trust    — "Don't know you"
```

**Every objection is one of these four, in disguise.** Memorize BUNT and you will never be surprised again.

Mapping common objections:

| What they say | What they mean (BUNT) |
|---|---|
| "Too expensive" | Budget |
| "Let me think about it" | Urgency (usually) or Trust |
| "Send me info" | Urgency or Trust |
| "We already have a solution" | Need |
| "I need to check with my team" | Urgency or Trust |
| "Can you discount?" | Budget or Trust |
| "Not a priority right now" | Urgency |
| "I'm not sure we need this" | Need |
| "How do I know this works?" | Trust |

### F3 — Feel / Felt / Found (The Soft Handle)

Best for **emotional objections** and **price objections**. It puts the prospect on the side of past customers who are now happy.

> **Objection:** "It's too expensive."
>
> **Response:** "I understand you **FEEL** that way. We have a lot of customers who **FELT** that way before working with us — and what we've **FOUND** is that has changed after they used our solution. [Insert a specific example with a number.]"

Rules for F3:
1. Never fake the empathy. If you don't genuinely understand why they feel that way, ask another question.
2. The "Found" statement must be specific. Generic = suspicious. "We found clients get ROI in 60 days" = weak. "We found [Client X] paid back the contract in 47 days through one campaign" = strong.
3. End with a question, not a statement. "Does that change how you're thinking about it?"

### ARM — Affirm / Respond / Move It Back (The Firm Handle)

Best for **logical objections** and **stalls**. It acknowledges the concern, responds with a fact, then moves the conversation forward.

> **Objection:** "We have no budget for this."
>
> **Affirm:** "I understand budget is a concern for you."
>
> **Respond:** "We have several different pricing structures that would be right for you."
>
> **Move It Back:** "This would be a great chance to see a demo and see how we can help you solve [their specific pain]. Can we get 20 minutes on the calendar Thursday?"

The "Move It Back" is the key. Never leave an objection handled and hanging. Always propose the next step.

### The Five-Way Objection Handling Stack

When the objection is big, stack the moves:

1. **Listen completely.** Do not interrupt. Silence after they finish.
2. **Restate in your words.** "So what I'm hearing is [X]. Is that right?" (Hormozi: restatements build trust.)
3. **Agree before you disagree.** "That makes sense. If I were in your shoes, I'd probably ask the same thing."
4. **Respond with proof.** F3 for soft, data for hard.
5. **Close back to the next step.** ARM's "Move It Back."

### The Three Real Objections (Hormozi's frame, applied here)

Every BUNT maps to one of Hormozi's three real objections from `sales.md`:

- **Budget / Urgency** → "I can't afford it" → Go back to **value and cost-of-inaction**. Never lower price.
- **Need** → "I don't need it" → You didn't find the pain in discovery. Go back to **pain and implication questions**.
- **Trust** → "I don't know you" → Buy more proof. **Case study, reference call, ROI calculator.**

## PART 5 — PIPELINE HYGIENE (No Stalls In The Pipe)

Source: SaaS Academy Sales Process + Pipeline Stages.

### The 7 Pipeline Stages (with Exit Criteria)

| Stage | Definition | Exit Criteria |
|---|---|---|
| 1. **Prospecting** | Lead added to CRM, showed interest via marketing | Downloaded lead magnet OR replied to outreach |
| 2. **Qualification** | Lead fits ICP requirements | Meets ICP criteria AND booked discovery call |
| 3. **Discovery** | Discovery call booked | Completed discovery call AND booked demo |
| 4. **Demo Booked** | Demo call scheduled | Completed demo AND signed up OR proposal sent |
| 5. **Proposal Sent** | Demo done, proposal out for sign-off | Proposal signed OR officially lost |
| 6. **Stale** | Cold for 30-60 days post-demo | Re-engaged OR moved to Closed Lost |
| 7. **Closed Won** | Agreement signed | Handoff to Customer Success |

**Rule:** Every deal must have a defined next step and date. If a deal has no "next step by [date]," it's not a deal — it's a stall. Move it to Stale or kill it.

### The Mutual Action Plan (MAP)

For any deal above $25K ACV or involving 3+ stakeholders: create a MAP.

A MAP is a shared document (Google Doc or deal room) that lists:
1. The buyer's desired outcome (in their words).
2. Every step between today and go-live.
3. Owner per step (buyer side and seller side).
4. Target date per step.
5. Known risks and mitigations.

The MAP does four things:
- Forces the buyer to commit publicly to a timeline.
- Surfaces unknown stakeholders ("who owns security review?").
- Gives your Champion ammunition when selling internally.
- Lets you forecast accurately.

**Deals without MAPs slip. Deals with MAPs close.**

### Maintaining Momentum After Discovery / Demo

After every call with multiple stakeholders, do these 8 things within 24 hours:

1. Summarize notes and add to CRM
2. Write out next steps for you AND for each buyer
3. Write out what's missing (unknowns, open questions)
4. Key questions for each stakeholder
5. Call each stakeholder 1:1 to get clarity
6. Follow up with an individual email to each
7. Add a personal video to the proposal/offer email (Loom, 90 seconds) addressing each stakeholder by name
8. Update the MAP

**Rule:** A deal with no activity in 7 days is losing. Every week, either you advance or it slips.

### Sales Leadership Meeting Cadence

| Cadence | Meeting | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Yearly | 1:1 Yearly Plan | Territory, quota, career plan |
| Quarterly | 1:1 Gameplan + Group Sales Meeting | Pipeline review, forecast, playbook updates |
| Monthly | Call Coaching + 1:1 | Deal-by-deal review, coaching |
| Weekly | Group Sales Meeting + Sales Deal Coaching + Sales Call Coaching | Momentum, stuck deals, role-play |

## PART 6 — JACKSON ON PERSUASION AND EXECUTIVE PRESENCE

Lynda Jackson is a communications coach — she trains executives on keynote delivery, persuasive speaking, and rhetorical craft. Her work is the **verbal architecture** of consultative selling. In an enterprise pitch or a board meeting, **how you say it** often matters more than **what you say**.

### Audience-First Design (Performance Design for Executives)

Before any pitch, sales presentation, or investor meeting, answer these 10 questions:

1. What is the goal of this performance?
2. What do I want to do with this audience?
3. What do I want to give them?
4. What does this audience need to hear from me?
5. What do I want them to remember?
6. What emotional effect do I want to have on them?
7. What do I want the audience to do after hearing me?
8. What three words do I want them to use describing the experience?
9. What is the title of this performance?
10. Is this part of a series?

**Rule:** If you cannot answer all 10 in writing, you are not ready to present. Most salespeople and founders wing this. The ones who don't win more deals.

### Ethos + Pathos (The Persuasion Stack)

Every high-stakes sales conversation is a persuasion act. Aristotle named the three appeals:

- **Ethos** — Credibility. Why should they believe you?
- **Pathos** — Emotion. What do they feel?
- **Logos** — Logic. What do the numbers say?

Most salespeople over-index on Logos (features, spec sheets, ROI calculators). The deal is closed on Ethos and Pathos. The numbers come at the end, as the **reason the brain gives for the decision the gut already made.**

**Ethos in selling:**
- Cite your track record in their words. ("We've done this 200 times for companies like yours.")
- Acknowledge the opposing position. ("Honestly, I know there are three other vendors you're considering.")
- Establish common ground early. ("We worked with your former VP at [company].")
- Use only credible sources. A single bogus stat loses the whole deal.

**Pathos in selling:**
- Tell the story of ONE specific customer, with a name, a face, and a transformation. Not "our customers see 30% lift." Instead: "Let me tell you about Sarah at [company]. She was losing sleep over [pain]. Three months later..."
- Paint the future state vividly. What does their Monday morning look like after you solve this?
- Name the cost of inaction — personally, not just in dollars. "If this drags another quarter, what does that mean for your team? For your year?"

### The 6 Rhetorical Devices (Jackson's Core List)

Used by every great political, keynote, and sales presentation speaker.

**1. Hypophora** — Ask a question, then answer it.
> "Why are we here today? Because 70% of churches still can't reach their community between Sundays — and that's the problem we solve."

**2. Anaphora** — Repeat the opening phrase across multiple clauses. (MLK: "I have a dream...")
> "We can reach them on Monday. We can reach them at midnight. We can reach them when they are alone, afraid, and searching for meaning."

**3. Epistrophe** — Repeat the closing phrase across multiple clauses.
> "The young mom needs a moment of peace. The grieving widow needs a moment of peace. The exhausted pastor needs a moment of peace."

**4. Contrastive Pair** — Set A against not-A. (JFK: "Ask not what your country can do for you — ask what you can do for your country.")
> "We don't sell software. We deliver reach."

**5. List of Three** — Audiences retain information in threes. (Blood, sweat, tears. Life, liberty, happiness.)
> "Faster. Cheaper. At scale."

**6. Hypophora + Rhetorical Question stack** — Used to open and close.
> "What would change if every church in America could reach their community every day? I'll tell you what would change..."

**Rule for sales use:** Every pitch deck, every VSL, every discovery-call opening should use at least 2 of these. They are the verbal equivalent of design — without them, your message is flat.

### The 10 Close Architectures (From Jackson's "Types of Closes")

These are **speech closes** — the final 30 seconds of a pitch, keynote, or boardroom ask. They are the verbal architecture that lands the sale. Map them to sales moments:

| Close Type | How it works | When to use in sales |
|---|---|---|
| **Bookend** | Return to your opening story/quote. "We have arrived where we began." | Investor pitch, keynote, discovery-call recap |
| **Challenge** | "Let's do this together." Call to action. | Demo close, proposal presentation |
| **Echo** | Pull one word from a quote, echo it. ("What I hear, I forget. What I do, I understand. Now is the time to DO.") | Motivational close, offsite, sales kickoff |
| **Repetitive** | Anaphora stacked: "Architects cannot renovate it. Businesses cannot incorporate it. Only YOU can orchestrate it." | Vision / big-ticket pitch |
| **Title** | End with the title of the presentation. | Branded deck, investor narrative |
| **Sing-Song** | Repeat a phrase so the audience finishes it. ("Together, we can...") | Offsite, team rally, launch event |
| **Callback** | Reference back to a story you told earlier and complete it. | Customer success narrative, case-study close |
| **Movie** | Reference a well-known movie/book to frame the moment. | Emotional close, big-ticket consumer |
| **Quotation** | Famous quote as a spotlight on your point. | Investor pitch, thought-leadership keynote |
| **Third Party** | Quote + context + "and so must we." (Lincoln / Kennedy / etc.) | Board presentation, high-stakes M&A |

**Rule:** Pick one close per presentation. Write it out word-for-word. Memorize the first and last sentence. Everything in the middle can be improvised; these cannot.

### The Confident Speaker (Delivery Mechanics)

Also applies to discovery calls, demos, and video outreach:

- Maintain easy eye contact (break the audience/camera into 3 sections; move).
- Project voice at all times (diaphragm, not throat).
- Speak slowly and deliberately. Vary the pace.
- Use pauses AT ANY TIME. Pauses read as confidence.
- Strong, interesting start. Strong, interesting finish.
- Move but do not pace.
- Do not apologize to the audience — ever.
- Vary volume to mark the important words.
- Ask the audience questions to involve them.
- **Always give the impression of being in control** — even when something goes wrong.

**Voice dials:** Volume 1-10. Pace 1-10. Pause 1-10. Calibrate live. Most sellers speak at Pace 8, Volume 5, Pause 1. That's why they lose. Target Pace 4, Volume 6, Pause 5. Slow, loud, with silences.

### Impromptu Technique (for objection handling and Q&A)

When the prospect throws a curve:

1. Decide quickly what your ONE message is. (BUNT categorization helps.)
2. Write three main points in your head — or don't, and pick one.
3. Do not memorize. Speak from the message.
4. Strong start (a rhetorical device). Strong finish (one of the 10 closes above).
5. Point 1, Point 2, Point 3. That's it.
6. Volume 6. Slow pace. Confident posture.
7. Eye contact.
8. Trust your instinct on the extra words.

**Impromptu is the skill that separates $100K reps from $500K reps.**

## PART 7 — HORMOZI vs THIS CANON (Where They Overlap and Differ)

| Dimension | Hormozi | This Canon |
|---|---|---|
| Motion | Direct-response, high-volume | Consultative, multi-stakeholder |
| Deal size | $3K-$30K ACV typical | $25K-$500K+ ACV typical |
| Cycle length | One-call close (target) to 14 days | 30-180 days |
| Buyer | Individual / small-business owner | Buying committee (3-15 people) |
| Primary skill | Conviction + objection stamina | Discovery + political mapping |
| Weapon | CLOSER + VSL + proof stack | MEDDIC + MAP + Champion |
| Volume | 100+ calls/week per rep | 10-30 calls/week per rep |
| Price lever | Value equation, never discount | Value engineering + procurement dance |
| Objection book | 3 real objections | BUNT (B + U + N + T) |
| Proof mechanism | Case study volume | Reference calls + ROI model + peer intro |

**Where they overlap (use both, always):**
- **CLOSER framework** runs inside every discovery call.
- **Sell the destination, not the vehicle** (the Maui rule) applies in every demo.
- **Conviction corrects tone.** Belief in your product is the single biggest driver of close rate in both motions.
- **Never lower your price.** Discounts decrease LTV by ~40% — Hormozi's law applies to enterprise too.
- **Proof hierarchy** — in-person > virtual, live > recorded, raw > polished. True for enterprise proof materials too.

**Where this canon adds (do these on top):**
- Multi-touch prospecting cadence (LINK Method).
- Multi-stakeholder pipeline mapping (MEDDIC + MAP).
- Demo personalization beyond the standard script.
- Rhetorical craft (Jackson) layered on delivery.
- BUNT framework for objection categorization (cleaner than Hormozi's 3-objection model for multi-stakeholder deals where objections come from different people).

**Where Hormozi is still right:**
- In one-to-one calls, CLOSER beats most consultative frameworks.
- 99% of consultative sellers under-index on volume and over-index on "relationship building." Hormozi's reps-before-refinement rule applies.
- Most "consultative" deals are over-complicated SMB deals that should be one-call closes. If your ACV is under $10K — Hormozi's playbook is better. Full stop.

## PART 8 — APPLICATIONS FOR A CONSUMER SUBSCRIPTION + CONSULTATIVE HYBRID BUSINESS

The company primarily runs a **consumer subscription** motion (App Store, free-to-paid, monthly/annual). That's Hormozi territory (volume, creative, paywall, VSL). **But the company also runs several consultative motions** where this file is the operating manual.

### Use Case 1 — Church Partnership Deals (B2B Consultative)

**Context:** Partnering with individual churches or denominations to distribute the company content, run co-branded campaigns, or integrate prayer experiences into their Sunday flow.

**Apply:**
- **LINK Method** on LinkedIn, targeting senior pastors, executive pastors, and denominational leaders. 5-touch cadence, heavy on value (Bible in a Year stats, worship partnership examples, community engagement data).
- **Discovery Call Toolkit** — uncover the real pain: "Members disengage Monday-Saturday," "Can't track who's actually spiritually engaged," "Small group attendance declining." Pain → Implication → Vision.
- **MEDDIC qualification** — Senior Pastor, Executive Pastor, Worship Pastor, Youth Pastor, Board Chair, Treasurer. All stakeholders. Identify the Economic Buyer (usually Senior Pastor or Board).
- **MAP** — 60-90 day deployment plan with the church's calendar mapped (Easter, Christmas, summer series).
- **Jackson's rhetoric** — pastors ARE professional rhetoricians. Your pitch has to clear their bar. Use Ethos (the company's mission), Pathos (transformation stories), Contrastive Pair ("We don't compete with Sunday service. We extend it.").

### Use Case 2 — Enterprise Media Licensing (B2B High-ACV)

**Context:** Licensing the company content libraries (Bible in a Year, meditations, kids' content) to media companies, streaming services, smart-speaker platforms, hotels, airlines.

**Apply:**
- **MEDDPICC** (add Paper process and Competition — procurement, legal, security review are mandatory here).
- **Mutual Action Plan** is non-negotiable. These deals take 90-180 days.
- **Champion identification** — find the product lead who wants to differentiate their offering with faith content. They are your fighter.
- **Proof stack** — download numbers, engagement metrics, retention curves, celebrity endorsements (the proof hierarchy from `sales.md`).
- **Jackson's 10 Close Architectures** — board-level pitches need the Third-Party Close or the Bookend Close. Name-drop and vision.
- **Pricing conversation** — Hormozi's "never lower" holds. But structure with usage tiers, minimum guarantees, and co-marketing add-backs.

### Use Case 3 — Content Creator / Ambassador Deals

**Context:** Partnership deals with faith creators, pastors, authors, and ambassadors who produce content for the company.

**Apply:**
- **LinkedIn + Twitter prospecting** using the LINK engage-then-ask flow.
- **BUNT-handling** — creators usually object on Trust ("Will this be on-brand?") and Urgency ("I'm booked for the year"). Use F3 on both.
- **Discovery** — what are THEIR audience outcomes? How does partnership with the company help them reach more people / monetize better / deepen their mission?
- **MAP** for content delivery schedule, rights, royalty structure, renewal terms.

### Use Case 4 — Advertising / Sponsorship Sales (Inbound + Outbound)

**Context:** Selling sponsorship inventory across the company podcasts, app placements, email sponsorships.

**Apply:**
- **Inbound qualification** — BANT is fine for SMB sponsors. MEDDIC for brand sponsors over $100K.
- **Demo** — personalized insertion mock (their ad in our top episode), audience composition breakdown, attribution story.
- **Objection** — "We need to see the ROAS" is Trust + Budget. F3: "I hear you — every brand we sign feels that way. What we found with [Brand X] is they saw [specific metric] in the first 60 days. What would you need to see to feel confident?"
- **Close** — Alternative close with quarterly insertion commits. Calendar close tied to upcoming events (Lent, Advent, Easter, Christmas).

### Use Case 5 — Executive Communication (Jackson Applied Internally)

Steve's keynotes, board presentations, all-hands, investor updates. Not selling the product — selling the vision. All 10 of Jackson's performance-design questions apply. Pick a close architecture. Rehearse the first sentence and last sentence.

**The Ethos / Pathos / Logos stack for the company internal comms:**
- **Ethos** — Your track record (Bible in a Year #1 podcast, 100M+ reach, category creator).
- **Pathos** — The specific member whose life changed. Name them. Tell their story.
- **Logos** — The growth numbers, the retention curve, the LTV math.

Order matters: **Ethos → Pathos → Logos.** Most founders lead with Logos. That's why most decks land flat.

## PART 9 — THE 10 RULES OF CONSULTATIVE SELLING (THE SHORT LIST)

Pin this above the desk.

1. **Discovery is the sale.** If you didn't get it in discovery, you won't get it in the close.
2. **70/30.** Prospect talks 70%, you talk 30%. If you flip it, you lose.
3. **No compelling event, no deal this quarter.**
4. **A deal without a Champion is a wish.**
5. **Every objection maps to BUNT.** Categorize first, respond second.
6. **Features describe the plane. Benefits describe Maui. Sell Maui.** (Hormozi)
7. **MAP or it slips.** Above $25K ACV, always.
8. **Ethos → Pathos → Logos.** In that order. Every time.
9. **Never lower the price.** Restructure, bundle, scope-reduce — never discount. (Hormozi's law holds enterprise too.)
10. **Volume negates luck, even here.** The best consultative rep still runs more touches than the average one. 30+ new LinkedIn connections per week. 10+ new discovery calls per week. 5+ live demos per week. Inputs drive outputs.

## APPENDIX A — THE 5-MINUTE PRE-CALL CHECKLIST

Before every discovery call:

- [ ] Pulled up their LinkedIn profile
- [ ] Scanned last 3 posts for themes / pain signals
- [ ] Checked their company's recent news / funding / press
- [ ] Identified the likely compelling event (end of fiscal year? new hire? product launch? board pressure?)

- [ ] Pulled their pre-call survey responses (if sent)
- [ ] Closed other tabs. Silenced notifications. Water on the desk. Posture set.

If any box is unchecked: postpone the call rather than wing it.

## APPENDIX B — THE 8-STEP POST-CALL SEQUENCE (SAME DAY)

After every discovery, demo, or proposal call:

1. Summarize call in CRM within 1 hour.
2. Write next steps for you AND the buyer.
3. Write what's missing / open.
4. Key questions for each stakeholder.
5. Individual call to each stakeholder (or email where warranted).
6. Personalized follow-up email to each.
7. Loom video (60-90 seconds) on the proposal addressing each stakeholder by name.
8. Update Mutual Action Plan.
