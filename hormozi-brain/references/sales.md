# Alex Hormozi: Sales Mental Models

Complete reference of every framework, principle, and quote related to how Hormozi thinks about selling, closing, handling objections, scaling sales teams, and the psychology of persuasion.

## THE CLOSER FRAMEWORK

Hormozi's complete sales call structure. Each letter is a phase of the conversation. The entire framework is designed to move someone from stranger to buyer through a logical, empathetic sequence.

### C — Clarify Why They Are There

The first job is to understand why this person showed up. What problem are they trying to solve? What prompted them to book this call today? This is not small talk. This is diagnostic.

Key questions:
- "What made you decide to book this call?"
- "What's going on right now that made this a priority?"
- "What are you hoping to get out of this conversation?"

The goal is to get them talking about their current situation and desired outcome in their own words. You need to hear the gap between where they are and where they want to be — described by them, not by you.

### L — Label Them with a Problem

Once you understand their situation, label the problem clearly and specifically. Labeling does two things: it makes them feel understood, and it gives the problem a name, which makes it feel solvable.

"So it sounds like the core issue is [specific problem label]. Is that right?"

The label must be specific, not generic. Not "you're struggling with marketing." Instead: "you're generating leads but they're not converting because you don't have a follow-up system, so you're leaving money on the table every single day."

When you label accurately, the prospect feels heard. When they feel heard, they trust you. When they trust you, they listen to your solution.

### O — Overview Past Pain

This is the most critical phase. You walk them through their history of failed attempts. This is the pain cycle.

**The Pain Cycle Questions (in order):**

1. **"What have you tried?"** — Get every past attempt on the table. Every program, course, coach, strategy, DIY effort.

2. **"How long did you try it?"** — Establishes commitment level and patience.

3. **"How long ago was that?"** — Establishes how long they have been suffering with this unsolved problem.

4. **"How did it work?"** — They will tell you it did not work. Let them say it. Do not say it for them.

5. **"What else have you tried?"** — Repeat the cycle. Get every failed attempt. The more failed attempts you surface, the more pain is present, and the more they need a new solution.

The purpose of this phase is NOT to make them feel bad. It is to make the cost of inaction vivid and undeniable. They need to feel the full weight of how long this has been a problem, how many things they have tried, and how much time, money, and energy they have wasted — so that doing nothing is no longer acceptable.

This phase also pre-handles objections. When they have told you about five things that did not work, they cannot say "I'll just figure it out on my own" — they have already proven to themselves that they cannot.

### S — Sell the Vacation

**Keep this under 3 minutes.** This is the most common mistake salespeople make — they talk too long about the product.

"Sell the vacation, not the plane flight." Nobody cares about seat assignments, turbulence, and layovers. They care about the beach, the cocktails, and the sunset.

**Rules for this phase:**
- Use stories and analogies, NOT modules and features
- Paint the picture of their life after the problem is solved
- Connect your solution to their specific pain points from the Overview phase
- Do not list what they get. Describe what changes.
- Under 3 minutes. Seriously. Under 3 minutes.

If you find yourself explaining module 4 of your 12-week program, you have lost. If you find yourself saying "and you also get..." more than twice, you have lost. The prospect does not care about what is in the box. They care about what the box does to their life.

### E — Explain Away Concerns

After the pitch, concerns will arise. There are only three real objections in all of sales:

**1. Price ("I can't afford it")**
This is almost never actually about money. "If we were giving away Ferraris for $5,000, would they find the money?" Of course they would. Price objections are always a failure to communicate value. If the prospect truly understood the value and believed they would get the result, they would find the money.

When you hear a price objection, do not lower the price. Go back to value. Restate the dream outcome. Restate the cost of inaction. Make the gap between price and value undeniable.

"I totally understand. Let me ask you this — if this works and you [specific outcome they described], what's that worth to you over the next year? Five years? Now, is [price] a reasonable investment to get that?"

**2. Decision Maker ("I need to talk to my spouse/partner/etc.")**
Three approaches, in order of preference:

- **Circumvent** — Handle this before it comes up. "Is there anyone else involved in making this decision?" Ask this early in the call. If yes, get them on the call.
- **Past Agreements** — "Have you and [spouse] ever made decisions independently and told each other after? What was the last big purchase you made on your own?" Establish precedent that they can and do make decisions alone.
- **Ask Forgiveness** — "In my experience, when people believe something is right for them, they make the decision and share the good news. Would your [spouse] be upset that you invested in [their dream outcome], or would they be proud of you for taking action?"

**3. Stalls ("I need to think about it" / "Let me sleep on it")**
"Let's think about it together. That's what I'm here for."

Then identify which of the three buying conditions is missing. A person needs three things to buy:
1. **Believe you can solve their problem** — If this is missing, go back to proof, testimonials, and the mechanism.
2. **Like and trust you** — If this is missing, you moved too fast. Build more rapport.
3. **Have the financial means** — If this is the real issue, discuss payment plans, financing, or adjusted scope.

"Which of these is the holdup? I just want to make sure I've given you everything you need to make a confident decision."

### R — Reinforce the Decision

The sale is not over when they say yes. The sale is over when they have fully committed and feel great about it. Buyer's remorse kills deals, increases refunds, and destroys LTV.

**Reinforcement tactics:**
- **Video from the CEO** — A personal welcome video that makes them feel like they joined something special.
- **Handwritten card** — Physical mail stands out in a digital world. A handwritten note saying "Welcome, we're excited to work with you" creates an emotional anchor.
- **Onboarding within 48 hours** — Do not let them sit with their decision for a week before hearing from you. Contact them within 48 hours. The longer the gap between purchase and first contact, the higher the refund rate.
- **Quick win** — Give them something to do immediately that produces a small result. This validates their decision and creates momentum.
- **Community introduction** — Introduce them to other customers who are getting results. Social proof after the sale is just as important as social proof before the sale.

## THREE THINGS ON A SALES CALL

The entire sales conversation should consist of only three types of communication:

### 1. Questions
Questions control the conversation. The person asking questions is leading. The person answering questions is following. Every question should have a purpose — either diagnostic (understanding their situation), emotional (surfacing pain or desire), or closing (moving toward the decision).

### 2. Restatements
Repeat back what they said in your own words. This proves you are listening, builds trust, and gives them a chance to correct you. "So what I'm hearing is..." or "Let me make sure I understand..." Restatements are the most underused tool in sales. They cost nothing and create enormous rapport.

### 3. Anecdotal Stories (30-Second Belief Breakers)
Short, specific stories about past clients who had the exact same objection, concern, or situation — and what happened. These are 30-second stories, not 5-minute monologues.

"I had a client, Sarah, who said the exact same thing. She was working 60 hours a week and didn't think she had time for this. Within 3 weeks, she had freed up 15 hours by implementing just the first module. Now she's working less and making more."

Belief breakers work because they are specific, relatable, and show — rather than tell — that the objection is unfounded. Build a library of 20-30 of these stories covering every common objection.

## SELL THE VACATION, NOT THE PLANE FLIGHT

The central sales analogy Hormozi uses to teach positioning and pitch construction.

### The Maui Analogy

Imagine you are selling a trip to Maui. There are four ways to get there:

- **Swim** — Free, but you will probably die. (DIY with no guidance)
- **Boat** — Cheap, but slow and uncomfortable. (Budget option)
- **Commercial Flight** — Reasonable price, gets you there reliably. (Standard offer)
- **Private Jet** — Expensive, but fast, luxurious, and guaranteed. (Premium offer)

"Likelihood of arrival is assumed. Variables are speed and quality."

The key insight: nobody is buying the transportation. They are buying Maui. The beach. The mai tais. The sunset. The week with their family. Your job is to sell Maui — and then explain that your vehicle is the best way to get there.

### Application
- Do not spend the call explaining your 12-module course structure (the plane flight)
- Spend the call painting the picture of their life after the problem is solved (Maui)
- Then briefly explain that your program is the private jet — the fastest, most reliable way to get there
- Features describe the plane. Benefits describe the destination. Sell the destination.

## THE CONVICTION FRAMEWORK

"Conviction will correct your tone."

Sales effectiveness is 10% what you say and 90% how you say it. Tone, pace, confidence, and certainty are communicated subconsciously. A salesperson who is uncertain about their product will communicate that uncertainty no matter how perfect their script is.

### Words = 10%, How You Say Them = 90%

A mediocre script delivered with absolute conviction will outsell a perfect script delivered with hesitation every single time. This is why belief in the product is the prerequisite to all sales skill.

### How to Build Conviction

**Re-read testimonials daily.** Every morning before calls, read 5-10 testimonials from people your product has helped. This is not motivational — it is calibration. It puts you in a state of genuine belief based on evidence.

**Fix your product constantly.** If there is something about your product that makes you hesitate, fix it. Do not learn to sell around flaws. Eliminate the flaws. The best salespeople sell products they would buy themselves.

**The Mother Test: "Would I sell my mother this?"** If the answer is no, do not sell it to anyone. Fix the product until the answer is yes. If you would enthusiastically sell this to your mother, your conviction will be unshakable.

### Conviction vs. Manipulation
Conviction is not manipulation. Manipulation is convincing someone to buy something that is not good for them. Conviction is the absolute certainty that what you are selling will genuinely improve their life. If you have that certainty, selling is a moral obligation — not selling is withholding help.

## OBJECTION HANDLING (DETAILED)

### Price Objections

**Core principle: Price objections are always a failure to communicate value.**

"If we were giving away Ferraris for $5,000, would they find the money?" Yes. Everyone would find $5,000 for a Ferrari. The reason they "can't afford" your offer is not that they lack money — it is that they do not perceive the value to be dramatically higher than the price.

**The Fix:**
1. Do not lower the price. Do not offer a discount. Do not flinch.
2. Go back to the dream outcome. "Help me understand — if this works exactly as described, what would that be worth to you?"
3. Restate the cost of inaction. "You mentioned you've been dealing with this for [X years] and it's costing you [specific pain]. What does another year of that cost?"
4. Reframe the price. "It's not $5,000. It's $14/day for a year. Is $14/day worth [their dream outcome]?"
5. If truly a financial issue, offer payment plans. Never reduce scope or value.

### Decision Maker Objections

**Circumvent (prevention is better than cure):**
Ask early: "Is there anyone else who would be involved in a decision like this?" If yes, reschedule with both parties present. Never pitch to someone who cannot say yes.

**Past Agreements:**
"Have you and your wife ever made an investment decision on your own and told each other after?" Most people have. Buying a car, signing up for a gym, making a business purchase. Establish that independent decisions are normal and acceptable.

**Ask Forgiveness:**
"If you went home tonight and told your spouse you invested in solving [their biggest problem], would they be upset? Or would they be proud that you took action?" Reframe the decision as courage, not recklessness.

### Stalls ("Let Me Think About It")

**"Let's think about it together."** Do not let them leave to think alone. Thinking alone means forgetting the pain, rationalizing inaction, and talking themselves out of it.

**Diagnose the real issue.** A stall means one of three things is missing:

1. **They don't believe you can solve it.** Response: "What specifically are you unsure about? I want to make sure I address it." Then provide proof — testimonials, case studies, guarantees.

2. **They don't like or trust you enough.** Response: Slow down. Be more human. Ask about their life. Share your own story. Trust is built through vulnerability and genuine interest, not through pressure.

3. **They genuinely cannot afford it.** Response: "I totally understand. Let's look at what options might work with your current budget." Then offer payment plans or a down-sell.

## THE AVAILABILITY PRINCIPLE

> "The #1 correlation with increased sales wasn't close rates — it was increased availability."

This finding came from Acquisition.com's analysis across portfolio companies. The business with the most open calendar slots wins — not because they're better closers, but because they made it easier to buy.

**More open slots = more booked calls = fewer cancellations = more sales.**

The business with the most availability wins. It's not because they're better or smarter — they just made it easier to become the obvious choice.

**Tactical application:** Staff shifts from 6am EST to 9pm PST, 7 days/week. Maximize the window where someone can talk to your team. Every hour your calendar is closed is revenue you're leaving on the table.

## SCALING SALES TEAMS: THE 6 C'S

### 1. Closer Sequence
A documented, step-by-step sales process that every salesperson follows. Not a rigid script, but a structured sequence of phases (like CLOSER). This ensures consistency, makes training easier, and makes performance measurable. If your sales process lives only in one closer's head, you do not have a scalable team — you have a dependency.

### 2. Consistent Daily Training (60 Minutes Per Day)
Every day, the sales team trains for 60 minutes. This is non-negotiable. Training includes:
- Script review and refinement
- Role plays (one person sells, one person plays a difficult prospect)
- Objection handling drills
- Reviewing call recordings and giving feedback
- Studying product knowledge and testimonials

"Most sales teams train once during onboarding and never again. That's like a professional athlete practicing once and then only playing games."

### 3. Call Recordings
Every sales call is recorded. Recordings are reviewed by managers and used for training. This creates accountability, enables coaching, and provides data for improvement. Without recordings, feedback is based on memory and self-reporting — both unreliable.

### 4. Communication Cycles
Regular, structured communication between sales leadership and the team:
- Daily huddles (10-15 minutes, energy and focus)
- Weekly one-on-ones (individual coaching and development)
- Monthly reviews (performance metrics, trends, pipeline)
- Quarterly planning (goals, targets, strategy adjustments)

### 5. Cuts (Bottom 10% Quarterly)
Every quarter, evaluate the team and let go of the bottom 10% of performers. This sounds harsh. It is necessary. The bottom performers drag down team morale, waste leads, and create a culture where mediocrity is acceptable. Cutting consistently keeps the team sharp and creates urgency to perform.

### 6. Competition and Career Path
Salespeople are competitive by nature. Use this. Leaderboards, bonuses, prizes, public recognition. But also provide a career path — sales rep to team lead to sales manager to VP of Sales. Without a career path, your best closers will leave for a company that offers one.

## SALES HIRING

### The Test: Four Things to Evaluate

**1. Speed of Response**
How quickly did they respond to the job posting or inquiry? Speed of response correlates with hunger, discipline, and work ethic. A candidate who responds in 10 minutes is very different from one who responds in 3 days.

**2. Intelligence**
Not academic intelligence. Practical intelligence. Can they think on their feet? Can they process new information quickly? Can they connect dots? Sales requires real-time problem solving in unscripted conversations.

**3. Listening**
Do they actually hear what you say, or are they waiting for their turn to talk? In the interview, say something specific and see if they reference it later. Good listeners make great salespeople because sales is mostly listening.

**4. Coachability**
Give them feedback in the interview. Change something about their pitch. See how they respond. Do they get defensive? Do they argue? Or do they immediately implement the feedback? Coachability determines whether someone will improve over time. An uncoachable salesperson is a fixed liability.

### Training Focus: Prospect Knowledge, NOT Product Knowledge

This is counterintuitive. Most companies train salespeople on the product — features, modules, pricing tiers, technical specs. This is backwards.

Train on the prospect. Who are they? What are their fears, desires, and frustrations? What have they tried before? What language do they use to describe their problems? What keeps them up at night?

A salesperson who deeply understands the prospect can sell anything. A salesperson who deeply understands the product but not the prospect will recite features to people who do not care.

### 14-Day Onboarding Protocol

**Days 1-7: Immersion**
- Listen to 40+ recorded sales calls (both successful closes and losses)
- Study the sales script / CLOSER framework
- Study testimonials and case studies
- Study the ideal customer profile in depth
- Written test on prospect knowledge (not product knowledge)

**Days 8-14: Practice**
- Daily role plays with existing team members
- Shadow live calls (listen only)
- Practice calls with warm leads (low stakes)
- Daily feedback sessions with sales manager
- Graduated to live calls only after demonstrating competency

## THE 25-CALL RULE

It takes 25 calls for a new salesperson to reach basic proficiency.

### The Science
This is based on stress response theory. When humans encounter a new, high-stakes situation (like a sales call), the body triggers a stress response — elevated heart rate, sweaty palms, racing thoughts, fight-or-flight activation. This stress response impairs cognitive function, tone, and composure.

Research shows it takes 25-30 neutral exposures to a stressful stimulus for the stress response to normalize. After 25 calls, the body no longer treats each call as a threat. The salesperson can think clearly, respond naturally, and be present in the conversation instead of battling their own nervous system.

### Implications
- Do not judge a new salesperson's ability based on their first 25 calls
- Do not put a new salesperson on your best leads for their first 25 calls
- Expect calls 1-25 to be mediocre — that is biological, not a skill issue
- Start seeing real signal on skill and potential around call 26-50
- If someone is still struggling after 50 calls, it may be a fit issue

## THE MARRIAGE PROPOSAL METHOD

You would never walk up to a stranger and say "Will you marry me?" Yet that is exactly what most salespeople do — they pitch a stranger on a major commitment without building up to it.

### The Right Sequence

Instead of asking "Will you marry me?" cold, you build through a series of smaller, agreeable steps:

1. **"Could you see yourself spending your life with someone?"** (Yes, of course.)
2. **"What would that person be like?"** (They describe their ideal.)
3. **"Do I fit that description?"** (If you have been a good partner, yes.)
4. **THEN ask.** (The answer is almost always yes because you built to it.)

### Application to Sales

1. **"Could you see yourself solving this problem?"** (Yes, of course.)
2. **"What would the ideal solution look like?"** (They describe exactly what you offer.)
3. **"Does what I described match what you're looking for?"** (If you listened well, yes.)
4. **"Then let's get you started."** (Natural progression, not a hard close.)

Each step is a small yes that builds momentum toward the big yes. By the time you ask for the sale, they have already said yes three times. The close is not a surprise — it is a formality.

### Why This Works
- It puts the prospect in control — they are describing what they want, not being told what to buy
- It creates alignment — you are matching their words to your solution
- It eliminates pressure — the close feels like a mutual decision, not a one-sided push
- It surfaces objections early — if they say no at step 2, you know exactly what to address

## THE SCALE OF 1-10 CLOSE

A versatile technique for uncovering hidden objections and moving toward a close.

### The Technique

**"On a scale of 1 to 10, how would you rate [your experience / your interest / your confidence in this]?"**

### If They Say 10
Close. They are ready. "Awesome. Let's get you started."

### If They Say Less Than 10
**"What would make it a 10?"**

This is the power of the question. They will tell you exactly what is missing. They will hand you the objection, the concern, or the condition on a silver platter. You do not have to guess. You do not have to push. They tell you precisely what to fix, address, or offer.

Common responses and what they reveal:
- "If I knew it would work for my specific situation" = needs more proof/testimonials
- "If the price were a little lower" = needs value reframing or payment plan
- "If I could talk to someone who's done it" = needs social proof / reference call
- "If it included X" = needs a bonus or modification
- "If I had more time to decide" = needs urgency reframe or fear addressed

### After They Tell You What Would Make It a 10
Address that specific thing. Then ask again: "Now that we've addressed that, where are you on the scale?" Repeat until they are at a 10 or you have surfaced an irreconcilable issue.

### Why This Works
- It gives you specific, actionable intelligence instead of vague resistance
- It makes the prospect do the diagnostic work for you
- It creates a collaborative dynamic (working together to get to 10) instead of adversarial (you pushing, them resisting)
- It works in any context — sales, management, relationships, customer feedback

## ADDITIONAL SALES PRINCIPLES

### The Tone Principle
Your tone communicates more than your words. A concerned, empathetic tone during the pain phase. A confident, certain tone during the pitch. A casual, assumptive tone during the close. Master your tone and you master sales.

### The Silence Principle
After you ask a closing question, stop talking. The first person to speak loses. Silence creates pressure that the prospect will fill — usually with either a yes or the real objection they have been hiding. Most salespeople are so uncomfortable with silence that they talk themselves out of the sale.

### The Assumptive Close
Do not ask "Would you like to move forward?" Ask "Which payment option works best for you?" or "Should we start Monday or Wednesday?" Assume the sale and present a choice between two yeses, not a choice between yes and no.

### The Takeaway
If a prospect is on the fence, suggest that maybe this is not the right fit. "Honestly, based on what you've shared, I'm not sure this is right for you." This counterintuitively increases desire because people want what they cannot have. It also positions you as a trusted advisor rather than a pushy salesperson.

### The 48-Hour Follow-Up Rule
If a prospect does not close on the call, follow up within 48 hours. After 48 hours, the emotional momentum from the call dies. The pain fades. The excitement fades. They go back to their default state. Your window is 48 hours.

### The Stack Close
Present every element of the offer individually with its own value, then stack them:
- "The core program, which is worth $X..."
- "Plus the bonus, which alone is worth $Y..."
- "Plus the guarantee, which makes this risk-free..."
- "Total value: $Z. Your investment today: [fraction of Z]."

The visual and emotional impact of a large stacked value compared to a small price creates an irresistible gap.

### Revenue Per Sales Call
The key metric for sales team health. Total revenue divided by total calls. Not close rate — revenue per call. A salesperson with a 20% close rate at $10,000 produces $2,000 per call. A salesperson with a 40% close rate at $2,000 produces $800 per call. Revenue per call is what matters.

### The Transfer of Belief
"Sales is a transfer of belief from the salesperson to the prospect." You cannot transfer what you do not have. If you do not genuinely believe your product will help this person, no technique will save you. Belief is the foundation. Everything else is just communication.
