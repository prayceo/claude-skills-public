# Alex Hormozi: $100M Money Models — Monetization Operating System

Complete reference of every framework, principle, and tactic from $100M Money Models (2025). This is the definitive playbook for getting more customers to spend more money faster.

---

## THE CORE PREMISE

A Money Model is a deliberate sequence of offers — what you offer, when you offer it, and how you offer it — designed to make as much money as you can, as fast as you can.

The goal: **Make enough profit from one customer to acquire and service at least two more customers in less than 30 days.** When you achieve this, cash stops being a constraint and growth becomes self-funding.

> "There might not be anything wrong with your product. You might just have something wrong with your money model."

---

## THE THREE STAGES

Building a Money Model follows three stages. **Perfect one stage before moving to the next.** Simple scales; fancy fails.

### Stage I: GET CASH
**Goal:** Perfect a single Attraction Offer that acquires customers and covers costs.

- Focus: Get customers reliably
- Key metric: Can you acquire a customer profitably?
- Mistake: Trying to build a complex funnel before proving you can get ONE customer to pay

### Stage II: GET MORE CASH
**Goal:** Add Upsell and Downsell Offers to maximize 30-day profit per customer.

- Focus: Make sure each customer pays for themselves AND funds the acquisition of more customers
- Key metric: 30-day cash collected per customer vs. fully loaded CAC
- Mistake: Skipping Stage I and jumping to upsells without a proven attraction offer

### Stage III: GET THE MOST CASH
**Goal:** Integrate Continuity Offers to maximize total lifetime value.

- Focus: Stack recurring revenue so each customer's total spend keeps growing
- Key metric: LTV and net revenue retention
- Mistake: Adding continuity before you have reliable customer acquisition and 30-day monetization

### The Sequence in Practice
1. First, get customers reliably
2. Then, make sure they pay for themselves reliably
3. Then, make sure they pay for OTHER customers reliably
4. Then, maximize each customer's long-term value
5. Then, spend as many advertising dollars as you can to print as much money as possible

## THE FOUR OFFER TYPES

Every Money Model uses four types of offers, each serving a specific purpose in the customer journey.

### 1. ATTRACTION OFFERS (Stage I)
**Purpose:** Convert strangers into initial paying customers. Solve the cash constraint.

Attraction Offers are the entry point. They must be compelling enough that strangers say yes, and profitable enough that you don't lose money acquiring customers.

#### Attraction Offer Tactics

**Win Your Money Back**
- Goal-based refund tied to results or actions
- "Complete the program and hit [milestone], and we'll refund your investment"
- Creates commitment (they do the work) and confidence (risk reversal)
- Those who complete the program get great results → testimonials → more customers
- Three refund criteria types: **Results** (hit X metric), **Actions** (attend all sessions, log progress), or **Both** (follow directions AND get the result)
- Good criteria are: easy to track, likely to produce results, and require customers to advertise the business (posting progress, tagging on social, referring, leaving reviews)
- Only ~10% of customers actually qualify for their money back based on data from thousands of gyms
- Offer **store credit** instead of cash back — testing showed same customer acquisition, better economics
- **How to apply credit:** Spread $600 credit over 12 months ($50/mo discount) rather than giving 3 months free. Keeps skin in the game.
- **Make everyone a winner in private:** Halfway through, offer the rollover upsell as if they've already won — lowers anxiety and keeps them longer
- **Let the losers win too:** If they refuse the first upsell and fail the challenge, act like they won anyway and credit their deposit toward the long-term offer
- All check-in meetings should be mandatory criteria AND upsell opportunities: nutrition orientation (supplement offer), progress check (membership offer), transformation feedback (membership offer again + prepay discount)

**Giveaways**
- Advertise a grand prize to attract a large audience
- Collect leads (everyone who enters) — ask for contact info AND eligibility/character questions ("Why should we pick you?")
- Pick one winner, then offer EVERYONE ELSE a "partial scholarship" / promotional offer at 10-30% off gross margins
- The giveaway is the lead magnet; the promotional offer is the monetization
- Assign a dollar value to the grand prize to anchor the promotional offer price
- **Double the leads:** Give away TWO prizes — if someone they refer wins, the referrer wins too. Creates viral referral loops.
- **Triple urgency:** Add deadlines to enter (7 days), to claim the promotional offer (7 days), and to use it (hours to days)
- Run daily countdown updates across all channels with social proof and benefit reminders during the giveaway window
- If the giveaway doesn't work, the grand prize wasn't grand enough — test 10x the value
- Limit entries to the number of people you can actually call within 7 days
- Have downsell offers ready for those who can't afford the promotional offer — offer the same percentage discount on any other product
- Cap entries by time, number, or both to create real scarcity

**Decoy Offers (The Turkey Talk)**
- Advertise a cheap, stripped-down version alongside your premium offer
- The cheap version makes the premium version look like incredible value by contrast
- The decoy isn't meant to sell — it's meant to make the real offer sell better
- **The Turkey Talk:** Explain WHY the cheap option won't work (like cooking a turkey at double temperature — you burn it). Build the logical case for premium.
- Present both options side by side, then ask: "Which do you think will get you to your goal faster?"
- Permission to sell premium: "Are you here for free stuff or lasting results?" When they say results, skip to premium.
- If they take the decoy, that's fine — it gives you time to upgrade them via upsells later
- **Surprise and delight:** If someone takes the decoy, throw in a few low-cost premium features anyway to build goodwill for future upsells
- Discount offers have higher show-up rates than free offers — if you have high no-show costs (doctors, lawyers), use discounts instead
- Advertise benefits not features — "21-day transformation" not "workouts and meal plans"
- Four ways to frame the same discount: percentage off, absolute dollars off, free portion ("3 months free"), or total package with crossed-out original price. Test which converts best.

**Buy X Get Y Free**
- Reframe the price through bundling
- Works better when the free item (Y) has HIGHER perceived value than the paid item (X)
- Example: "Buy the consultation ($500), get the implementation kit ($2,000 value) free"
- **The Boot Factory Model:** Triple the price of one item and give away two more "free" — same total price but the free framing attracts far more customers. Three $200 boots = $600 vs. one $600 pair + two free pairs. Same price, wildly different response.
- **Always give MORE free stuff than paid stuff.** "Buy 1 get 2 free" beats "buy 2 get 1 free" every time. People see free QUANTITY.
- The free items can be DIFFERENT from the paid items — mix and match. Sometimes more cheaper free items beat fewer expensive free items (3 free pairs of socks > 1 free shirt, even at equal cost).
- **Services version:** "Buy 6 months get 6 months free" (better) → "Buy 6 months get 12 months free" (best). The more free time relative to paid, the more compelling.
- **Raise prices BEFORE giving away free stuff** to preserve margins. Permanently raise prices since this is what all new customers come in on.
- **Make this offer to existing recurring customers for fast cash** — cap at 10% of customer base so you don't wreck cash flow. Prepaid customers can still be upsold different stuff.
- If customers only buy once (like tourists), make that single purchase as large as possible — provide the value to back it up.
- Don't make this offer if you can't manage cash — if you get a year of payments in one month, you need to deliver for the whole year.

**Pay Less Now or Pay More Later**
- Give people a choice: pay full price later (after they've experienced it) or pay a discounted price now with additional bonuses
- The "pay later" option: $0 today, card on file, gets charged $X after Y days unless they cancel. Must meet conditions (e.g., attend the event) to qualify for the guarantee.
- The "pay now" option: 20-50% discount + exclusive bonuses (like recordings, 1-on-1 call, printed materials)
- **Sequence:** First get them to accept the pay later option (easy yes). THEN offer the pay now option with discount + bonuses. Since card is already on file, conversion is seamless.
- Advertise as FREE since the pay later option technically costs nothing upfront. Gets massive lead volume.
- **Promise a clear yes/no result** — measurable, simple, hard to dispute. "Did your reading speed double? Yes or no."
- Conditional guarantee: They can only cancel if they qualified (showed up, attended, etc.). Track attendance.
- If >10% of pay later customers cancel, your promise is too big, conditions are too lax, or price is too high.
- **Hint at the next offer early in the experience.** At events/workshops, say: "Everyone wants to know about [next thing] — I'll get to it at the end." Creates anticipation and pre-sells before you even make the offer. This accidentally 2-3x'd supplement sales when Hormozi tried it.

### 2. UPSELL OFFERS (Stage II)
**Purpose:** Maximize 30-day profit by solving the NEXT problem the customer faces after buying.

The timing of upsells is critical — present them at the point of maximum motivation, usually immediately after or during the initial purchase. Every customer interaction is a potential upsell moment: orientation calls, progress reviews, onboarding sessions.

#### Upsell Tactics

**Classic Upsell ("You can't have X without Y")**
- Offer a complementary product or service that enhances the original purchase
- Present immediately after the initial sale when motivation is highest
- Example: Gym membership → supplements (Hormozi's gyms generated ~$200/customer in supplement upsells at 80% gross margins)
- **Get them to say no to say yes:** Ask "You don't want anything else, do you?" — trained response is "no," which here means "no, I don't want anything ELSE" = they keep what you just offered. Clever inversion.
- **Surprise and delight with bonuses:** If you have 4 bonuses to close fence-sitters, add them one at a time. If they buy before all 4, give them all 4 anyway. Overdelivery builds LTV.
- **Hyper-Buying Cycle:** Customers enter a short window of maximum spending when starting something new (weddings, babies, new hobbies, moving). During this window, make as many offers as you can — they'll spend a disproportionate amount in a short period.
- **Use free bonuses to create upsell problems:** A free bonus solves one problem but reveals another. The earmuff example: free earmuffs → need to store them → $30 storage upsell. The bonus itself cost next to nothing.
- **The faster people get access, the more they value it.** A $10K thing now > a $10K thing later. Deliver upsells instantly if possible. Bonus: if they have it in their hands before saying yes, it's harder to give back.
- **Bundle upsells and name them** by customer type or result: "Fastest Results Bundle," "Transformation Package," "Minimum Package." Easier to sell one named thing than nine individual items.
- **Integrate upsells into delivery:** Make what you upsell part of how you deliver other offers. Meal plans included supplement suggestions → people asked about supplements without being sold. Gym Launch training suggested specific software → gym owners bought it.
- **BAMFAM — Book A Meeting From A Meeting.** End every appointment by scheduling the next one. A customer should know the next time they see you and why before they leave.
- **Offer as many upsells as you can solve problems for.** The worst thing isn't them saying no — it's them saying yes to someone else because you never asked.
- **Upsell guarantees, warranties, and insurance** — don't give them away free. Add 5-50% onto the price. Example: art studio charged 10% extra for damage replacement they used to provide free. 30% of customers bought it. Pure profit.

**Menu Upsell (Four-Part Framework)**
Menu upsells combine up to four tactics in sequence:

1. **Unsell** — Tell them what they DON'T need. Cross out items they won't use. This builds trust instantly and makes them lean into what you DO recommend. Employees love unselling — let them show customers how to "game the system" on purpose.
2. **Prescribe** — Tell them exactly what they DO need with personalized, detailed instructions. Write it out step by step: "Take one of these at night, two of these after lunch, one of these after workout." Prescription upselling converts dramatically more than vague recommendations. When you present instructions AS IF they already own it, you remove the option of not buying.
3. **Offer A or B** — Ask which product they PREFER, not whether they want to buy. "Do you like chocolate or vanilla?" Either answer = sale. Never present a yes/no option when you can present an A/B option.
4. **Card on File** — "Want to use the card on file?" Instead of asking them to take out their card (reminder of past ugly buying decisions, hassle), reference a card you already have. Removes hidden friction.

**Pro tip for first purchases:** Ask "Which card do you want to use?" (not "Do you want to buy?")

**A/B anything:** Quantity (one or two?), start dates (tomorrow or Monday?), flavors, time slots, delivery speed, sizes, colors, materials, personnel ("John or Sarah?"), communication preferences. With creativity, anything becomes an A/B upsell.

**Nudge new customers:** "This is my favorite" or "A lot of people love this" or "Two is usually better if you like it." These one-liners move sales.

**The Economist Play:** If you have two options and want to drive bundle sales, add a third decoy at the same price as the bundle. The Economist offered: digital ($59), print ($125), digital+print ($125). Nobody takes print-only when the bundle is the same price, so the bundle looks like an obvious deal. This drove massive bundle adoption.

**The Magnetic Middle (3-Tier Pricing Psychology):**
- To push people from SMALL to MEDIUM: Price small close to medium, large stays high. (Small $6, Medium $7, Large $9)
- To push people from MEDIUM to LARGE: Price medium close to large, small stays low. (Small $5, Medium $8, Large $9)
- If most are already buying large: raise all your prices.

**Anchor Upsell (The Gasp)**
- Present a premium, high-price option FIRST (5-10x your core offer price)
- Then show the standard option — it looks like a much better deal by contrast
- The anchor isn't necessarily designed to sell; it's designed to make the real offer seem like a bargain
- Example: $16,000 suit shown first → $2,200 suit feels like a relief and a deal
- **The 5-Step Anchor Process:** (1) Present the anchor, (2) Get the gasp (expect the mini panic attack), (3) Come to the rescue — ask if they care about what makes it premium, (4) Present your main offer, (5) Ask for payment
- **The bigger the gasp, the more they buy.** Don't be stressed by the reaction — it's the mechanism working.
- **Have the cheaper option already ready** before they gasp. The suit shop owner had it pulled before showing the $16K suit.
- **Don't treat it as fake or the customer will too.** Actually sell it. Make a premium offer you'd happily deliver. Some customers WILL buy the premium, adding outsized profits.
- **Keep primary features the same, change secondary features.** Both suits are suits (primary). The premium has designer fabric (secondary). After anchoring, getting the same primary feature at 1/8th the price = obvious deal.
- **The asymmetric loss:** Making a $1,000 offer to someone with a $100 budget loses you $100. Making a $100 offer to someone with a $1,000 budget loses you $900. Always have premium offers ready — the upside is 9x the downside.

**Rollover Upsell**
- Credit a previous purchase toward the next, higher-value offer
- Apply existing spend as a "down payment" on the upgrade
- Reframes past spending as "already invested money," reducing the psychological barrier to the next purchase

**Four Rollover Use Cases:**
1. **Reengage old customers (Winback Campaign):** Reach out to customers 6+ months since last purchase. Offer to credit their past purchases toward something more expensive. Hormozi recorded personalized videos for 200 past customers offering $4K credit each — 20% took the offer = $1.9M in new ARR from one day of recording.
2. **Rescue your own upset customers:** Instead of refunding, rollover their payment toward a do-over or a different product. "Let me credit that toward [better solution]."
3. **Steal competitors' upset customers:** Scrape negative reviews of competing products. Offer to credit whatever they paid the competitor toward your product. "I'll credit your remaining payments with [competitor] toward switching to us."
4. **Upsell current customers:** Apply a recent small purchase toward a longer commitment. $600 first purchase → $50/month discount over 12 months.

**Pricing rule:** Make the upsell offer at least 4x the rollover credit. This ensures even with the full credit applied, the discount is ≤25%.

**Add urgency:** Make rollover offers one-time only, right then. A "once in a customer lifetime" offer. If they don't take it now, they can pay full price later.

**The Famous Gift Card Play:**
- Sell $200 gift cards for $20 (90% off). Limit 2 per customer. They can ONLY use them on other people.
- When the gifted person comes in, rollover the $200 gift card value toward an offer priced at least $1,000 (the 4x rule).
- Result: Customers literally PAY YOU to refer their friends. Plus you get pocket change from the card sales.
- Works especially well as a holiday offer.

### 3. DOWNSELL OFFERS (Stage II)
**Purpose:** Convert people who said "no" to the primary offer. Get a "yes" where you would have gotten nothing.

**The Rules of Downselling:**
- "No" means no to THIS offer, not no to everything. Don't hide — make another offer.
- Downsells are trades, not discounts. If you give something, get something. Personalize.
- **NEVER drop your price on the same thing.** That's not downselling, it's discounting, and it destroys trust. Customers talk. If they find out someone got the same thing for less, you've created an ethical and trust problem.
- You CAN offer to pay less NOW and more OVER TIME (payment plan). You CAN offer less STUFF for less money (feature downsell). You CANNOT offer the same stuff for less money just because they pushed back.
- It's less about having 100 different products and more about having **100 different ways to offer the same product.**

#### Downsell Tactics

**Payment Plan Downsell (The 7-Step Process)**
The process shifts from getting paid more upfront to getting paid more over time. Stop when they buy.

1. **Reward paying in full** — Present the price WITH interest already included. Then offer a discount for prepaying. "It's $15,000 — but if you pay today, you save $5,000." Same math as charging interest, but framed as a reward. Better feels.
2. **Third-party financing** — Another company pays you now, customer pays them over time (like car dealers). Also: ask "Would you rather I decide your payment terms, or you decide?" — they say they decide → tell them to use a credit card. Wild that this reframe works, but it does. Also offer layaway (pay before receiving).
3. **Half now, half later** — "When's the next time you get paid?" Then: "Put half down today, the rest when you get paid." If they can't, ask "What's the most you can put down today?" Align payments to paychecks (every 2 weeks), not monthly — this boosts 30-day cash collection.
4. **Temperature check** — After 2-3 failed payment attempts, PAUSE. "On a scale from 1 to 10, how bad do you want this?" 8+: "Awesome, don't worry — we're going to figure out a way to make this happen for you." 7 or below: "What would make it a 10?" Then redirect to a feature downsell instead.
5. **Split into three payments** — One-third now, one-third next two paychecks.
6. **Spread evenly** — Equal payments over the service duration (e.g., 16 weekly payments for a 16-week program).
7. **Free trial** — Last resort. See Trial With Penalty below.

**Seesaw Downselling (Simplified Alternative):**
Ask "Would you rather have giant monthly payments or tiny ones?" They say tiny. Then: "Normally it's $X, and if you prepay today you get a huge discount and zero monthly payments." This frames payment plans as negative and highlights prepaying. If they still can't: "The more you put down now, the lower your monthly payments." This is great for less experienced salespeople.

**Built-in payment plan upsell:** During the payment plan, periodically offer the original prepay discount if they pay off the balance. Customers forget they have this option — when reminded, some jump at it. Give sales reps the same bonus for closing the balance as for new sales.

**Billing cadence matters for churn:** Monthly billing = 10.7% monthly churn. Quarterly = 5%. Annual = 2%. Always present pricing from most upfront to least upfront — it also results in longer retention.

**Trial With Penalty**
- Customer tries for free. But card goes on file. If they don't meet conditions (attend sessions, complete homework, show up to check-ins), they get charged.
- Conditions should mirror your Win Your Money Back criteria — the actions that create great long-term customers.
- **5-Step Trial Downsell Process:** (1) Offer the trial last after they've said no to paid offers. (2) Always get a card — if they balk, say "that's how we've always done it." (3) Always get them to agree to stay and pay IF they get results. (4) Explain the fees AFTER getting the card (higher take rate). Have them initial the fee clause. (5) Make check-ins mandatory — these are your upsell opportunities.
- **How to upsell from a trial (3 scenarios):** If they love it → offer longer term or higher tier. If they hate it → ask what they'd change, agree you messed up (only one person can be angry — it's you), offer to make it up with a better-fit offer. If they didn't use it → reach out multiple times, offer to waive the fee if they come in for a meeting, then try to re-engage or offer something different.
- Breaking up fees (e.g., $50 per missed session) works better than one lump fee for most businesses.
- **Just call it a "free trial."** Don't say "trial with penalty" — nobody wants to be penalized.
- **Use Pay Less Now or Pay More Later** for one-time products/services. Use **Trial With Penalty** for recurring products/services.

**Feature Downsell**
- Remove features to reduce the price, but keep the core transformation intact
- The customer still gets value; you still get revenue; nobody walks away empty-handed
- **Never negotiate price. Ever.** People who demand to pay less for the same thing are "business terrorists — I don't negotiate with terrorists."
- **Start by removing the MOST valuable feature first** — when customers see what they lose, many re-upsell themselves on the original offer. Example: Remove the money-back guarantee → customers realize its value → many choose to pay full price to keep it. This one tactic took a business from 25% close rate to 75% (35 buying the main offer, 40 buying the downsell).
- **Name your packages:** Name the most expensive after an aspirational status ("The Whale Package," "Total Transformation," "High Roller"). Name the cheapest "The Minimum" — it implies they have to at least get that.

**The Feature Quality Matrix (15+ dimensions to adjust):**
- Time availability: specific times vs. whenever
- Days of week: MWF vs. any day
- Time block: 15-min calls vs. 60-min calls
- Location: one location vs. all locations
- Cancellation: reschedule fees vs. free reschedule
- Response speed: minutes vs. hours vs. days
- Delivery speed: same-day vs. next-week
- Service ratio: 1-on-1 vs. 1-to-many
- Communication: text vs. chat vs. video call
- Provider: owner vs. senior employee vs. junior
- Live vs. recorded
- DIY vs. done-with-you vs. done-for-you
- Duration: works forever vs. works for X time
- Personalization: generic vs. custom
- Insurance/guarantee: length, coverage, conditions

**Temperature Check Rule:** After every 2 feature downsells, check 1-10 again. 8+: continue feature downselling. 7 or below: ask "What would a 10 look like?" and recombine features. You can alternate between payment plan downsells and feature downsells in the same conversation — this becomes very difficult to refuse.

**After each downsell, ask "Deal?" or "Fair enough?"** — works astonishingly well.

**Free orientations boost DIY downsells:** When someone refuses all done-for-you offers, invite them to a free orientation. At the orientation, offer a DIY product. About half of attendees buy.

**Barter for advertising:** If someone objects on price, offer a discount in exchange for: leaving reviews on all sites, recording a video testimonial, making public social posts at beginning/middle/end, and introducing you to 2 friends. The advertising value exceeds the discount. Win-win.

**Feature downsell current customers proactively:** If a customer isn't using features they're paying for, offer a lower price for just what they use. They either realize they want to keep it (and start using it again) or they're grateful for the savings. Customers downsold into a custom lower package have the SECOND HIGHEST LTV of all customers — they have a product they like at a price they find fair.

### 4. CONTINUITY OFFERS (Stage III)
**Purpose:** Provide ongoing value for ongoing payments. Maximize total lifetime value through recurring revenue.

Continuity transforms one-time buyers into subscribers. The initial transaction is just the beginning. **Always make continuity the LAST offer in the sequence** — get upfront cash from attraction, upsell, and downsell offers first, then add continuity. This way you get both upfront cash AND recurring revenue.

No successful continuity business has a standalone membership offer — they all have other bells and whistles. Continuity alone is tough to advertise profitably because nobody wants to commit to recurring payments for something they haven't tried.

**The math:** Offering $1,000 as a one-time price to 100 people → 10 buy = $10,000 now. Offering the same value at $50/month → 40 buy = $2,000 now + $40,000 over time + 4x the customers for future upsells. Continuity trades cash today for exponentially more cash tomorrow.

#### Continuity Tactics

**Continuity Bonus Offer**
- Join the recurring program and get an incredibly valuable bonus for free
- The bonus is typically worth more than the first few months of payments
- Creates strong initial retention (they stay to "earn back" the bonus value)
- Example: Sign up for annual maintenance ($200/mo) and get a $1,000 equipment upgrade free
- **Sell the bonus, not the membership.** "Join my membership" is weak. "Get this free [valuable thing]" is strong. Advertise the bonus; explain the continuity after they show interest.
- **Make bonuses stuff you already have/do:** Past newsletters, onboarding you'd do anyway, existing digital content. Zero incremental cost, high perceived value.
- **Physical bonuses for digital products, digital bonuses for physical products.** Digital membership → free branded hat/shirt/tool. Physical gym → free live-streamed classes.
- **Use realistic pricing** on bonuses — inflated values destroy trust.
- **Customer titles by tenure:** Give customers status names at 3/6/12+ months (Silver, Gold, Diamond, etc.). One business found customers cared MORE about their title than other bonuses — they introduced themselves by their title.
- **The Continuity-to-Upsell Combo:** Offer the bonus as a standalone purchase for 33%+ more than the first month of continuity. This gives some customers a reason to pay more upfront while others stack recurring revenue.

**Continuity Pricing Ratio Table (Standalone vs. Membership):**
How much more expensive should your standalone offer be to drive a specific % into continuity?
```
1.33x standalone price → ~50% choose continuity
1.66x standalone price → ~60% choose continuity
2.00x standalone price → ~70% choose continuity
2.33x standalone price → ~80% choose continuity
2.66x standalone price → ~90% choose continuity
```
Example: $199/month membership. To get 70% to choose continuity, price standalone at $398/month ($199 × 2.00). Data from testing across thousands of gyms.

**Bulk prepaid upsell:** After they accept continuity, immediately offer bulk time at a discount. "Buy 5 months, get 1 free." Even if only 1 in 8 takes it, it can raise 30-day profits by 50%.

**Continuity Discount Offer**
- Give free time if the customer commits to a longer term
- Apply the discount in one of four ways:
  1. **Upfront** — free time before paid term starts. Best conversion, but delays cash and may have higher churn. Only use if you can enforce contracts (cell phones, storage, real estate).
  2. **At the end** — free bonus time earned after completing all payments on time.
  3. **Spread over time** — $600 discount over 12 months = $50/mo off. Keeps cash flowing while providing full discount value.
  4. **After first 1-2 payments** — collect a bit of cash first to cover advertising/delivery, then apply the discount. Present as "first and last month upfront" or add an activation fee before the bonus kicks in.
- **Don't eat into the term — extend it.** "3 months free" should mean 12 paid months + 3 free = 15 months total, NOT 9 paid months + 3 free = 12 months.
- **Give the discount via physical gift card** — mail it if needed. They can apply whenever after the first few payments. Many forget to use it = full price sign-up. They can also gift it to a friend = free referral.
- **Lifetime discount at your greatest churn point:** If average customer drops off at month 4, offer everyone a permanent lower rate if they stay past month 4. Example: Rice company offered 5% off subscription, then 15% lifetime discount if they stayed 5 straight months.

**Waived Fee Offer**
- First, establish a significant startup fee (3-5x the monthly rate) as part of joining month-to-month
- Then offer to waive the ENTIRE fee if they commit to 12+ months
- If they cancel early inside the commitment, they pay the fee
- **Why it works:** Fees get them to START (they avoid it by committing) AND fees get them to STICK (quitting costs more than staying)
- **Present it simply:** "Option A: Pay $5,000 setup + $1,000/month, cancel anytime. Option B: Commit to 12 months at $1,000/month, setup fee waived. If you leave early, you pay the setup fee."
- **Justify the fee:** "It costs us money to get you started. If you want short-term flexibility, you cover setup costs. If you commit long-term, we cover them."
- **Smaller fee = more upfront cash** (more people go month-to-month). **Larger fee = more commitments** (more people avoid it by committing). Tune based on your cash needs.
- **Drop the fee after commitment is fulfilled.** If someone stays 12 months, they've earned free cancellation.
- **Cancellation fee for a cause:** "What's a cause you absolutely hate? Great — if you cancel early, I'll donate your setup fee to them." Two reasons to stay: don't want to lose money AND don't want it going to something they despise.
- Works especially well for services that take a long time to show results: SEO, investing, weight loss, etc.

#### Continuity Revenue Maximization Hacks

**Bill every 4 weeks, not monthly.**
There are 12 months in a year but 13 four-week cycles. $100 every 4 weeks vs. $100/month = 8.3% more annual revenue for zero additional work. On a 20% margin business, this increases annual profit by 41%. Same number of customers will buy. Just change a few words.

**Charge the 3% processing fee.**
Add "plus 3% processing fee" to your price — 4 extra words. No one has ever not bought because of the processing fee. On a 10% margin business, 3% added to top line = 30% more profit. Goes straight to bottom line.

**Get two forms of payment.**
Offer to waive the processing fee if they provide a backup payment method. Most say yes. Try to get ACH (automated clearing house — direct bank link, cheapest to transact besides cash). This fixes the two biggest recurring revenue killers: expired cards and insufficient funds.

**Align billing to paycheck schedules.**
Charge on the days people get paid. Higher success rate on transactions. If a charge declines, run it multiple times throughout the day — different times of day have different balances. This alone recovers a third of declined payments.

#### Cancellation Strategy

**Make cancellation fee = the discount they received.** If they got $600 in discounts by committing, they can cancel anytime by paying $600. This puts them back to the month-to-month rate. Simple to explain, feels equitable.

**Make sure customers know HOW to cancel.** If they have nowhere to complain inside your business, they will complain OUTSIDE your business. A clear cancellation path gives you a chance to save them.

**Exit interview saves:** Offer to waive the cancellation fee if they agree to an exit interview. Then:
- Let them vent. Get MORE angry about the problem than them — they may calm down and save themselves.
- If they complained about a fixable problem, fix it.
- If they wanted a better product, do a rollover upsell into a higher tier.
- If they're using features they don't need, feature downsell to a cheaper custom package.
- **Routinely saves 33-50% of cancellation attempts.**

**If >5% of committed customers want to cancel early,** the product needs work — pricing can nudge but can't overcome a terrible product.

## THE BELIEF CONTINUUM

A framework for moving prospects from skepticism to buying conviction.

```
Disbelief → Doubt → Neutral → Interest → Belief → Conviction → Purchase
```

At each stage, different types of proof are most effective:
- **Disbelief → Doubt:** Third-party validation, data, credentials
- **Doubt → Neutral:** Case studies, specific results from similar people
- **Neutral → Interest:** Demonstrations, free value, lead magnets
- **Interest → Belief:** Testimonials, guarantees, risk reversal
- **Belief → Conviction:** Social proof at scale, urgency, scarcity
- **Conviction → Purchase:** Simple call to action, assumptive close

## KEY MONEY MODELS PRINCIPLES

1. **Simple models scale; fancy models fail.** Perfect one offer type at a time. Measure in quarters, not weeks.
2. **Every customer interaction is a monetization opportunity** — orientation, progress reviews, onboarding, support calls. Make check-ins mandatory AND upsell opportunities.
3. **A customer paying $0 is worth $0.** Even a small downsell is infinitely better than a lost sale.
4. **Continuity is the final brick.** Without recurring revenue, you're perpetually on a treadmill.
5. **Price is not the problem — the Money Model is the problem.** If customers aren't spending enough, restructure the sequence of offers.
6. **Test prices incrementally.** Make new offers cheap first to get yeses and feedback. Then raise until the extra cash from yeses doesn't make up for the noes.
7. **The best time to upsell is immediately after the first purchase** — motivation peaks at the moment of commitment.
8. **Credit past spending toward future spending** — Rollover Upsells reduce the psychological barrier to bigger purchases.
9. **Unsell before you sell** — telling people what they DON'T need builds more trust than telling them what they do.
10. **The Money Model should fund its own growth.** If it doesn't, the model is broken — not the market.
11. **100 ways to offer your product > 100 products to offer.** If you offer personal training, you can offer 1/2/3/4 sessions per week. One product, four offers.
12. **Switch from the broke-person mantra ("this won't work for my business") to the rich-person mantra ("how will I make this work for my business?").** All money models work. Be creative.
13. **Don't negotiate with business terrorists.** Never lower your price on the same thing. Change what they pay (payment plan) or what they get (feature downsell), but never the price for the same offer.
14. **When your money model starts working, your business starts breaking.** Scaling reveals operational constraints. That's part of the game — find someone who can build the team to deliver your vision.
15. **Hard selling is for weak products.** If someone doesn't want it, that's okay. Make offers available when customers have the problem, and find someone who wants it. It's a numbers game.
16. **Be transparent. State the facts.** If the facts aren't compelling, change reality to make them compelling, or learn to frame them better. Don't lie — you short-change yourself long-term. A bad reputation sticks for life.
17. **If a customer asks for their money back, give it back.** Don't take blood money. Focus on getting the next customer.
18. **The real money comes from people who succeed, not people who fail.** Deliver results → they buy more → they refer more → the flywheel spins.
