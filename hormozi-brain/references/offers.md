# Alex Hormozi: Offer Creation & Pricing Mental Models

Complete reference of every framework, principle, and quote related to how Hormozi thinks about creating offers, setting prices, and maximizing value.

## THE VALUE EQUATION

The foundational formula that governs all offer creation:

```
Value = (Dream Outcome x Perceived Likelihood of Achievement) / (Time Delay x Effort & Sacrifice)
```

### The Top of the Equation (Maximize These)

**Dream Outcome** — The ideal end state the customer wants. The bigger and more vivid you can paint this, the more valuable the offer feels. This is not about features. It is about the transformation. A weight loss program does not sell meal plans. It sells confidence, attractiveness, energy, and longevity.

**Perceived Likelihood of Achievement** — The customer's belief that they will actually get the result. This is where social proof, testimonials, guarantees, case studies, credentials, and specificity all live. The reason a Harvard degree costs more than an online course is not the information — it is the perceived certainty of outcome.

Ways to increase Perceived Likelihood:
- Testimonials and case studies (real people, real results, real timelines)
- Guarantees that remove risk from the buyer
- Social proof (volume of customers, recognizable names)
- Specificity (the more specific the promise, the more believable)
- Credentials and authority signals
- Demonstration (show, don't tell)

### The Bottom of the Equation (Minimize These)

**Time Delay** — How long it takes to get the result. The shorter the time to outcome, the more valuable the offer. "Speed is one of the easiest value multipliers. Look at any service category and see if you can deliver the result in half the time. Fast beats free." People will pay dramatically more to get a result faster. Same-day delivery. Instant approvals. Results in 30 days vs. 12 months.

**Effort & Sacrifice** — How much work, pain, and lifestyle change the customer has to endure. "As I've studied bigger more successful businesses, I noticed they're trying to eliminate as much effort, sacrifice, and time as possible." Netflix eliminated driving to Blockbuster. Amazon eliminated going to stores. Uber eliminated finding a cab. The less the customer has to do, the more they will pay.

### How to Use the Value Equation

When constructing any offer, systematically ask:
1. How can I make the dream outcome bigger or more vivid?
2. How can I increase their belief that they will achieve it?
3. How can I get them the result faster?
4. How can I make it require less effort and sacrifice on their part?

Every improvement to any of these four variables increases the value of the offer without changing the cost of delivery.

## THE GRAND SLAM OFFER

A Grand Slam Offer has seven distinct components that stack together to create something so compelling that prospects feel irrational saying no.

"Make offers so good people feel stupid saying no."

### The 7 Components

**1. Promotion Type**
Three categories: Free, Discount, or Premium. Each has a place depending on the business model and stage. Free creates flow. Discount creates urgency. Premium creates exclusivity and higher perceived value. Most businesses default to discounting when they should be going premium.

**2. Core Offer (The Steak, Not the Sizzle)**
This is the primary deliverable — the main thing they are buying. It must be clearly defined and directly tied to their dream outcome. The core offer should be simple enough to explain in one sentence. If you cannot explain what someone gets in one sentence, the offer is too complicated.

**3. Bonuses (Additional Value Stacked)**
Bonuses increase the perceived value without increasing the price. The best bonuses address the next logical objection or obstacle. If someone buys a fitness program, a meal plan bonus addresses "but what do I eat?" Each bonus should have its own value anchor — a standalone price that makes the total package feel like a steal. Stack bonuses until the value-to-price ratio is absurd.

**4. Scarcity (Limited Availability)**
Scarcity is about quantity limits. Only 20 spots. Only 50 units. Only accepting 10 clients this month. Scarcity must be real or you destroy trust permanently. Types of scarcity: limited seats, limited production runs, limited time slots, limited inventory, cohort-based enrollment.

**5. Urgency (Time-Based Pressure)**
Urgency is about deadlines. Offer expires Friday. Price goes up Monday. Bonuses disappear at midnight. Urgency compresses the decision timeline. Without urgency, prospects default to "I'll think about it," which is the same as "no." Deadlines force action.

**6. Guarantee (Stacked Guarantees)**
Guarantees reverse risk from the buyer to the seller. The stronger the guarantee, the more confident the prospect feels. Types of guarantees to stack:
- Unconditional (money back, no questions asked)
- Conditional (if you do X and don't get Y, money back)
- Anti-guarantee ("all sales final" — works for ultra-premium positioning)
- Performance-based (we hit the metric or you don't pay)
- Time-based (try it for 30/60/90 days)
- Outcome-based (we guarantee the specific result)

Stack multiple guarantees together for maximum risk reversal.

**7. Magic Naming Formula (Re-skin the Same Offer)**
The same offer with a different name can feel like a completely different product. Naming framework: use a unique mechanism name + the timeframe + the container type. Example: "The 6-Week Profit Accelerator" vs. "Business Coaching" — same deliverable, completely different perceived value. Re-naming and re-packaging an offer is the fastest way to revive a stale funnel without changing the actual product.

## SEVEN WAYS TO USE FREE

Free is not a business model. Free is a tool. Used correctly, it creates flow that you monetize.

"Create flow first, then monetize the flow, then add friction."

### The 7 Free Models

**1. Free Bribe Offer**
Give something valuable away for free in exchange for contact information or a micro-commitment. Lead magnets, free reports, free assessments. The free thing should be genuinely useful and demonstrate expertise. It starts the relationship and creates reciprocity.

**2. Limited Free**
Free for the first X people. Free for the first 100 customers. This combines free with scarcity. It creates urgency to act now while it is still free, and it establishes that the thing has real value (because it will cost money later).

**3. Free Trial + Penalty**
Let them try it free, but there is a penalty for quitting or not continuing. The penalty could be financial (credit card on file, charged after trial) or social (public commitment). This model works because people hate losing something they already have more than they value gaining something new (loss aversion).

**4. Free with Deposit**
Give it away free but collect a refundable deposit. The deposit ensures commitment and filters out tire-kickers. When they complete the program/action, the deposit is returned. This creates accountability without actual cost to the customer.

**5. Free Forever**
The freemium model. The base product is free forever. Revenue comes from upgrades, premium features, or a percentage of power users who convert. Works at massive scale. Requires a product where the free tier is useful but the paid tier is dramatically better.

**6. Free Giveaway**
Pure value with no strings attached. Give away your best content, your best frameworks, your best ideas. This builds trust, authority, and audience at scale. Monetization happens downstream through paid products, services, or access. "Give away the secrets, sell the implementation."

**7. Free with Commitment**
Free if you commit to a specific action or timeline. Free gym membership if you come 3x per week. Free coaching if you implement everything. The commitment filter ensures only serious people participate, which improves outcomes, which improves testimonials, which improves the offer.

## THREE QUALITIES OF GREAT OFFERS

Every offer that converts at a high rate shares these three qualities:

### 1. Believability (The Reason Why)
The offer must be believable. If it sounds too good to be true, people will not buy. Every element of the offer needs a "reason why." Why is it discounted? Why are you giving this away? Why is the guarantee so strong? The reason why does not have to be sophisticated. It just has to exist. "We're running this special because we just moved to a new location and need to fill our schedule" is a perfectly good reason why.

### 2. High ROI (Communicable in Few Words)
The return on investment must be obvious and expressible in a single sentence. If you cannot explain the ROI in under 10 words, the offer is too complex. "Pay $1,000, make $10,000" is instantly understandable. "Join our comprehensive multi-module program for holistic business development" is not.

### 3. Business Model Behind It
A great offer must also be a great business. If the offer is so generous that you lose money on every customer, it is not a great offer — it is a great way to go bankrupt. The business model must support the offer. This means understanding unit economics, lifetime value, cost of fulfillment, and margins before constructing the offer.

## NEVER LOWER YOUR PRICES

Hormozi's most emphatic pricing principle. Lowering prices is almost always the wrong move.

### The Cautionary Tale
"I lowered from $800/week to $600/week. Churn was unchanged. Lost $5M/year." The customers who churned at $800 still churned at $600. The discount did not improve retention. It only destroyed revenue. The customers who stayed at $800 would have stayed regardless. The price cut gave away margin for nothing.

### The Tripling Example
"Tripling from $99 to $299: one-third left, two-thirds stayed at 3x = 2x revenue with fewer customers." When you triple your price, you do not lose two-thirds of your customers. You lose about one-third. The remaining two-thirds are paying 3x, which means roughly 2x total revenue — with fewer customers to serve, fewer support tickets, fewer refund requests, and higher quality clients who get better results.

### Why Higher Prices Are Better
- Higher prices attract more committed customers
- More committed customers get better results
- Better results create better testimonials
- Better testimonials attract more customers
- Higher prices fund better service delivery
- Better delivery creates even better results
- The virtuous cycle compounds

### When Low Prices Kill You
Low prices attract bargain hunters who are the hardest to serve, most likely to complain, most likely to refund, and least likely to get results. They poison your testimonials, burn out your team, and destroy your margins. You end up working harder to make less money with worse clients.

## PRICE RAISE FRAMEWORK

A systematic process for raising prices without losing the sale:

### Step 1: Give the Sales Team a "Home Base" Number
This is the target price you actually want. The sales team needs confidence in a number they can anchor to. If the target is $5,000, that is their home base.

### Step 2: Anchor ABOVE the Target
Present the full value or "retail" price significantly above the target. If the target is $5,000, anchor at $8,000 or $10,000. This makes the actual price feel like a deal by comparison. The anchor establishes what the offer is "worth." The gap between anchor and actual price is the perceived savings.

### Step 3: Prepayment Discount to Actual Target
Offer a discount for paying in full upfront, bringing the price down to the actual target number. "The full investment is $8,000, but if you pay in full today, it's $5,000." This rewards commitment, improves cash flow, and reduces payment default risk.

### Step 4: Uneven Payment Splits (Front-Loaded)
If they cannot pay in full, offer payment plans — but front-load them. Instead of 4 equal payments of $2,000, do $3,000 down, then 3 payments of $1,667. This reduces default risk and ensures you collect the majority of revenue even if they stop paying.

### Step 5: Clear the Pipeline First with Urgency
Before implementing a price raise, close out everyone in the current pipeline at the current price with urgency: "Price goes up Monday." This creates a rush of closes at the old price, validates demand, and makes the transition clean.

## PRICING TACTICS (From Acquisition.com Value Emails)

### The Middle Pricing Trick
If you sell a cheap tier and an expensive tier, add a mid-tier with features halfway between but priced only 10% below the expensive option. This skews more buyers to the premium tier because the mid-tier makes the premium look like a bargain by comparison.

### Turn 7s to 9s
There is no conversion difference between $97 and $99, or between $997 and $999. Switch all prices ending in 7 to 9. Same take rate, more money per sale. Same logic applies to adding .99 to round numbers.

### The Five Rules of Pricing
1. **Double the price rarely cuts conversions in half** — the drop-off isn't 1:1. Most businesses dramatically overestimate the volume loss from a price increase.
2. **Raising prices is easier than lowering them** — you'd have to explain to existing customers why prices dropped, implying they overpaid.
3. **Products have different value to different customers** — charge based on willingness to pay (value-based pricing), not cost-plus.
4. **Have more than one tier** to maximize revenue across your entire customer base. Different segments will self-select into different price points.
5. **Customer surplus = difference between price and value.** Superior pricing enables a superior product because it funds better delivery.

**The profit math:** If you double your price, you can 6X your profit. This is because most costs are fixed — doubling revenue on the same cost base creates exponential margin expansion.

## SIX WAYS TO INCREASE LIFETIME VALUE (LTV)

LTV is the total revenue a customer generates over their entire relationship with your business. There are only six ways to increase it:

### 1. Increase Price
The simplest and most overlooked. Raise your prices. "Don't model the pricing of small businesses. Look at mature large businesses." Large businesses charge more because they have discovered what the market actually bears. Small businesses underprice out of fear and insecurity, not strategy.

### 2. Decrease Fulfillment Cost
Same revenue, less cost to deliver = higher profit per customer = higher effective LTV. Systematize, automate, create leverage. A group coaching program costs nearly the same to run for 10 people as it does for 50.

### 3. Increase Purchase Frequency
Get customers to buy more often. Subscriptions, memberships, consumables, and replenishment models all drive frequency. A customer who buys once a year at $1,000 is worth $1,000. A customer who buys monthly at $200 is worth $2,400.

### 4. Upsells
Offer a more premium or expanded version of what they already bought. After they buy the course, offer the mastermind. After they buy the software, offer the enterprise tier. Upsells work because the customer has already demonstrated willingness to pay and trust in your brand.

### 5. Cross-Sells
Offer complementary products or services. The customer bought fitness coaching — now offer nutrition coaching. They bought the website — now offer SEO. Cross-sells expand the relationship into adjacent needs.

### 6. Down-Sells
When a customer is about to leave or cannot afford the main offer, offer a lower-priced alternative. A customer paying $0 is worth nothing. A customer paying $50/month on a down-sell is worth $600/year. Down-sells recover revenue that would otherwise be lost entirely.

## THE SERVICE DELIVERY CUBE

Six dimensions that define how you deliver your offer. Each dimension is a spectrum you can adjust to change the value, cost, and scalability of your service.

### Dimension 1: Ratio
- **1:1** — One provider to one client. Highest perceived value. Highest cost. Least scalable.
- **Small Group** — One provider to a small group (3-20). Balances personalization with leverage.
- **1:Many** — One provider to an unlimited audience. Lowest perceived value per person. Lowest cost. Most scalable.

### Dimension 2: Client Involvement
- **DIY (Do It Yourself)** — You provide the information/tools, they do the work. Lowest cost to deliver. Lowest results on average.
- **DWY (Done With You)** — Collaborative. You guide, they execute. Medium cost. Better results because of accountability.
- **DFY (Done For You)** — You do everything. Highest cost. Highest results (in theory). Most premium pricing.

### Dimension 3: Support Channels
How you communicate: email, chat, phone, video call, in-person, community forum, Voxer, text. Each channel has different perceived value, different response time expectations, and different cost.

### Dimension 4: Format
- **Live** — Highest engagement, highest perceived value, least scalable
- **Recorded** — Scalable, reusable, but lower engagement
- **Written** — Most scalable, lowest production cost, lowest engagement

### Dimension 5: Speed and Convenience
How fast do they get access? How fast do they get responses? How fast do they get results? Speed is a value multiplier at every level. Instant access > next day > next week.

### Dimension 6: The 10x / 1-10th Exercise
For every element of your offer, ask two questions:
1. "What would it look like if I made this 10x better?"
2. "What would it look like if I made this cost 1/10th?"

These two questions force creative thinking about value and efficiency. Sometimes the 10x version reveals an entirely new tier of service. Sometimes the 1/10th version reveals waste you can eliminate without reducing value.

## ARBITRAGE THINKING

"All businesses are arbitrage."

Every business buys something at one price and sells it at a higher price. That is the entire game. The gap between what you pay and what you charge is your margin.

**Price should NEVER be related to cost — only to market demand.** The cost to deliver a service has nothing to do with what you should charge for it. A surgeon's operation costs the hospital a few thousand in supplies and staff time. The bill is $200,000. The price is based on the value of the outcome and the alternatives available to the buyer, not the cost of the inputs.

If it costs you $100 to deliver a result that is worth $10,000 to the customer, you should be charging $5,000, not $200. Pricing from cost upward is the amateur mistake. Pricing from value downward is the professional approach.

### Implications
- Never justify prices by listing costs
- Never feel guilty about margins
- Always price based on the value of the transformation
- Your job is to widen the gap between cost and price — on both sides (reduce cost AND increase price)
- The market determines the ceiling. Your efficiency determines the floor. Your profit lives in between.

## THE ONLY 8 THINGS YOU CAN SELL

Every business in the world, regardless of industry, sells one or more of these eight things:

### 1. Products
Physical or digital goods. Tangible items that the customer owns after purchase. Books, software, supplements, equipment, clothing.

### 2. Services
Labor or expertise applied to solve a problem. Consulting, coaching, agency work, repair, maintenance, cleaning.

### 3. Access
Entry to a person, group, place, or resource. Memberships, communities, exclusive events, VIP access, mastermind groups.

### 4. Attention
Eyeballs. Advertising, sponsorships, media placements. If you have an audience, you can sell access to that audience. This is the business model of every media company, influencer, and content creator.

### 5. Risk (Insurance)
Taking on someone else's downside. Insurance companies, guarantees, warranties, hedging products. You are selling peace of mind and protection from loss.

### 6. Money (Lending)
Providing capital now in exchange for more capital later. Banks, lenders, investors, payment plan providers. You are selling time and access to funds.

### 7. Labor
Raw human effort and time. Staffing agencies, contractors, freelancers. You are selling hours and output.

### 8. Information
Knowledge, data, frameworks, and know-how. Courses, books, research reports, consulting, training. You are selling the shortcut — the ability to skip trial and error.

### Why This Matters
When you understand that everything falls into one of these eight categories, you can:
- Identify new revenue streams by asking "which of these 8 am I NOT selling?"
- Bundle multiple categories for higher perceived value (product + service + access = premium offer)
- Understand your competitive landscape more clearly
- See opportunities for arbitrage between categories

## ADDITIONAL OFFER PRINCIPLES

### The Dream Outcome Amplification
Do not sell the thing. Sell what the thing gets them. Do not sell the drill. Do not even sell the hole. Sell the beautiful painting hanging on the wall in the finished home where their family gathers for the holidays. Go as far down the outcome chain as you can.

### The Decommoditization Principle
Commodities compete on price. Offers compete on value. If you are competing on price, you do not have an offer — you have a commodity. The purpose of all the Grand Slam Offer components is to make your thing uncommoditizable and incomparable.

### Naming as Differentiation
If it has the same name as everything else, it will be compared to everything else. A "fitness program" is a commodity. "The 90-Day Metabolic Reset Protocol" is an offer. Same content. Different frame. Different price point.

### Value Stacking Presentation
When presenting an offer, assign a dollar value to every component individually before revealing the total price. This creates a massive perceived value gap. "$2,000 value + $1,500 value + $500 value + $1,000 value = $5,000 total value, yours today for $997." The gap between stacked value and price is the deal.

### The Trim and Stack Method
Take your current offer. Remove anything that does not directly contribute to the dream outcome (trim). Then add bonuses that address the next biggest obstacles to success (stack). Repeat until the offer is lean but loaded with value.

### Fulfillment as a Competitive Moat
"The best offer in the world is worthless if you cannot deliver." Offer creation and fulfillment are two sides of the same coin. Overpromise and underdeliver once — you get a refund, a bad review, and a destroyed reputation. The best businesses make offers they can overdeliver on consistently.
