*Source: Dan Birdwhistell's public writing, FishmanAF Newsletter (AI Video Ads), course frameworks*

## The Creative-First Philosophy

Dan's most consistent public message across $800M in spend:

> "Creative is the most impactful variable in Meta Ads. Not targeting. Not bid strategy. Creative."

**Why creative dominates:**
In the era of Advantage+ and broad targeting, Meta's AI handles audience discovery. The creative is the signal the algorithm uses to find your audience. A bad creative teaches the algorithm to find the wrong people. A great creative finds buyers the algorithm would never have targeted manually.

**The creative virtuous cycle:**
```
Strong creative → algorithm finds high-converting users
                → conversion signal improves
                → algorithm gets smarter
                → future creative performs even better
```

**The creative vicious cycle (what bad creative does):**
```
Weak creative → algorithm finds low-converting users
              → poor conversion signal
              → algorithm degrades
              → costs rise, performance falls
```

## The SCOA Creative Brief Framework

Dan's framework for writing creative briefs that produce high-performing ads — whether for human production or AI generation.

**SCOA = Setting, Characters, Objects, Action/Voice**

### S — Setting

Specify the exact environment where the ad takes place.

**For authenticity:** Use real, specific locations rather than generic settings.
- Weak: "suburban home"
- Strong: "Sunday morning kitchen in suburban Nashville, Tennessee — coffee brewing, morning light through window"

**For AI video (Veo3):** Use real neighborhoods/cities — Veo3 references Google Street View data for specific locations, dramatically improving realism.

**Setting categories for your product creatives:**
- Morning bedroom (waking up, phone in hand) — habit formation context
- Kitchen/coffee nook (daily routine anchor) — habit integration context
- Community parking lot (community context) — social/community angle
- Commute (car, earbuds) — mobile usage context
- Quiet evening (after kids are in bed) — reflection/winding down context

### C — Characters

Specify the exact person(s) in the ad. The more specific, the more authentic the result.

**Include:**
- Age range (not "young adult" — "32-year-old")
- Physical description (relevant details only)
- Clothing (signals context and identity)
- Demeanor / emotional state
- Voice tone (for video)
- Backstory cues (implied through appearance)

**Character archetypes that convert for your product:**
- Busy parent finding morning peace
- Young professional building a daily practice
- Someone returning to wellness after time away
- Community-going person who wants more daily connection
- New believer building their first daily habit

### O — Objects

Props that ground the scene and signal context.

**For your product ads:**
- Phone showing the app (required — product demonstration)
- Coffee mug (morning routine signal)
- Content Library (wellness context)
- Journal (reflection/growth signal)
- Headphones (audio/podcast consumption habit)

**Dan's insight:** Objects are often overlooked in briefs. A phone propped next to a coffee in morning light signals daily habit. A Content Library next to a phone signals wellness context without saying it. Let objects do narrative work without dialogue.

### A — Action/Voice

The specific actions, movements, and dialogue in the creative.

**For video:**
- Camera direction (handheld for UGC, static for testimonial)
- Body language instructions ("looks down at phone, smiles, looks up at camera")
- Dialogue with inflection cues ("says slowly, with warmth: 'I never thought I'd look forward to waking up'")
- Stage directions for key moments ("holds phone toward camera showing app screen")

**For static:**
- Subject position and gaze direction
- What the subject is doing with their hands/phone
- Expression (joyful, peaceful, reflective, surprised)

## Creative Format Hierarchy

Based on Dan's public frameworks and performance patterns from his client work.

### Format 1: UGC-Style Video (Highest converting)

**Why it wins:**
- Looks like organic content — algorithm rewards with lower CPMs
- Authenticity builds trust faster than polished ads
- Platform native — users don't feel interrupted
- Increasingly impossible to distinguish from real UGC

**UGC production principles:**
- First-person perspective (like the person is recording themselves)
- Light camera shake or handheld movement
- Subject speaks directly to camera
- Minimal production polish — avoid obvious studio lighting, professional color grading
- Sound imperfections are fine (mild background noise is authentic)
- Short (3–15 seconds is the sweet spot)

**UGC script structure:**
```
Hook (0-3 sec): Grab attention with specific, relatable statement
Problem (3-8 sec): Name the pain point they recognize
Solution (8-12 sec): Show/tell the product solving it
CTA (12-15 sec): Simple, low-friction action
```

**UGC hooks that work for your product:**
- "I used to start every morning on social media. Now I do this instead."
- "47 days ago I started a new habit and it completely changed my mornings."
- "I'm not great at doing this on my own, but this makes it easy."
- "I'm not a morning person, but now I actually wake up early on purpose."

### Format 2: Talking Head / Testimonial

**Why it works:**
- Human face = trust signal
- Direct-to-camera = intimacy and credibility
- Story arc creates emotional investment
- Works for social proof and authority angles

**Structure:**
```
Identity establish (0-3 sec): Who is this person? (implied, not stated)
Relatable problem (3-10 sec): The before state
Pivotal moment (10-20 sec): Discovery of the product
Transformation (20-30 sec): The after state
CTA (final 5 sec): "Try it free" or simple action
```

**For your product:** The before/after personal transformation is a natural narrative arc.
Example: "I grew up going to community but honestly, my daily practice was basically non-existent as an adult. I downloaded your product kind of randomly and three months later I have a 90-day streak. I didn't think I was the kind of person who could build this habit."

### Format 3: Sketch Comedy / Narrative

**Why it works:**
- Entertainment first, product second — algorithm rewards engagement
- Humor creates shareable moments
- Surprising structure breaks scroll pattern
- Highly memorable (comedy sticks)

**Sketch structure (Dan's framework):**
1. Set the scene quickly (cold open — no exposition)
2. Establish tension or misdirection
3. Punchline or unexpected twist
4. Reaction beat (deadpan, silence, or over-the-top response)
5. Product reveal as resolution (optional but powerful)

**Wellness-based sketch angles for your product:**
- "My resolution was to engage more. My streak is now 3 years." (deadpan reaction)
- Husband surprised at wife not being on social media at 6am — she's using your product
- Person expecting to be tired, looks at phone, sees their engagement session completed → visibly energized

### Format 4: Visual Metamorphosis / Transformation

**Dan's description:** "Trip in a Box" — mundane everyday setting transforms visually into aspirational state.

**Structure:**
- Act 1: Realistic, ordinary, relatable setting (the before)
- Trigger: A catalyst moment (app opening, gesture, sound)
- Act 2: Transformation into aspirational state
- Product reveal + CTA

**For your product:**
- Chaotic morning (alarm, kids, rushing) → phone shows your product notification → character pauses, sits down, peaceful → transformation complete
- Anxious person in waiting room → opens your product → session begins → calm washes over face → "Peace that passes understanding"

### Format 5: Static Image / Single Card

**Still highly effective for:**
- Direct response (download, trial start)
- Retargeting (seen video ad → static reinforcement)
- Simple benefit communication

**Static image best practices:**
- Bold, readable headline (people won't zoom in — must be legible at thumbnail size)
- Clear product screenshot or lifestyle image (not just logo)
- Minimal text (Meta penalizes high text-to-image ratio)
- Strong visual contrast (must stop the scroll)
- One clear CTA

**Static image formats that work:**
- App screenshot with overlaid testimonial quote
- Before/after lifestyle split
- "Day X of my streak" type content (user-generated aesthetic)
- Simple benefit statement with product visual

## AI Video Production Playbook (2025)

*From Dan's FishmanAF Newsletter, August 2025 — his most detailed public framework*

### The Production Workflow

**Phase 1: Creative Direction (before prompting)**
- Define the emotional arc: what feeling should the viewer have at 0 seconds? At 8 seconds?
- Choose the format: UGC, sketch, metamorphosis, testimonial?
- Write the hook in plain English first
- Draft the dialogue/script as if writing for a human actor

**Phase 2: Veo2 Prototyping**
- Use Veo2 (no generation limits, no audio) to test visual approach
- Questions to answer in this phase:
  - Does the scene render realistically?
  - Does the character behave and look right?
  - Is the setting grounding and authentic?
  - Is the emotional tone coming through visually?
- Iterate on prompts here — it's free/low-cost compared to Veo3

**Phase 3: Veo3 Production**
- Apply everything learned from Veo2 testing
- Add SCOA specifics: precise character details, exact dialogue with inflection cues
- Specify prohibitions: "no captions," "no text overlays," "no wall art"
- Expect 3–6 attempts per concept — plan for this
- Export in 16:9 and 1:1 (currently outperforming 9:16 for AI-generated video)

**Phase 4: Integration into Testing**
- Treat AI video exactly like human-produced video in testing
- No shortcuts — same testing framework applies

### Dan's AI Creative Tips

**Specificity = quality:**
- "Woman in her 30s, auburn hair, wearing a soft blue cardigan, sitting at a kitchen table" → better output than "woman"
- Specific locations trigger Google Street View data → dramatically more realistic settings
- Specific voice inflections → more natural dialogue delivery

**UGC-first approach:**
- First-person perspective, handheld feel — outperforms polished video
- "Light camera shake" instruction produces authentic-feeling movement
- Skip audio or captions in prompt — platforms add captions automatically
- 16:9 and 1:1 are outperforming 9:16 for AI-generated content (counterintuitive vs. human UGC)

**Sketch comedy principles for AI:**
- Write the full setup/punchline structure before prompting
- Keep time constraint in mind (3–8 second limit for Veo3 clips currently)
- Reactions are as important as punchlines — specify them explicitly
- Single, surprising twist beats multi-layered humor at this length

**What AI video cannot do yet (as of mid-2025):**
- Consistent character appearance across multiple clips (face changes between scenes)
- Accurate text rendering (misspellings in signs/overlays are common)
- Specific product integration (hard to get app UI to appear correctly)
- Long-form coherent narrative (>15 seconds gets unstable)

## Creative Fatigue Management

### The Fatigue Detection System

Track these metrics weekly per creative:

**Primary fatigue signals:**
- CTR: declining >20% from first-week baseline → investigate
- CPA: rising >25% from first-week baseline → investigate
- Frequency: above 3.5 for the same audience → high fatigue risk

**Secondary signals:**
- CPM rising without volume increase (audience level saturation)
- Ad relevance diagnostics declining (Meta's own quality score)
- Comments turning negative (users recognizing and commenting on seeing the same ad repeatedly)

**How to check frequency by creative:**
Meta Ads Manager → Breakdown → Delivery → Creative → add "Frequency" column

### The Creative Refresh Protocol

**Step 1: Identify fatiguing creative (weekly audit)**
Pull CTR, CPA, and frequency by creative. Flag any exceeding thresholds.

**Step 2: Diagnose root cause**
- High frequency → same message shown too many times → new creative needed
- Low frequency but declining CTR → audience match getting worse → test new audience or new hook
- High frequency + rising CPA → fatigue is confirmed → retire this creative

**Step 3: Brief the refresh**
Don't brief a new ad from scratch. Brief a refresh:
- Keep: the core message angle that was working
- Change: the hook, the character, the setting, or the format
- This is faster to produce and often retains most of the original creative's effectiveness

**Step 4: Launch refresh in testing sandbox**
Don't replace fatigued creative with the refresh immediately. Run the refresh in testing first to confirm performance before replacing.

**Step 5: Gradual transition**
Reduce budget on fatigued creative as refresh ramps up. Don't do a hard cutover — this creates a learning reset.

## Creative Production Operations

### Building an Always-On Creative Pipeline

Dan's key insight: most accounts fail at creative ops, not creative quality. Having a pipeline that produces 3–5 new concepts per month is more important than perfecting any single concept.

**Monthly creative calendar structure:**
- Week 1: Review performance, identify top performers and fatiguing ads
- Week 2: Brief 3–5 new concepts based on insights
- Week 3: Production week (human UGC, AI video, or static)
- Week 4: Launch new concepts in testing campaign; analyze results

**Concept ratio to keep in pipeline:**
- 2 new hooks on a proven message angle (low risk, high probability of working)
- 1 new message angle (medium risk, potential discovery)
- 1 format experiment (higher risk, potential breakout)
- 1 seasonal/moment-based (timed to upcoming calendar event)
