# Design Critique Framework

**Source framework:** Bruno Bergher's writing on design critique, PM crits, prescriptive vs. descriptive feedback, and the four elements of effective asks.

---

## Critique vs. Review vs. Approval

| | Critique | Review | Approval |
|---|---------|--------|----------|
| Purpose | Improve the work | Evaluate the work | Decide to proceed |
| Tone | Collaborative, curious | Evaluative | Decisive |
| Who leads | The presenter asks for specific feedback | The reviewer provides assessment | The decision-maker gives go/no-go |
| Outcome | New ideas, refinements | Assessment of quality/readiness | Ship or don't ship |
| When | During exploration and development | Before finalization | Before launch |

**Common mistake:** Treating critique as approval. When a PM provides "feedback" that is really a go/no-go decision, it undermines the designer's ability to explore and kills creative risk-taking.

## The Anatomy of a Good Critique

### Before the Critique

**Presenter preparation:**
1. Define the stage of work (exploration, wireframe, high-fidelity, pre-ship)
2. Articulate 2-3 specific questions to receive feedback on
3. Share context: user problem, constraints, prior decisions
4. If possible, share the work in advance for async review

**Participant preparation:**
1. Review the work with fresh eyes (don't critique from memory of prior versions)
2. Hold your initial reactions lightly -- dig for the reasoning behind the design choices
3. Prepare descriptive observations, not prescriptive suggestions
4. Consider the stated questions -- focus your feedback there

### During the Critique (45-minute structure)

**Phase 1: Presentation (5-7 minutes)**
- Designer presents the work with context
- States the specific feedback questions
- Describes the constraints and decisions already made

**Phase 2: Silent Review (3-5 minutes)**
- Everyone reviews the work quietly
- Jot down observations, questions, and reactions
- Resist the urge to discuss -- silent observation produces better individual thinking

**Phase 3: Clarifying Questions (5 minutes)**
- Participants ask questions to understand, not to critique
- "What happens when the user has no community requests yet?"
- "Why did you choose a card layout over a list?"

**Phase 4: Feedback Round (15-20 minutes)**
- Each participant offers 1-2 observations
- Focus on the stated questions first
- Use descriptive language (see spectrum below)
- Facilitator ensures everyone contributes, no one dominates

**Phase 5: Synthesis (5-7 minutes)**
- Designer reflects back what they heard
- Identifies the 2-3 most impactful feedback themes
- States their planned next steps

### After the Critique

- Designer documents key feedback and decisions
- No obligation to act on every piece of feedback -- the designer decides what to incorporate
- If a major direction change emerges, schedule a follow-up in 2-3 days

## The Prescriptive-to-Descriptive Spectrum

### Level 1: Highly Prescriptive (Avoid)

These tell the designer exactly what to change:
- "Make the font bigger"
- "Put the button on the right side"
- "Use a modal instead of a bottom sheet"

**Why it's problematic:** It treats the designer as an executor, not a problem solver. The PM's specific solution may not be the best one.

### Level 2: Solution-Suggestive (Use Sparingly)

These suggest a direction while acknowledging alternatives:
- "Have you considered using a bottom sheet here? It might keep the user in context better."
- "I wonder if a larger font would help with the hierarchy issue."

**When acceptable:** When the PM has domain expertise (e.g., "In mobile apps, users expect to see the engagement text before the author name -- have you considered reordering?") or when time pressure requires faster convergence.

### Level 3: Problem-Descriptive (Default Mode)

These describe the observed problem without suggesting a solution:
- "I'm having trouble distinguishing between the primary and secondary actions on this screen"
- "The community request feels buried -- I didn't notice it until the third time I looked"
- "The emotional tone here feels more corporate than personal"

**Why it's best:** It gives the designer the problem to solve with their expertise. Multiple people may describe the same problem from different angles, giving the designer rich signal.

### Level 4: Observation-Based (Advanced)

These share raw observations without even naming the problem:
- "I noticed I kept looking at the top-right corner expecting something to be there"
- "My eye was drawn to the illustration first, then the headline, then the subtitle"
- "I found myself wanting to swipe but couldn't"

**When to use:** In early-stage critique of explorations and concepts. Pure observations give the designer maximum freedom to interpret what they mean.

## Feedback by Design Stage

### Exploration Stage

**Appropriate:** Direction-level feedback, problem validation, conceptual alternatives.
**NOT appropriate:** Visual details, copy editing, technical feasibility.

### Wireframe Stage

**Appropriate:** Flow and navigation, information hierarchy, missing states and edge cases.
**NOT appropriate:** Color, visual style, micro-interaction details.

### High-Fidelity Stage

**Appropriate:** Visual hierarchy, consistency with product patterns, copy clarity, accessibility, emotional tone.
**NOT appropriate:** Questioning the fundamental approach (too late), major structural changes.

### Pre-Ship Stage

**Appropriate:** Polish, edge case review (empty, error, loading states), cross-platform consistency, real-device testing.

## PM Crits: Applying Critique to PM Work

Bergher's PM Crit framework adapts design critique for PM work products. The insight: if critique makes designers better, it can make PMs better too.

### What PMs Can Critique

- **PRDs and feature briefs:** Is the problem well-defined? Are success metrics clear?
- **Prioritization frameworks:** Is the ranking defensible? What biases might influence the order?
- **Strategy documents:** Is the logic coherent? Are the assumptions explicit?
- **User research plans:** Are we asking the right questions? Reaching the right users?

### PM Crit Structure (30 minutes)

1. **Present** (5 min): PM shares the work product and states 2-3 specific questions
2. **Silent review** (3 min): Participants read and think independently
3. **Questions** (5 min): Clarifying only -- understand the work before critiquing
4. **Feedback** (12 min): Each participant offers descriptive observations
5. **Synthesis** (5 min): Presenting PM identifies themes and next steps

### Benefits for PM-Designer Collaboration

- PMs develop empathy for the vulnerability of presenting work for feedback
- PMs learn to ask for specific feedback (a skill that helps designers too)
- The team develops a shared language for giving and receiving feedback

## Building a Critique Culture

### Signs of a Healthy Critique Culture

- People volunteer to present work early, before it's polished
- Feedback focuses on the work, not the person
- Junior team members feel safe offering observations
- The work visibly improves after critique

### Signs of a Broken Critique Culture

- People only present finished work (for validation, not feedback)
- Feedback is dominated by senior voices
- People get defensive or argumentative
- People avoid critique sessions

### How to Build the Culture

**Start small.** Begin with 2-3 willing participants. Success breeds participation.

**Model vulnerability.** Leaders should present first and receive feedback gracefully.

**Enforce the rules.** No prescriptive feedback. No personal attacks. Focus on stated questions. Everyone contributes.

**Iterate on the format.** After 4-5 sessions, retrospect on the critique process itself.

## Async vs. Synchronous Feedback

Not all feedback requires a meeting:

**Use async feedback (comments in Figma, written notes) when:**
- The work is in early exploration and you're providing directional reactions
- You're reviewing a series of designs and need time to think
- The feedback is about details (copy, edge cases) rather than direction
- The designer explicitly asked for async review

**Use synchronous feedback (live meeting, design review) when:**
- A major direction decision needs to be made
- There's disagreement that would benefit from dialogue
- The work is in a late stage and needs final alignment
- You're running a formal design critique

### The Feedback Meeting Structure

For formal design reviews outside of critique:

1. **Designer presents** (5 min) -- Context, goals, what they'd like feedback on
2. **Silent review** (2-3 min) -- Everyone looks at the work without speaking
3. **Clarifying questions** (3 min) -- "What happens when...?" "Why did you choose...?"
4. **Feedback round** (10 min) -- Each person offers 1-2 pieces of feedback, descriptive
5. **Designer responds** (3 min) -- Synthesizes, asks follow-ups, identifies next steps

## Cross-Functional Empathy in Feedback

### What Designers Care About (That PMs Often Underestimate)

- **Craft quality.** Designers have trained for years to distinguish good from great. Asking them to ship "good enough" feels like asking a surgeon to be "close enough."
- **User advocacy.** Designers see themselves as the user's voice. Overriding design decisions for business reasons can feel like betraying users.
- **Portfolio and growth.** Designers need portfolio-worthy work to advance their careers. Projects that are too small or too constrained don't serve their growth.
- **Creative autonomy.** Being told exactly what to make is demoralizing. Designers need space to explore and surprise.

### What PMs Care About (That Designers Often Underestimate)

- **Shipping velocity.** PMs are measured on output and impact. Long design cycles feel like blocked progress.
- **Stakeholder management.** PMs absorb pressure from leadership, sales, and customers. What seems like unnecessary urgency may reflect real external pressure.
- **Business sustainability.** Revenue, retention, and growth aren't just metrics -- they're what keeps the company alive and the team employed.
- **Scope management.** Every feature added to the design is a feature that must be built, tested, and maintained.

### Building Empathy in Practice

- **Shadow each other.** PM attends a design critique; designer attends a sprint planning or stakeholder review.
- **Share your pressures.** Be honest about what's stressing you and why.
- **Acknowledge trade-offs.** When you override a design decision for business reasons, acknowledge the trade-off explicitly.
- **Celebrate each other.** When the product ships and succeeds, publicly credit the collaboration.

## your product Application

### Critique for Wellness-Sensitive Design

your product's design critique should include an additional dimension: **personal and emotional appropriateness.** Beyond standard usability and visual quality feedback, critiques should ask:

- Does this design create the emotional space appropriate for engagement and engagement?
- Would this feel respectful to users across consumer denominations?
- Does the visual language support or undermine the personal tone?
- Is this feature encouraging genuine daily practice, or does it feel gamified or performative?

### Recommended Critique Cadence

- **Weekly:** 30-minute team critique (rotating presenters, 1 project per session)
- **Bi-weekly:** PM Crit session (rotating PM presenters)
- **Monthly:** Cross-team critique (invite engineering, content, or expertal team for fresh perspectives)
- **Quarterly:** "Coherence review" -- the whole team reviews the full product for consistency and emotional coherence

### Who Should Participate

Core design critique should include the product designer, PM, and 2-3 peers (other designers, design-minded engineers, or content strategists). For your product specifically, include a wellness content advisor periodically -- someone who can offer perspective on whether design choices honor the personal context.

**your product tip:** For daily engagement design reviews, async Figma comments work well for visual feedback. For new feature explorations (community groups, community features), schedule live sessions where PM and designer can discuss the emotional and personal context together.
